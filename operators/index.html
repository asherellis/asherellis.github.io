A Compendium · c. 1975–1995

Cold War Dads

In the years between the fall of Saigon and the fall of the Soviet Union, the world's most capable men cultivated the world's most ordinary appearances. KGB illegals drove Chevrolets and attended PTA meetings. Delta operators wore polo shirts and let their hair grow out. CIA case officers carried briefcases through airport terminals on three continents and looked, to a customs agent, like mid-level regional managers on their way to a sales conference.

They looked exactly like your dad. This was not an accident.

KGB Directorate S. 1st SFOD-D. Ground Branch. Alpha Group. Vympel. SEAL Team Six. The quiet professionals and the forgettable faces.

The Archetype
The Template That Held
UNCLASSIFIED
Soviet
Col. Rudolf Abel (William August Fisher)
KGB Directorate S · Brooklyn, New York · arrested June 1957 · exchanged February 1962

He was born in Newcastle upon Tyne to Russian émigré parents, joined Soviet intelligence in the 1920s, and spent the late 1940s and 1950s running Soviet intelligence networks from a studio apartment in Brooklyn under the name Emil Goldfus—artist and photographer. When the FBI arrested him in 1957, they found shortwave radios, cipher pads, a hollow shaving brush, and a hollow nickel that had once passed through a Brooklyn newsboy's hands containing a microdot. He named himself Rudolf Abel to his captors—a prearranged signal to Moscow that he had been taken. His FBI mugshot shows a mild-looking man in his late 50s who could be a retired schoolteacher. He was the most sophisticated KGB illegal ever caught in America, and the aesthetic template the entire program would follow for the next forty years.

1975–1979
Formation
UNCLASSIFIED
American
Col. Charles "Chargin' Charlie" Beckwith
Founder & first commander, 1st SFOD-D · Fort Bragg, NC · 1977–1981

Before he was the father of Delta Force, Beckwith was a halfback at the University of Georgia under Wally Butts. In 1962, he became the first American officer to serve with the British SAS—a year embedded with 22 SAS in Malaya that changed the trajectory of American special operations. He spent the next fifteen years trying to convince the Pentagon to build something like it. He wrote memos. He briefed generals. He was passed over for promotion and given bad postings. He kept writing memos. In 1977, Fort Bragg's post commander finally called him in and told him to go build his unit. He looked like a football coach. He sounded like a football coach. The unit he built was, and remains, the most capable direct-action force in the world.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Gen. Yuri Drozdov — KGB First Chief Directorate, Directorate S
Chief of Illegals Directorate · Yasenevo, Moscow · 1979–1991

When Drozdov took command of Directorate S in 1979, he inherited the most carefully managed secret in Soviet intelligence: a worldwide network of officers embedded in Western countries under false identities, sometimes for decades. He expanded it. Under his leadership, Directorate S ran illegals in the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, West Germany, and Australia. The selection criteria were unusual for a military organization: ability to acquire foreign languages without an accent, capacity to internalize a foreign culture so completely that the officer could pass as a local in a random conversation with a stranger, and above all a neutral, unmemorable physical appearance. Drozdov described the ideal illegal as "a man who could sit on a park bench for six hours without anyone noticing him." He wore a cardigan.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
KGB Alpha Group (Directorate A) — Kabul, December 1979
KGB First Chief Directorate · formed July 1974 · operational by 1979

Alpha Group was formed in 1974 in response to the Munich massacre, when the Soviet leadership looked at a Palestinian terrorist operation and decided they needed something equivalent. Their group photos from the late 1970s show men in nondescript athletic clothes who could be a provincial volleyball team. On the night of December 27, 1979, Alpha Group operators stormed the Tajbeg Palace in Kabul, killed the Afghan president Hafizullah Amin and his guards in forty-five minutes, and initiated the Soviet-Afghan War. One KGB officer died in the assault, along with 200 of Amin's guards. The Soviet leadership had decided he was unreliable. The corrective measure took forty-five minutes. The Alpha operators involved received state awards at a quiet ceremony with no press.

1980–1984
The Covert War
UNCLASSIFIED
American
Maj. Richard "Dick" Meadows
U.S. Army Special Forces (Ret.) · Tehran, Iran · January–April 1980

In January 1980, Meadows flew into Tehran under a false identity and set up a cover as an Irish businessman. His mission: case safe houses, meet local assets, and prepare the ground for Operation Eagle Claw—the Delta Force rescue of 52 American hostages from the U.S. Embassy. He was retired. He volunteered. He looked like a retired American insurance executive, which was not an easy thing to look like in Tehran in January 1980. A decade earlier, he had led the raid on Son Tay prison in North Vietnam: technically perfect, prisoners had been moved, zero extracted, no operators lost. The Army still studies it. When Eagle Claw was aborted on April 25th after a mechanical failure and a fatal collision at Desert One, Meadows and his team exfiltrated independently. He was not captured. Nobody knew he had been there.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Cdr. Richard Marcinko — SEAL Team Six
Founding Commander, DEVGRU predecessor · Dam Neck Annex, Virginia Beach · 1980–1983

When the Navy tasked Marcinko with building a maritime counterterrorism unit after Eagle Claw exposed the gaps in American capability, he made a deliberate decision about what the unit would look like. He recruited from the Teams but favored the strange ones: men with beards, tattoos, and motorcycle jackets who had somehow ended up as Navy SEALs. The unit's dress code was: whatever fits the environment. Marcinko's own look during this period—shaggy hair, mustache, denim—was indistinguishable from a longshoreman or a biker or a bar owner. He was the commanding officer of the most elite maritime unit in the world. He was dishonorably discharged in 1992 for contracting fraud. He also wrote a memoir, and it sold. The hair was always part of it.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Vympel (KGB Directorate V)
KGB First Chief Directorate · Balashikha, Moscow Oblast · formed August 19, 1981

Vympel was created for a category of mission that did not officially exist: wartime deep-penetration operations in foreign countries—sabotage of nuclear power stations, railways, and communications infrastructure; assassination of key Western civilian and military leaders; disruption of the NATO command structure in the opening hours of a conflict. All officers spoke at least one Western language fluently. All had studied or worked in target countries. All had what the selection board called "neutral physiognomy"—faces that did not invite a second look. Training photos from their Balashikha facility show men who could be physical education teachers or municipal engineers. The selection criteria explicitly favored those who were physically forgettable. This was considered a talent.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
William F. Buckley
CIA Station Chief, Beirut · captured March 16, 1984 · died June 1985

On March 16, 1984, Buckley was taken outside his Beirut apartment by Islamic Jihad, a Hezbollah-linked group operating at Iranian direction. He was the CIA's station chief and the most valuable intelligence officer in the most important intelligence station in the Middle East. His captors systematically tortured him for fourteen months, extracting what the CIA later assessed was a comprehensive account of American intelligence assets and methods in the region. Videotapes of the interrogations were delivered to the CIA station in Athens. He died in June 1985. His remains were identified in December 1991. His CIA badge photo shows a quiet man in a jacket and tie who looks like a professor at a small university. He had spent 30 years in the agency. Nobody who saw him in Beirut suspected he was anything other than what his cover said.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Jack Platt & Gennady Vasilenko
CIA / KGB · Washington, DC · 1978–ongoing

In 1978, CIA counterintelligence officer Jack Platt and KGB officer Gennady Vasilenko arrived in Washington within months of each other. Both were assigned to recruit the other. Instead, they became friends. They played tennis. They drank beer. They wore polo shirts and had mustaches that were indistinguishable from each other's. Neither was able to definitively say, in the early years, whether the friendship was a recruitment effort by the other or something genuine. It was something genuine. The KGB eventually arrested Vasilenko multiple times on suspicion of being a CIA asset—he wasn't, but his friendship with Platt made him look like one. He spent years in Soviet prisons. He was freed in the 2010 spy swap alongside Anna Chapman and nine other Russian illegals. Robert De Niro reportedly intervened on his behalf. The polo shirt period predated all of this.

1985–1989
The Year of the Spy — and After
РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Col. Victor Cherkashin — Washington Rezidentura
KGB First Chief Directorate, Line KR (Counterintelligence) · Washington, DC · 1981–1985

In April 1985, Aldrich Ames walked into the Soviet Embassy on Wisconsin Avenue in Washington and handed an envelope to Cherkashin. The envelope contained the names of three Soviet citizens Ames assessed were working for the CIA, and an offer to provide more. A few months later, Robert Hanssen mailed a letter to the Washington KGB residency containing classified material and an offer to cooperate. Cherkashin handled both of them, simultaneously, without either knowing about the other. He looked, in every available photograph from this period, like a mid-level Soviet diplomat at a Georgetown cocktail party, which was the cover. In his memoir he writes that he never thought of himself as a spy. He thought of himself as a diplomat. This is, in most ways, exactly what made him so effective.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Aldrich H. Ames
CIA Directorate of Operations, Soviet/East European Division · Langley, VA · 1985–1994

Ames was not a KGB officer. He was a CIA counterintelligence analyst—the man responsible for coordinating the agency's Soviet sources—who in April 1985 began selling those sources to the KGB for money. At least ten were executed. He did this for nine years. He earned approximately $4.6 million. He drove a Jaguar XJ6 to CIA headquarters on a GS-14 government salary. His supervisors noticed. They filed a report expressing concern. The report sat. He was arrested in February 1994. His FBI arrest photo—taken at his home in Arlington—shows a slightly dazed man in his early 50s in rumpled clothes who looks as though he's been pulled from bed at an inconvenient hour. He had. The remarkable thing about the photo is that it looks exactly like every other photo of him from the previous decade. Nothing in his appearance, in any photograph from any year, suggests what he was.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Col. Oleg Gordievsky — Operation PIMLICO
KGB London Rezidentura (and MI6 asset from 1974) · exfiltrated July 19, 1985

Gordievsky was the KGB's station chief in London. He was also an MI6 asset who had been providing intelligence to the British since 1974. His intelligence about Operation Ryan—the Soviet program monitoring for signs of a Western first strike—and his account of how Able Archer 83, a NATO nuclear exercise, had been interpreted in Moscow as potentially real, contributed directly to Reagan's decision to tone down his anti-Soviet rhetoric. In May 1985, Ames told the KGB there was a British asset within the KGB's London station. Moscow recalled Gordievsky. On July 19, 1985, he shook his surveillance team and made his way to the Finnish border. British intelligence officers, traveling in diplomatic cars, picked him up near Vyborg and hid him in the trunk of their vehicle. At the Finnish border, an MI6 officer's wife changed her baby's dirty diaper on the trunk of the car at the checkpoint, distracting the KGB's trained dogs. He heard Sibelius' Finlandia on the car radio when they crossed the border.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Gen. Vitaly Yurchenko — Au Pied de Cochon
KGB Fifth Directorate / Washington Station · defected August 1985; re-defected November 1985

Yurchenko defected to the CIA in Rome on August 1, 1985 and was assessed as the most valuable Soviet defector in years. Over three months, he identified two Americans working for Soviet intelligence: NSA analyst Ronald Pelton (arrested November 1985) and CIA officer Edward Lee Howard (who escaped to the Soviet Union before arrest). On November 2, 1985, Yurchenko and his CIA handler were having dinner at Au Pied de Cochon, a French restaurant on Wisconsin Avenue in Georgetown. He turned to his handler and said: "What would you do if I got up and walked out? Would you shoot me?" His handler said no. "I'll be back in 15 or 20 minutes," Yurchenko said. "If I'm not, it will not be your fault." He did not come back. Two days later, the Soviet Embassy held a press conference at which Yurchenko, in a suit and tie, said he had been kidnapped and drugged by the CIA. He flew to Moscow. He wore a beige suit to the Soviet press conference.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Andrei Bezrukov ("Don Heathfield") & Elena Vavilova ("Ann Foley")
KGB Directorate S · Toronto, then Cambridge, MA · c. 1985–2010

The Bezrukovs were KGB illegals embedded in Canada in the early 1980s, living as "Don Heathfield" and "Ann Foley." They relocated to the Boston area in the 1990s. Andrei—as Heathfield—obtained a Harvard Kennedy School master's degree and worked as an international business consultant. Elena ran their household and taught their two sons, who grew up as native English speakers and had no idea their parents were KGB officers. The family photographs from this period show exactly what you would expect: a professional couple in their late thirties or early forties, two boys, a reasonable house in a reasonable suburb of Boston, a sensible family car. In June 2010, they were among ten Russian illegals arrested in an FBI sweep. Their sons—who were American in every way that mattered—were left in the United States when their parents were exchanged.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Milton Bearden — CIA Islamabad Station
CIA Chief of Station, Islamabad, Pakistan · 1986–1989 · Operation Cyclone

By the time Bearden arrived in Islamabad in 1986, the CIA's covert Afghan program was already the largest in the agency's history. His job was to make it larger. CIA Director William Casey had given him $1 billion and an instruction: drive the Soviets out. Bearden oversaw the delivery of FIM-92 Stinger surface-to-air missiles to the mujahideen beginning in September 1986—the introduction that neutralized Soviet air superiority and changed the calculus of the war. He grew a beard for the assignment. His photographs from the period show a large, genial man in civilian clothes who could be an agricultural development consultant from Montana, which was roughly the cover. He managed approximately 250,000 fighters across nine provinces and helped end the Soviet-Afghan War. The beard was considerable.

1990–1995
Endgame
UNCLASSIFIED
American
Delta Force, C Squadron — Snipers
1st SFOD-D · Fort Bragg, NC · c. 1990–91

Three men standing outdoors. One has a mustache. They are wearing the kind of clothes you would wear to go fishing, or to pick your kids up from school, or to a barbecue at a neighbor's house on a Saturday afternoon in Fayetteville, North Carolina. Their rifles are visible in the background. They are some of the best snipers in the world. The photo circulated widely on JSOC Archive and became, for a period, the canonical image of what the culture of these units actually looked like during the 1975–1995 period: competent, quiet, deeply unspectacular in appearance, and aware that appearance was itself a professional attribute. You would walk past all three of them in a grocery store without registering anything except that they were there.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
CIA Special Activities Division / Ground Branch
Directorate of Operations · Central America, Afghanistan, Africa, the Balkans · c. 1982–1995

The officers of CIA Ground Branch—the paramilitary arm of the Directorate of Operations—maintained a dress code that could be summarized as: khakis, hiking boots, and whatever shirt implied they were with an agricultural NGO or a mining company or an infrastructure consultancy. Through the 1980s they were active in Nicaragua, El Salvador, Angola, Chad, and Afghanistan. They trained and equipped insurgent forces, conducted direct-action missions under Title 50 authority, and returned to Langley looking like they'd been at a mid-level project review meeting in Denver. They were photographed almost never. When they were, they looked like people who had been doing exactly what their cover said they were doing. That was the whole point.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Task Force Ranger — Mogadishu
1st SFOD-D / 3rd Ranger Bn / 160th SOAR · Mogadishu, Somalia · August–October 1993

The pre-mission photographs of the Task Force Ranger operators—taken at their compound near the Mogadishu airport in the summer of 1993—show men who look like private contractors on a security detail in a hot country: tan, short-haired, wearing civilian clothes and carrying weapons in a practiced way that doesn't invite questions. Operation Gothic Serpent was a mission to capture the senior lieutenants of Somali warlord Mohamed Farrah Aidid. On October 3rd, 1993, an assault on the Olympic Hotel became an eighteen-hour battle. Eighteen Americans died. Delta operators Gary Gordon and Randall Shughart, who requested permission to insert by rope to protect a downed Black Hawk pilot knowing they were outnumbered, were posthumously awarded the Medal of Honor. In their photographs from before the mission, they look like guys you'd see at a Home Depot.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Vasili Mitrokhin
KGB First Chief Directorate — Archivist · defected to UK, 1992

For twelve years, from 1972 to 1984, Mitrokhin was the KGB's chief archivist in the First Chief Directorate's foreign intelligence archive. He spent those years copying by hand the most sensitive operational records in Soviet intelligence history: agent networks, dead drops, clandestine meeting sites, technical operations, assassination plots, intelligence sources inside Western governments. He wrote them in tiny script on scraps of paper, hid them in his shoes, transferred them to typewritten pages at home, and buried them in milk canisters under his dacha. When the Soviet Union fell, he tried to give the archive to the CIA. They passed. He went to MI6. They sent a team to Estonia to dig up the milk canisters—six trunks of material—and flew him to England. His photographs show the world's most unremarkable man: cardigan, glasses, a face that has never caused anyone to look twice. He was carrying the most consequential intelligence archive of the Cold War under his vegetable garden.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Gennady Vasilenko
KGB officer · Moscow; Guyana; Washington, DC · imprisoned by KGB 1988, 2005; freed 2010

Vasilenko was a KGB officer who was genuinely, innocently friends with CIA counterintelligence officer Jack Platt. The KGB did not believe this was possible. They arrested him in Guyana in 1988, brought him back to Moscow, and interrogated him. They arrested him again in 2005, after Aldrich Ames had been in American custody for a decade and Robert Hanssen had been arrested in 2001—and the KGB had, by process of elimination, concluded that Vasilenko must have been the source. He wasn't. He spent years in Lefortovo Prison. Robert De Niro, who had become friends with Vasilenko through Platt, advocated for his release. Vasilenko was freed in the June 2010 spy swap—the same one that brought Anna Chapman home. In every photograph from his KGB years, he looks like a Soviet physical education teacher, which was his cover. He was genuinely friends with a CIA officer. That was his whole crime.

Further Reading

  • Delta Force — Charles Beckwith (1983)
    Beckwith's own account of founding the unit. Unvarnished.
  • The Quiet Professional — Alan Hoe (1993)
    Biography of Dick Meadows. The best book on what the quiet professional archetype actually looks like up close.
  • The Spy and the Traitor — Ben Macintyre (2018)
    Gordievsky biography. Probably the best single Cold War intelligence narrative written. The diaper-on-the-trunk detail is true.
  • The Main Enemy — Milton Bearden & James Risen (2003)
    CIA vs. KGB from the inside, through the final decade. Bearden's account of the Islamabad years is definitive.
  • Spy Handler — Victor Cherkashin (2005)
    The KGB handler's memoir. Remarkably candid about the Ames and Hanssen recruitments.
  • The Mitrokhin Archive — Christopher Andrew & Vasili Mitrokhin (1999)
    What Mitrokhin buried in his dacha. All of it.
  • Best of Enemies — Gus Russo & Eric Dezenhall (2018)
    The Platt-Vasilenko friendship. The last great spy story of the Cold War, as advertised.
  • Black Hawk Down — Mark Bowden (1999)
    Mogadishu. The standard.
