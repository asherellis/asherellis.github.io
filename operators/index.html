A Compendium · c. 1975–1995

Cold War Dads

In the years between the fall of Saigon and the fall of the Soviet Union, the world's most capable men cultivated the world's most ordinary appearances. KGB illegals drove Chevrolets and attended PTA meetings. Delta operators wore polo shirts and let their hair grow out. CIA case officers carried briefcases through airport terminals on three continents and looked, to a customs agent, like mid-level regional managers on their way to a sales conference.

They looked exactly like your dad. This was not an accident.

KGB Directorate S. 1st SFOD-D. Ground Branch. Alpha Group. Vympel. SEAL Team Six. GRU Spetsnaz. The quiet professionals and the forgettable faces.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Gen. William "Wild Bill" Donovan — OSS
Office of Strategic Services · Washington, DC / worldwide · 1942–1945

William Joseph Donovan received the Medal of Honor for leading his battalion across the Ourcq River under machine gun fire in October 1918 — the last major American engagement of the First World War. He spent the following two decades as a Wall Street lawyer, U.S. Attorney, occasional Republican candidate, and informal intelligence traveler, visiting Mussolini's Italy, Franco's Spain, and Churchill's Britain at Roosevelt's direction, reporting on European military preparations. In July 1941, Roosevelt asked him to build an American intelligence organization. There was no template. Donovan created one. The Office of Strategic Services, chartered in June 1942, operated across every theater of the war: Research and Analysis produced the first integrated American all-source intelligence assessments; Special Intelligence ran agent networks in occupied Europe, North Africa, and Asia; Special Operations conducted guerrilla support and sabotage missions; X-2 managed counterintelligence and penetration operations against Axis services; Morale Operations conducted propaganda and deception. The OSS personnel were what Cold War CIA officers would later become: well-educated Americans — academics, lawyers, athletes, businessmen — operating in denied areas under cover identities, doing things that could not be officially acknowledged. Donovan's specific contribution was the institutional model: a unified organization that combined collection, analysis, paramilitary action, and covert influence under a single command structure, with the cultural expectation that its officers would look and behave indistinguishably from the environments they were operating in. The OSS was dissolved September 20, 1945 — fifteen days after Gouzenko walked out of the Soviet Embassy in Ottawa. Truman initially believed peacetime intelligence agencies were police-state instruments and wanted none. The Berlin Blockade, the Czech coup, and Gouzenko's disclosures changed the calculus. The National Security Act of 1947 created the Central Intelligence Agency, drawing explicitly on the OSS organizational model. Most of the names in this compendium's Archetype section — Helms, Wisner, Harvey, Angleton, and the generation they trained — were OSS veterans or trained by OSS veterans. The operational culture they carried into the Cold War was the one Donovan had built. In photographs from his OSS directorship, Donovan looks like a large, broad-shouldered American in his late fifties who has thought carefully about what needs to happen next and has allocated specific people to specific problems. He usually had. He died in 1959. The CIA buried him at Arlington. His uniform is in the agency's museum. The organization is still running on his model.

Soviet
Klaus Fuchs — Los Alamos
GRU / KGB (Soviet intelligence) · Los Alamos, New Mexico; Harwell, UK · 1944–1950

Klaus Emil Julius Fuchs was born in Germany in 1911, the son of a Quaker pastor and a committed Socialist. He became a Communist at the University of Kiel in the early 1930s, fled Germany after the Nazis' rise, studied theoretical physics in Britain under Max Born, and by 1940 was one of Britain's most capable physicists. Interned briefly as a German national after France fell, recruited into the British Tube Alloys nuclear program, transferred to New York in 1943 and Los Alamos in 1944, he spent the following five years passing to Soviet intelligence everything he understood about the design of the atomic and hydrogen bombs. The intelligence was comprehensive and accurate: the implosion design for the Fat Man weapon, the yields, the production rates, the key technical problems and their solutions, the state of progress on the thermonuclear program. American officials who reviewed what he passed assessed it as advancing Soviet nuclear weapons development by two to four years. He was motivated by ideology, not money. He believed that nuclear weapons knowledge belonged to all humanity and that American nuclear monopoly was more dangerous than Soviet nuclear knowledge. He told his GRU handler this, explicitly, and the handler believed him, because Fuchs was not lying. He was caught in 1949 through VENONA program intercepts and confessed in January 1950 with a thoroughness that British interrogators found almost disarming: he was fully cooperative, provided complete detail, and expressed no regret. He served nine of a fourteen-year British sentence, was released in 1959, and emigrated to East Germany, where he spent the next thirty years as deputy director of the Central Institute for Nuclear Physics in Dresden. He died in 1988. In the group photographs of the Manhattan Project scientists — at Los Alamos, at the Trinity test site, at the Oppenheimer seminars — Fuchs stands among his colleagues in ordinary clothes: a slight, bespectacled German physicist who looks entirely unremarkable in the company of the people who built the bomb. He is sometimes standing next to Oppenheimer. He looks exactly like the people around him. He was passing everything he knew to the Soviet Union. The two things were simultaneously true for five years. This is the principle that runs through every photograph in this compendium.

Julius and Ethel Rosenberg
KGB (NKVD) · New York City · recruited 1942 · executed June 19, 1953

Julius Rosenberg was an electrical engineer who had joined the Young Communist League in 1934, graduated from CCNY in 1939, and was recruited by Soviet intelligence in 1942. He ran a network of sources at the wartime Army Signal Corps and at defense contractors, passing radar and weapons technology to the Soviets through a handler network ultimately controlled by the KGB's New York rezidentura. His most significant asset was his brother-in-law David Greenglass, a machinist at Los Alamos who provided the Soviets with nuclear weapon design sketches and written descriptions — material that, combined with what Klaus Fuchs was passing simultaneously from the British component of the same project, gave Soviet weapons scientists a substantially complete picture of American implosion design. Ethel, Julius's wife, typed and passed documents and was aware of the network's activities; her exact operational role — always contested — has been partially clarified by KGB files made available after the Soviet Union's dissolution, which indicate she played a supporting rather than primary collection role. Both were arrested in 1950: Julius on June 17, Ethel on August 11. The primary evidence against them included VENONA program intercepts — which identified Julius by his codename LIBERAL in KGB cable traffic — but which could not be revealed at trial without compromising the VENONA program. They were tried in 1951 for conspiracy to commit espionage, convicted, and executed by electric chair at Sing Sing Prison on June 19, 1953. They were 37 and 35 years old. They maintained their innocence throughout. Julius's guilt was confirmed beyond any serious historical doubt by VENONA and by KGB archive material. The question of Ethel's guilt — specifically the capital charge for which she was prosecuted — remains disputed; historians who have examined the KGB files have concluded that she was included in the prosecution partly to pressure Julius into naming additional sources, which he declined to do. The political context of their trial and execution — McCarthy-era America, the Soviet Union's recent nuclear test, the Korean War — cannot be separated from its conduct. In their courtroom and prison photographs, Julius and Ethel Rosenberg look like what they were: a young Jewish couple from the Lower East Side of Manhattan, unremarkable in appearance, not dramatic, sitting in institutional clothes with the expressions of people who have fully processed the situation and are living with it.

Igor Gouzenko — "I Want to Stay"
GRU cipher clerk, Soviet Embassy Ottawa (defected to Canada) · Ottawa, Ontario · September 5, 1945

Gouzenko was a cipher clerk at the Soviet military attaché's office in Ottawa, encoding and decoding GRU communications. On the evening of September 5, 1945 — three weeks after Japan's surrender, the war still close enough that the Soviet Union was technically a fellow Allied power — he walked out of the embassy with 109 documents hidden under his shirt. The documents named GRU network members operating in Canada and Britain, including individuals at the British High Commission, and provided threads leading toward atomic espionage in the United States and the United Kingdom. He went first to the Ottawa Journal newspaper. An editor declined — this appeared to be an internal Soviet matter, and the newspaper wanted no part of it. He went to the Department of Justice. A civil servant told him to come back the next day. He went home. That night, GRU officers came to his door demanding the return of the documents and his immediate return to the embassy. He and his pregnant wife hid overnight in a neighbor's apartment while the GRU attempted to break in. The RCMP accepted him the following morning, after the intervention of the Minister of Justice. His disclosures led directly to the arrest of British physicist Alan Nunn May, who had passed atomic secrets to the Soviets from the Montreal laboratory. They provided investigative threads leading toward the networks that eventually reached Julius and Ethel Rosenberg. They established, for Western governments that had regarded Soviet intelligence as an Allied counterpart, that Soviet espionage against the West was systematic, extensive, and ongoing in peacetime. The Cold War, as a framework for understanding the postwar world, arguably began in Ottawa on September 5, 1945, in an apartment building on Somerset Street where a Soviet cipher clerk waited through the night for someone to believe him. Gouzenko lived in Ontario under the name George Brown for the rest of his life. He appeared publicly only wearing a white hood over his head to protect his identity. He died in 1982. In the one photograph taken of him unmasked — in an official setting before his identity became publicly known — he looks like a young Soviet official of the late 1940s: neat, slightly formal, entirely unremarkable. The documents under his shirt were the beginning of everything that follows in this compendium.

Soviet
Col. Rudolf Abel (William August Fisher)
KGB Directorate S · Brooklyn, New York · arrested June 1957 · exchanged February 1962

He was born in Newcastle upon Tyne to Russian émigré parents, joined Soviet intelligence in the 1920s, and spent the late 1940s and 1950s running Soviet intelligence networks from a studio apartment in Brooklyn under the name Emil Goldfus—artist and photographer. When the FBI arrested him in 1957, they found shortwave radios, cipher pads, a hollow shaving brush, and a hollow nickel that had once passed through a Brooklyn newsboy's hands containing a microdot. He named himself Rudolf Abel to his captors—a prearranged signal to Moscow that he had been taken. His FBI mugshot shows a mild-looking man in his late 50s who could be a retired schoolteacher. He was the most sophisticated KGB illegal ever caught in America, and the aesthetic template the entire program would follow for the next forty years.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Francis Gary Powers — Operation GRAND SLAM
CIA / Lockheed U-2 · Sverdlovsk, USSR · May 1, 1960

Powers was an Air Force pilot recruited by the CIA to fly U-2 reconnaissance aircraft over the Soviet Union at 70,000 feet — above the service ceiling of Soviet interceptors and surface-to-air missiles, or so the program's architects believed. On May 1, 1960, his U-2 was struck by an SA-2 Guideline missile over Sverdlovsk and he ejected. He was captured alive. The aircraft's wreckage — including the KH-type camera system and its film — was substantially recovered by Soviet forces. President Eisenhower initially denied the aircraft was on a spy mission; Khrushchev then revealed that they had the pilot and the wreckage. Eisenhower's cover story collapsed in real time. The Paris summit that had been scheduled between Eisenhower and Khrushchev collapsed with it. Powers was tried publicly in Moscow on espionage charges and sentenced to ten years. He served twenty-one months. On February 10, 1962, he was walked across the Glienicke Bridge in Berlin and exchanged for Rudolf Abel — the entry that opens this compendium. It was the archetypal Cold War exchange: one side gives back a man in an overcoat on a gray morning; the other side gives back a man in an overcoat on the same gray morning; the bridge connects the two halves of the divided city; both men walk toward their handlers without looking back. When Powers returned to the United States, CIA and congressional critics questioned why he had not activated the aircraft's self-destruct mechanism and had not taken his suicide needle. The criticism was largely unfair given the speed of the ejection sequence. He worked for Lockheed afterward and then as a traffic helicopter reporter in Los Angeles. He died on August 1, 1977, when his helicopter ran out of fuel near Los Angeles and he held the aircraft away from a group of children playing on a baseball field below. He was 47. The Glienicke Bridge exchange image — two overcoated men at opposite ends of a span on a winter morning — became the defining visual grammar of the Cold War spy trade. Abel had been on one end. Powers had been on the other. Neither had looked like what they were.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
The Glienicke Bridge
Havel River crossing, Potsdam / West Berlin · spy exchange venue · 1962–1986

The Glienicke Bridge — a cast-iron span crossing the Havel River between Potsdam and West Berlin — became, through the accumulated logic of the Cold War's prisoner exchange mechanism, the defining physical space of the spy trade. Its first exchange was February 10, 1962: Rudolf Abel walked from the East, Francis Gary Powers walked from the West, and each crossed without looking back. The bridge's geography made it the natural venue: the eastern side was Soviet-controlled East German territory; the western side was the American sector of West Berlin; the span carried no civilian traffic, only the weight of the ritual. The exchanges that followed created a grammar repeated across two decades — the overcoated figures approaching the midpoint from each direction in the pre-dawn cold; the brief transit at the center; the handlers who drove them back in silence; the brief statement that there was nothing to say. In February 1986, Anatoly Shcharansky was told at the last moment to walk in a crooked line across the bridge rather than straight — to prevent Soviet marksmen from using him as a ranging point for a final-moment shot. He walked crooked. He reached the West German side, picked up a handful of snow from the ground, and threw it into the air. The moment was photographed. In the photographs from the exchange mornings across twenty-four years — Abel and Powers, Shcharansky, the others — everyone on both sides looks like government officials who have driven out before dawn to stand at a bridge in winter and watch two men in overcoats walk toward each other. This is precisely what they were doing. The ritual worked because both sides found it useful. The men walking toward each other did not choose the bridge. The bridge had been chosen for them the moment they entered the business that brought them there.

Soviet
Konon Molody — "Gordon Lonsdale" · The Portland Spy Ring
KGB Directorate S · London, UK · 1955–1961 · exchanged April 1964

Molody spent part of his childhood in California with an aunt, acquiring an American English accent he later adapted to pass as Canadian. The KGB built him a legend: Gordon Arnold Lonsdale, born 1924 in Cobalt, Ontario. From 1955 he operated in the UK as Lonsdale—a small-scale businessman who sold jukeboxes and bubblegum machines, attended dinner parties in London, had a car and a social calendar and a reputation for being good company. In parallel he was managing a network of agents inside Britain's Portland Naval Base stealing Royal Navy underwater warfare secrets, and a support cell—Morris and Lena Cohen, operating as "Peter and Helen Kroger" from a house in Ruislip—who relayed the material to Moscow by shortwave transmitter. Acquaintances described him as charming, sociable, and thoroughly pleasant: the kind of company people actively sought. MI5 arrested him in January 1961. He was exchanged at the Heerstraße checkpoint in Berlin in April 1964 for Greville Wynne, the businessman who had carried messages for Oleg Penkovsky. The photographs from his British years show a pleasant-looking man in his late thirties at social functions who appears entirely at ease in the room. This was the design. Abel had been forgettable. Lonsdale was likable. Both worked.

Harold "Kim" Philby — Moscow Residency
KGB consultant (former MI6 / Cambridge Five) · Moscow · 1963–1988

Philby defected to Moscow in January 1963 and lived there for the next twenty-five years in a KGB-provided flat, advising on Western intelligence services and occasionally lecturing at KGB training programs. His photographs from the Moscow years show an elderly British gentleman in tweeds—a reasonable approximation of a retired Oxbridge don or a senior civil servant between appointments, except that he was in Moscow and had not aged particularly well. He was the most successful penetration agent in Western intelligence history: recruited by Soviet intelligence in the 1930s at Cambridge, he rose to become head of MI6's Soviet counterintelligence section—meaning that from 1944 to 1951, the man running British intelligence against the Soviets was a Soviet intelligence officer. He passed to the KGB the identities of every British agent inside the Soviet Union, the details of the Anglo-American intelligence sharing relationship, and the plans for Operation Valuable, the disastrous 1950 attempt to infiltrate anti-Soviet partisans into Albania. He died in Moscow on May 11, 1988. His KGB funeral was attended by senior officers. He had outlasted everyone. The tweeds were real.

Soviet
Guy Burgess & Donald Maclean — The Flight to Moscow
KGB / Cambridge Five (Foreign Office / British Embassy Washington) · London; Washington · 1934–1951 · fled May 25, 1951

They were both recruited at Cambridge in the early 1930s and both spent the following two decades in the upper tiers of the British foreign policy establishment while passing its secrets to Moscow. Maclean was serious, technically superb, and eventually head of the Foreign Office's American Department — with access to the full scope of Anglo-American postwar intelligence-sharing and the early architecture of Western nuclear policy. Burgess was brilliant, visibly dissolute, and spectacularly bad at maintaining any kind of cover; he was drunk at diplomatic functions in Washington and was eventually asked to leave the British Embassy for behavior his supervisors could no longer explain away. Both were Soviet agents since Cambridge. In May 1951, as MI5's investigation of "Homer" (Maclean's cover name in KGB cables) was closing in, Kim Philby — then posted in Washington as MI6's liaison to the CIA — arranged a warning. On May 25, 1951, Burgess and Maclean boarded a Channel ferry in Southampton and disappeared. They surfaced in Moscow. Burgess spent the rest of his life there drinking steadily, stocking his Moscow apartment with English books and Union Jacks, receiving occasional British journalists, and being visibly homesick until he died in August 1963. Maclean adapted better — he became a Soviet academic and wrote a book on British foreign policy, died in 1983. Their defection blew the Cambridge network open and initiated the investigation that eventually led to Philby. Of the Cambridge Five, they are the pair that look least like what the archetype demands: Maclean was the serious one, a tall gray-haired diplomat whose photographs across a Foreign Office career show a man of impeccable professional bearing; Burgess was the one who looked like what he was — a man who drank too much and had strong opinions and did not always behave well at parties. Both were KGB intelligence officers. One had the right look. The other didn't need to.

Soviet
Col. Oleg Penkovsky — HERO / YOGA
GRU Colonel (acting for CIA / MI6) · Moscow / London · April 1961–October 1962

Penkovsky was a GRU colonel with access to the Soviet military's most sensitive technical programs who approached Western intelligence voluntarily in 1960, was rebuffed initially, and made contact successfully in April 1961 through British businessman Greville Wynne acting as a courier. Over the following seventeen months he passed approximately 5,000 pages of photographs of classified Soviet military documents to CIA and MI6 — specifications and performance data for Soviet missiles, nuclear doctrine, vulnerability assessments of Soviet weapons systems, identities of GRU officers stationed abroad under cover. Codenamed HERO by the CIA and YOGA by MI6, he was handled by CIA officer George Kisevalter, among others. His motivation combined genuine ideological disillusionment with the Soviet system and what analysts described as personal vanity and a taste for Western consumer goods that the KGB subsequently emphasized in characterizing him. The intelligence he passed during the Cuban Missile Crisis in October 1962 was crucial: his earlier reporting had given US analysts the technical specifications to identify the missiles from U-2 photographs and to assess that the missiles were not yet operational, giving President Kennedy the margin to choose a naval blockade over a preemptive strike. He was arrested in October 1962 — the timing, in the middle of the crisis, has never been fully explained, and some analysts believe the KGB knew of him for months before acting. He was tried publicly in May 1963 and executed, probably on May 16, 1963. Greville Wynne was simultaneously tried and sentenced to eight years; he was exchanged in 1964 for Konon Molody. Of the figures in this compendium, Penkovsky may have had the highest direct impact on a specific world-historical outcome. In his GRU service photographs he looks like a Soviet military officer in his early forties, composed and precise, with the expression of a man who has made a decision about something important and is not planning to revisit it. He was not.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
James Jesus Angleton — CI Staff
CIA Counterintelligence Staff · Langley, Virginia · 1954–1974

Angleton ran the CIA's counterintelligence staff for twenty years — from 1954 to the December morning in 1974 when William Colby called him in and told him his tenure was ending. He had spent the war years at OSS in London working alongside Kim Philby, who was then his closest professional contact. When Philby's defection was confirmed in 1963, Angleton concluded that Soviet intelligence had penetrated the CIA at a senior level and that the contaminated intelligence product had been shaping American foreign policy for a decade. The mole hunt that followed consumed him and the careers of the officers it touched — most of whom turned out to be innocent, including Yuri Nosenko, the KGB defector Angleton concluded was a provocation, who spent years in CIA custody on his orders. He ran counterintelligence as a closed compartment: few officers at headquarters understood what the CI staff was doing; most were not cleared to ask. He maintained particularly close ties with Israeli intelligence and oversaw domestic surveillance programs that became, after 1974, central exhibits in the Church Committee hearings. He grew orchids at his Virginia home and gave extended, elliptical interviews to journalists who found him fascinating and impenetrable. He had been close friends with T.S. Eliot at Harvard. He drank steadily and wore impeccable suits and thought in recursive, multi-layered constructs that colleagues described as the "wilderness of mirrors" — Eliot's phrase, which he applied to Soviet disinformation and which became the standard metaphor for the entire counterintelligence discipline. He was right that Soviet intelligence had penetrated the CIA at senior levels. He was wrong about where. The actual penetrations — Ames, Hanssen — were found only after his death in 1987. In photographs from across his twenty-year tenure he looks like an East Coast attorney who reads deeply and sleeps poorly and has considered views about what the documents actually mean when you read them in the right order. He did. His conclusion was wrong. The files he spent twenty years building are the reason we know what was in them.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Frank Wisner — Office of Policy Coordination
CIA / OPC · Washington, DC; Frankfurt; Rome · 1948–1958

Frank Gardner Wisner was an OSS veteran from Laurel, Mississippi who joined the State Department's intelligence function in 1947 and was selected — at the direction of George Kennan and with the approval of DCI Roscoe Hillenkoetter — to lead the Office of Policy Coordination, established under NSC Directive 10/2 in June 1948. The OPC was not initially part of the CIA; it was a nominally independent covert action entity reporting jointly to State and Defense, grafted into the CIA only in 1950. From 1948 to 1958, Wisner ran the most ambitious peacetime covert action program in American history to that point: funding and building stay-behind networks in Eastern and Western Europe under GLADIO; supporting anti-Communist political parties in French, Italian, and West German elections; funding Radio Free Europe and Radio Liberty; running emigré resistance organizations that were sometimes penetrated by Soviet intelligence before they became operational. He was brilliant, driven, and eventually destroyed by it. The Hungarian Revolution of 1956 — when Radio Free Europe broadcasts, authorized under programs Wisner had built, encouraged Hungarians to believe Western military intervention would follow their uprising, and the West did nothing while Soviet tanks crushed Budapest — did something to Wisner that was not immediately visible. He had built the programs that gave false hope to the people who died trusting them. He visited the Austrian refugee camps after the Soviet tanks rolled; he saw the faces of the people who had believed the broadcasts. He suffered a psychotic break in 1958, underwent electroshock therapy, attempted to return to work, resigned from the CIA in 1962, worked briefly for the London station, and returned to his Maryland farm. In October 1965, he shot himself in his hunting lodge with his son's shotgun. He was 56. The operations he built — the stay-behind networks, the political covert action programs, the emigré support apparatus, the radio operations — are described throughout this compendium. The institutional and personal cost of constructing a covert empire that could not deliver what it implied is in this entry.

American
Richard Helms — The Professional
CIA Directorate of Plans / Operations · Director of Central Intelligence 1966–1973 · Langley; Tehran

Helms was recruited by the Navy during the Second World War from a European journalism career — he had been covering Germany for United Press in the 1930s, including a 1936 interview with Hitler — and joined the OSS in London, where he spent the war years learning the tradecraft he would practice for the next quarter century. He joined the CIA at its founding in 1947 and rose entirely through the Directorate of Plans: a career case officer who became the clandestine service's institutional memory, its operational conscience, and eventually its director. He served under Johnson and Nixon from 1966 to 1973, overseeing the Phoenix Program in Vietnam, Operation CHAOS (CIA domestic mail-opening and surveillance of American citizens), and the FUBELT operation against Allende in Chile. When Nixon ordered him to participate in the Watergate cover-up by pressuring the FBI, he refused. Nixon fired him in November 1972 and parked him as Ambassador to Iran, a posting he served dutifully while the CIA files accumulated. When he testified before the Church Committee in 1977, his account of the Chilean operation was artfully incomplete — technically accurate, strategically misleading about the full extent of CIA involvement. He was subsequently charged with perjury, convicted on two misdemeanor counts, fined $2,000, and given a two-year suspended sentence. When the verdict was announced, CIA officers spontaneously gathered to contribute to paying his fine. He wore the conviction as a professional credential. He was, in the judgment of most intelligence professionals, the model of the career CIA officer: technically superb, institutionally loyal, professionally discreet, and more loyal to the agency than to any individual president's preferences. He died on October 22, 2002, at 89. His photographs across fifty years in American intelligence show a slim, dark-suited man with excellent posture who could be an under-secretary of state, an attorney at a Washington firm, or a retired diplomat at a Georgetown dinner party — the same controlled expression at 40 and at 70, the face of a man who has spent a career knowing more than he will say about events that remain classified. He was the professional template. The men in this compendium who came after him were, in some sense, attempting to be Helms at different moments in the same institutional story.

American
Allen Welsh Dulles — The Architect
Director of Central Intelligence · CIA · 1953–1961

Dulles built the CIA as a permanent institution. He had come through the OSS in World War II — running Station Bern in Switzerland, making contact with German military officers willing to negotiate a separate peace, building the network of relationships that became the CIA's postwar European infrastructure — and joined the agency at its founding in 1947. As DCI under Eisenhower from 1953 to 1961, he oversaw Operation AJAX (Iran), the Guatemala coup, the U-2 program, the beginning of the Cuban operation, and, later revealed, a series of assassination plots against foreign leaders that would eventually become exhibits in the Church Committee's hearings. He built the institutional culture: the sense that the CIA was a permanent organ of American national power rather than a wartime expedient, staffed by men of education and discretion who understood that the country required a capability that could not always be disclosed. He was tall, white-haired, and pipe-smoking, and looked exactly like a retired State Department senior official or a senior partner at a downtown law firm who attended the right clubs and had interesting friends from the war years. He was the template for what the DCI was supposed to look like and what the CIA was supposed to do. He was fired by Kennedy in November 1961, ten months after the Bay of Pigs operation he had overseen failed in a way that exposed the agency's operational assumptions as fundamentally wrong. He served on the Warren Commission. He never spoke publicly about his dismissal. He died in January 1969. The institution he built, the methods he normalized, and the covert action philosophy he codified are the foundation of every operation in this compendium. The pipe was real.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
The Cuban Missile Crisis — Thirteen Days
CIA / DIA / NSA / ExComm · Washington, DC; Cuba · October 16–28, 1962

The Cuban Missile Crisis had two intelligence pillars. The first was Oleg Penkovsky's material — detailed intelligence about Soviet SS-4 and SS-5 missile technical characteristics and deployment procedures, collected by the CIA over 18 months, which gave American analysts the knowledge to identify the missiles in the U-2 photographs. The second was the U-2 photographs themselves, taken on October 14, 1962 by Air Force Major Richard Heyser, which showed Soviet missile installations under construction at San Cristóbal. Kennedy was briefed on October 16. For thirteen days, the ExComm — the Executive Committee of the National Security Council — met in secret while Kennedy maintained his public schedule. The intelligence problem throughout was unprecedented in its stakes: how quickly could the missiles be made operational? What were Soviet intentions? Would a naval blockade hold? The CIA's Office of National Estimates produced assessments that shaped each day's decision calculus. NSA's SIGINT provided partial communications intelligence. The DIA coordinated the military assessment. On October 27 — Black Saturday — a U-2 was shot down over Cuba by a Soviet SA-2 missile; a second U-2 accidentally entered Soviet airspace over Alaska; and Soviet submarine B-59 came within one officer's refusal of firing a nuclear torpedo (covered separately in this compendium through Vasili Arkhipov). The crisis ended when Khrushchev agreed to remove the missiles in exchange for a US non-invasion pledge and a secret agreement to remove American Jupiter missiles from Turkey. The missiles were gone by November. The thirteen days produced the Moscow-Washington hotline, the Limited Nuclear Test Ban Treaty, and a shared understanding that nuclear crises required direct communication between heads of state. In the ExComm photographs — Kennedy and his advisers in the Cabinet Room in shirtsleeves, legal pads on the table, the windows showing the Rose Garden — they look like men working a problem. The problem was whether the world would still exist on the 29th.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Vasili Arkhipov — The Second Signature
Soviet Navy, 69th Submarine Brigade · B-59, North Atlantic · October 27, 1962

On October 27, 1962 — the most dangerous single day of the Cuban Missile Crisis — Soviet submarine B-59 was being depth-charged by U.S. Navy destroyers in the Sargasso Sea. The submarine had been submerged for days without radio contact with Moscow. Its captain, Valentin Savitsky, concluded that war had begun. B-59 carried a nuclear torpedo with a yield of approximately ten kilotons. Soviet command authority for releasing the weapon required the agreement of three officers: the captain, the political officer, and the flotilla commander aboard — Vasili Arkhipov. Savitsky and the political officer voted to fire. Arkhipov refused. He argued that they lacked sufficient information to conclude that war had begun, that the depth charges were a signaling measure rather than an attack, and that they should surface and attempt communication. Eventually the submarine surfaced. The torpedo was not fired. Thomas Blanton of the National Security Archive, reviewing declassified Soviet documents in 2002, stated that Arkhipov's refusal probably saved the world. In the photographs from his Soviet Navy career — a compact, composed officer in Soviet naval uniform — he looks like a service-career Navy man of his generation and country. There is nothing in the photographs that indicates the specific day he made the specific decision that determined whether or not the decade that followed continued as it had been going. He died in 1998. His contribution was not publicly acknowledged until 2002, forty years after the event. He was in his late thirties on October 27, 1962, on a submerged submarine being depth-charged, looking at a man who wanted to fire a nuclear torpedo, and saying no.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Morris & Lena Cohen — "Peter and Helen Kroger"
KGB Directorate S · Ruislip, London · 1954–1961 · exchanged July 1969

They were born in the Bronx—Morris in 1910, Lena in 1913—and became Soviet intelligence agents in the 1930s through Soviet-aligned labor activism. During the 1940s they supported the atomic espionage network connected to Julius Rosenberg, handling material in New York. After the Rosenbergs' arrest in 1950, the Cohens disappeared. By 1954 they had resurfaced in London as Peter and Helen Kroger: New Zealand-born antiquarian booksellers. Project VENONA — The Signal in the Noise

Beginning in 1943, Army signals intelligence analysts at Arlington Hall began working on a collection of encrypted Soviet diplomatic and intelligence cables intercepted during the 1940s. The cables that had identified them could not be disclosed at their trial. Some VENONA decrypts were shared with British intelligence — where they were, presumably, available to Kim Philby, who was then MI6's counterintelligence liaison to the CIA. Whether Philby passed them to Moscow remains a question that VENONA itself cannot answer. In photographs from Arlington Hall during the 1940s and 1950s, the analysts working the problem look like mathematically-inclined young people at a government desk. The cables are text on paper. The National Security Agency was created by a classified Presidential directive in October 1952 — quietly enough that its existence went publicly unacknowledged for years, generating the informal designation "No Such Agency." The Helsinki Final Act — Basket III
CSCE / State Department · Helsinki, Finland · signed August 1, 1975

The Helsinki Final Act, signed August 1, 1975 by 35 nations at the Conference on Security and Co-operation in Europe, was structured as a trade. The Soviet Union and its Eastern European client states received Western acceptance of postwar borders (Basket I, security provisions), economic cooperation commitments (Basket II), and expanded trade relationships. In exchange, they accepted language in Basket III on the "human dimension": freedom of movement, freedom of information, and human rights. American conservatives at the time, led by Senators Jackson and Buckley, considered it an outright capitulation — recognition of Soviet territorial gains in Eastern Europe in exchange for paper commitments that the Soviets would ignore. They were wrong about the paper. The Helsinki process created the institutional infrastructure for the most consequential nonviolent resistance movements of the Cold War's final decade. Helsinki monitoring groups formed almost immediately in Moscow, Warsaw, Prague, Budapest, and across the bloc. The 1975 language gave Eastern European dissidents a framework within which to organize: they were not agitating for revolution — they were holding their governments accountable to agreements those governments had signed. Charter 77 in Czechoslovakia (signed by Václav Havel, among others), Solidarity in Poland, the Moscow Helsinki Group — all organized explicitly around the Basket III provisions. The KGB treated Helsinki monitoring groups as security threats, surveilling, arresting, and imprisoning members throughout the late 1970s and 1980s. The groups kept reconstituting. When Gorbachev's glasnost created political space after 1985, the organizational networks, leadership, and institutional memory formed around Helsinki compliance were already in place and already practiced in operating under state pressure. The Final Act is a printed document. Its consequences were written in the interrogation records of services that appear throughout this compendium — and ultimately in the dissolution of the empire those services protected.

Joint Task Force 1-79 · Iranian Desert / Desert One · April 24–25, 1980

Eight Navy RH-53 Sea Stallion helicopters launched from USS Nimitz on the night of April 24, 1980, and flew low-level into the Iranian desert toward a staging area designated Desert One. Three of the eight developed mechanical failures en route. With only five operational helicopters — the plan required a minimum of six to proceed — the mission commander, Maj. Gen. James Vaught, and on-ground commander Col. Charles Beckwith consulted and recommended aborting. President Carter approved the abort. During the repositioning at Desert One for extraction, helicopter number 8 hovered into the propeller arc of a C-130 transport aircraft. The collision ignited both aircraft. Eight American servicemen died. The burned aircraft, the bodies, and classified intelligence documents identifying CIA assets in Tehran were left behind. Khomeini put the wreckage on display. Jimmy Carter's presidency effectively ended in the Iranian desert on April 25th. The debrief produced seventeen specific organizational, training, and equipment failures. The conclusions became the founding documents of the modern American special operations architecture: the 160th SOAR, SOCOM, JSOC, the Intelligence Support Activity, a joint special operations training complex — the entire structure that produced the units described in the following sections of this compendium. In the pre-mission photographs at the Nimitz, the Delta Force operators and their support teams look like what they always look like: ordinary men at a routine briefing. Gen. Beckwith's expression is the same expression he wears in every photograph. The helicopters were parked on the deck. Everything was going to work. Nothing was going to work the way it needed to.

CIA Cairo Station / DIA · Cairo, Egypt · October 6, 1981

On October 6, 1981, Egyptian President Anwar Sadat was assassinated during the annual military parade commemorating the 1973 war, killed by members of al-Jihad who had smuggled live ammunition into the procession by concealing it in vehicles and on their persons. The intelligence failure was of a type that recurs throughout this compendium: general warning, accurate as to category and approximate timing, inadequate as to mechanism and actors. CIA was, in 1981, funding ideological cousins of the men who had just killed the leader of America's most important Arab ally. The chain of consequence from October 6, 1981 runs through the 1980s Afghan program and into the following decade. The Perimeter System — "Dead Hand"
Soviet Strategic Rocket Forces / General Staff · Soviet Union · developed late 1970s; operational 1985–present

The Soviet Perimeter system — known to Western intelligence as "Dead Hand" — was an automated nuclear launch command system maintained as a deterrent of last resort. Its premise was simple and terminal: if Soviet leadership was incapacitated in a first-strike nuclear attack before they could issue retaliatory launch orders, the system would issue those orders itself. Perimeter monitored seismic activity consistent with nuclear detonations, radiation levels, and communications blackouts at command facilities; if it detected indicators consistent with a nuclear attack and could not reach Soviet command authorities through normal channels, it could autonomously activate a network of specialized command rockets that broadcast launch signals to Soviet ICBM silos as they flew over Soviet territory. The missiles would then launch without any surviving human authorization. Perimeter solved the problem of decapitation: it made deterrence credible even if everyone empowered to order retaliation was already dead. It also created a category of nuclear war that could proceed entirely in the absence of human decision-making, triggered by sensor readings and communications failures rather than deliberate choice. The system was developed in the late 1970s, partly in response to American first-strike strategy research, and became operational in 1985 — the same year that Operation RYAN was actively monitoring for first-strike indicators, that Able Archer 83 had nearly been misread as a real attack, and that the Soviet leadership was most acutely fearful of American intentions. Its existence was classified. American intelligence had indications of its development but not a complete picture of its operational status during the Cold War. Former Soviet officials disclosed its existence in 1993. As of various Russian government statements, it remains operational. The bunkers and command sites that house the Perimeter system look, from satellite imagery, like ordinary military installations: hardened structures, antennas, fenced perimeters. The purpose encoded in their electronics — to continue a nuclear war when no one in authority survives to order it — is not visible in the imagery. It is the logical conclusion of forty years of deterrence theory: a decision, made once, that runs itself forever after.

BRIXMIS / SOXMIS — The Licensed Spies
British Commander-in-Chief's Mission to Soviet Forces Germany; Soviet Military Mission to British Forces Germany · East and West Germany · 1946–1990

Under the 1946 Robertson-Malinin Agreement and its American equivalent, each occupying power in postwar Germany was permitted to maintain a small military liaison mission in the other's zone — nominally for communication between commands, actually for licensed military reconnaissance. BRIXMIS, the British mission based in Potsdam, fielded teams in unmarked Land Rovers with diplomatic plates that drove through East Germany photographing Soviet and Warsaw Pact military equipment at distances close enough to read serial numbers on tank barrels. Teams collected discarded technical manuals and maintenance records from alongside motor pools, monitored training exercises, and measured vehicle dimensions that established weapons specifications that no technical collection could obtain. The American equivalent, USMLM, operated in parallel. SOXMIS — the Soviet mission in the British zone — ran the same operations in West Germany against NATO equipment and installations. Both sides harassed the other's teams: physical vehicle interception, deliberate routing through radioactively contaminated training ranges, and periodic "rammings" that were technically accidental. The value was substantial: NATO's first intelligence on several generations of Soviet main battle tanks, artillery systems, and helicopter gunships came from BRIXMIS photographs. BRIXMIS team leaders, operating in plain clothes in civilian vehicles with cameras and notebooks, produced technical intelligence at a cost far below any alternative collection program. They look, in surviving mission photographs, like English country-house officers on a field exercise — which is, in most cases, what they were, plus multiple cameras and a diplomatic plate on a vehicle driving past a Soviet motor pool. The missions ran for forty-four years, until German reunification in 1990. During the Pershing II deployment period of 1982-1984, BRIXMIS and SOXMIS teams were tracking each other's ground forces in a theater where both sides were deploying the weapons that would have started the war if anything had gone wrong.

Howard Hart — CIA Islamabad Station
CIA Directorate of Operations · Islamabad, Pakistan · 1981–1984

Hart was the CIA Islamabad station chief who built the Afghan covert action program from the ground up after the Soviet invasion — the operational predecessor to Gust Avrakotos, the man who established the working architecture with ISI's Directorate S and the framework through which the CIA funneled weapons and money to the mujahideen for the following decade. He was right about the operational environment. The deniability calculus aged less well: ISI's distribution priorities, which consistently favored Gulbuddin Hekmatyar's Hezb-i-Islami over other factions, were not CIA's priorities and the CIA had no control over them. Hart established the program and departed in 1984 before the questions about ISI's allocation decisions became impossible to avoid. The Islamabad station he handed to his successors — and to Avrakotos, who managed the program from Langley — was one of the largest and most complex CIA operations in the world. The men who ran it after him spent years navigating the consequences of the choices he made about whom in Pakistan to trust with the supply chain.

1985 — Annus Horribilis
CIA; NSA; FBI Counterintelligence · Washington, DC and worldwide · 1985

In the twelve months of 1985, American counterintelligence suffered the most catastrophic sustained series of betrayals in its history, nearly all occurring simultaneously and without initial connection to one another. In April, Aldrich Ames walked into the Soviet Embassy on Wisconsin Avenue and sold the Soviets the names of every CIA asset he knew, in exchange for $50,000. In May, the FBI arrested John Walker Jr. and his son Michael; Walker's ring had been delivering Navy cryptographic material to the Soviets for eighteen years. In August, Edward Lee Howard slipped FBI surveillance in Santa Fe using a counter-surveillance maneuver called a "jack in the box," reached Europe, and defected — the first CIA officer ever to do so. Also in August, Vitaly Yurchenko defected to the CIA in Rome, identified Ronald Pelton as the source who had sold the Soviets the details of NSA's IVY BELLS submarine program, and identified Edward Lee Howard by description. Pelton was arrested. In July, Oleg Gordievsky — a British agent inside KGB since 1974 — was recalled to Moscow for confrontation; British intelligence extracted him across the Finnish border in OPERATION PIMLICO before he could be arrested. In October, Jonathan Jay Pollard was identified as a spy for Israel. In November, Yurchenko walked out of a Georgetown restaurant and announced he was redefecting to the Soviet Union of his own free will. By December, CIA counterintelligence had reconstructed enough of the timeline to understand that Soviet assets in Moscow were being arrested in systematic sequence. The source of that sequence was Ames. He was not identified for nine more years. The counterintelligence officers reading the pattern in December 1985 look, in the conference room photographs from that period, exactly like senior government officials trying to solve a problem with insufficient information. They were. They were also sitting in the same building as one of the men responsible for the problem.

Maj. Gen. Yuri Shvets — KGB Washington Station
KGB First Chief Directorate (PR Line, political intelligence) · Washington, DC · 1985–1987

Shvets served as a KGB intelligence officer at the Washington rezidentura from 1985 to 1987 under diplomatic cover, working what KGB designated PR Line — political intelligence: the targeting of American political figures, journalists, policy analysts, and the influence networks that shaped administration policy. Shvets later became a journalist, an anti-Putin figure based in London, and a colleague of Alexander Litvinenko. He provided evidence to the UK inquiry into Litvinenko's death. In his Washington photographs from the mid-1980s he looks like a Soviet diplomat of the period at a diplomatic function: dark suit, conservative tie, the expression of a man compiling observations he cannot entirely report. He was. He reported it afterward instead.

American
The Moscow Rules — Tradecraft Under Total Surveillance
CIA/SIS/BND operational doctrine · Moscow, USSR · codified 1970s; operational throughout Cold War

The Moscow Rules were never a written document. They were the distilled operational discipline of working in a city where surveillance coverage was total, technical penetration was comprehensive, and any mistake ended the operation and frequently ended the asset. They evolved from the failures — missions that didn't work, sources that were rolled up, moments when behavior that seemed normal turned out to be visible — into a set of unwritten principles transmitted between generations of case officers. The core principles, as reconstructed from officer memoirs and training materials: assume every communication is monitored; assume everyone is potentially a provocation; never go against your gut; take your time; technology can fail; keep your options open; if in doubt, don't. These were not guidelines. In Moscow, they were survival conditions. The physical tradecraft built around the rules was equally specific: surveillance detection routes designed to pass through chokepoints where KGB teams would have to expose themselves; timings calculated to force surveillance to commit before the officer committed; brush passes — material transferred at a walk, without eye contact, in under two seconds — practiced until the motion was indistinguishable from adjusting a coat; signal systems that required no direct communication between the case officer and the asset. CIA Technical Services, under Tony Mendez and his colleagues, developed equipment engineered specifically around these constraints: containers that appeared to be ordinary objects, mechanisms that worked in winter gloves, communication systems that left no physical trace. The tradecraft and the technology developed together, each constraining and enabling the other. The Moscow Rules are the reason CIA officers operating in the Soviet capital in the 1975–1991 period look, in every photograph, exactly like what their diplomatic covers said they were. That identical appearance was not passive concealment. It was the result of continuous operational discipline, maintained every day for the length of a multi-year tour, enforced not by threat but by the certain knowledge that any deviation would be noticed by men who had been watching American diplomats for decades and knew what normal looked like.

He served until January 1993, overseeing the dissolution of the Soviet Union, the closure of scores of Cold War programs and stations, and the beginning of a transition to a threat environment the CIA had not been designed to understand. His institutional photographs from across the 27 years — the analyst, the DDI, the acting director, the DCI — show the same composed, academic-looking man at a government desk, aging gradually from junior official to senior, the expression unchanging across three decades of institutional transformation. He later became Secretary of Defense under both George W. Bush and Barack Obama. He is the figure of the intelligence community as permanent institution: not defined by any single administration or crisis, but by the patient accumulation of authority across every administration from Johnson through Obama. The government desk and the composed expression traveled with him for five decades.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
John Deutch — The Reluctant Director
Director of Central Intelligence · CIA · May 1995–December 1996

Deutch was a MIT chemistry professor and Deputy Secretary of Defense when Clinton appointed him DCI in May 1995, reportedly because he was the only candidate who could survive Senate confirmation and who the Pentagon trusted. He did not want the job — reportedly told the White House he would do it for two years and no more — and made no secret within the agency that he considered CIA's culture dysfunctional and its human intelligence collection capabilities inadequate. He was right about some of it. His relationship with the career clandestine service ranged from frosty to hostile; the inspector general's 1995 Guatemala report — which revealed that CIA assets on the payroll had been involved in the murders of American citizens and a Guatemalan insurgent married to an American — came out on his watch and forced terminations and reprimands that the clandestine service felt were unfair. He created a new standard for human source vetting that critics called the "scrub" and that field officers said made it impossible to recruit anyone with actual access to anything dangerous. He left in December 1996. Two years later, CIA security investigators discovered that for months he had been storing classified CIA documents — including clandestine service information — on personal laptop computers he also used for internet access at his home in Maryland. The computers had not been given the security configurations required for classified material. He was stripped of his security clearance. Clinton pardoned him on the last day of his presidency in January 2001, hours before the pardon deadline. In official photographs from the DCI year he looks like a professor at a government briefing — which is, accurately, what he was: a distinguished physical chemist trying to run an intelligence service he regarded with professorial skepticism, in a two-year term he had accepted with institutional reluctance.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
SVR Directorate S — The Program Continues
SVR (successor to KGB First Chief Directorate) · Yasenevo, Moscow · 1991 onward

On December 25, 1991, Mikhail Gorbachev resigned and the Soviet flag was lowered from the Kremlin. The KGB's First Chief Directorate, which had run Soviet foreign intelligence for forty years, survived the dissolution intact—renamed the SVR, the Foreign Intelligence Service of the Russian Federation, and relocated to the same headquarters at Yasenevo. Directorate S, which managed the illegals, transferred with it. The officers embedded in the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, and Australia under false identities received no instruction to surface. Their cover identities were unchanged. Their missions were essentially unchanged. Some had children who had grown up as American or Canadian citizens. Some had mortgages and car payments and library cards. The Cold War had ended for their neighbors but had not ended for them in any operational sense that affected their daily lives. The FBI's 2010 sweep—which arrested ten Russian illegals in a single operation—revealed how unbroken the program had been: several of those arrested had been embedded since the mid-1980s, their cover lives spanning the entire post-Soviet period. In the family photographs from the 1990s and 2000s, the illegal families look exactly like their neighbors at every neighborhood function. The organization that deployed them no longer existed under its original name. The families at the barbecues remained exactly where they had been placed.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Evgenii Primakov — SVR Director
Director, External Intelligence Service (SVR) · Yasenevo, Moscow · December 1991–January 1996

Primakov was appointed director of the External Intelligence Service on December 26, 1991 — the day after Gorbachev resigned and the Soviet flag was lowered. He had spent decades as an Arabist, academic, and trusted Soviet foreign policy operative: the kind of figure who had moved through the intersection of intelligence and foreign policy always in the clothes of an academic or diplomat, always with the deniability of an institutional role that was not quite intelligence. His task was to transform the KGB's First Chief Directorate — which had just lost its country, its budget, and its ideological reason for existing — into a viable foreign intelligence service for a government that did not yet know what it wanted. He kept the illegals program. He kept Yasenevo. He kept the analytical pipelines and the rezidentura network and the full institutional staff. He added diplomatic sophistication: he was the first SVR director with genuine foreign policy experience, and he ran the service less as a secret police operation than as a foreign policy instrument — more like the BND or the DGSE than like the KGB it had been. He served until 1996, when Yeltsin appointed him Foreign Minister — a trajectory that had not been available to KGB chairmen in the Soviet system. He later became Prime Minister. In his directorial photographs from the early 1990s he looks like a distinguished Russian academic and diplomat of the Brezhnev-era foreign policy establishment, slightly rumpled in the Eastern European academic tradition, with the expression of a man who has thought carefully about the situation. The institution he rebuilt survived him by several decades. So did its methods.

Soviet
Gen. Leonid Shebarshin — One Day as Chairman
KGB First Chief Directorate chief; Acting KGB Chairman · Yasenevo; Moscow · February 1989–August 1991

Shebarshin ran the KGB's foreign intelligence directorate from February 1989 through the August 1991 coup — the most consequential two and a half years in the history of Soviet foreign intelligence. He read the reports from Gordievsky's case as it closed. He supervised the rezidenturas that were receiving material from Ames. He was at Yasenevo when the Berlin Wall came down in November 1989 and when German reunification eliminated the HVA and the entire East German intelligence apparatus. On August 19, 1991, when the Emergency Committee announced its coup and Gorbachev was placed under house arrest in Crimea, Shebarshin was managing the FCD's response to an event that was simultaneously a constitutional crisis and a potential earthquake that could end the organization he ran. When the coup collapsed three days later and Kryuchkov was arrested at Vnukovo Airport on August 21, Yeltsin moved to dissolve the KGB by decree. For approximately twenty-four hours on August 22, 1991, Shebarshin was Acting Chairman of the KGB — the last officer to hold that title in the Soviet Union. Yeltsin appointed Vadim Bakatin as KGB Chairman the following day, tasked with dismantling the organization. Shebarshin retired and founded a private intelligence consultancy in Moscow that provided geopolitical and commercial analysis to Russian corporations — the first generation of the post-Soviet intelligence advisory industry. He wrote careful memoirs and gave thoughtful interviews. He was known for precise, slightly melancholy assessments of Russian foreign policy and Western misunderstandings of Russia. He died on March 30, 2012, of a gunshot wound in his Moscow apartment. The circumstances were ruled a suicide. He was 77. In his photographs from the FCD years — at Yasenevo, at diplomatic events — he looks like a Soviet diplomat of the Gromyko generation: composed, slightly formal, the expression of a man who has processed a great deal of information and arrived at his conclusions. He had. The organization he had run for two and a half years outlasted him by several decades under a different name.

Soviet
KGB Alpha Group — The Vote, August 1991
KGB Directorate A · Moscow · August 19–21, 1991

On August 19, 1991, the Emergency Committee that had seized power from Mikhail Gorbachev tasked Alpha Group with arresting Boris Yeltsin, who was barricaded in the Russian Parliament building—the White House—with tens of thousands of civilians gathered outside. Alpha Group assessed the mission. Their internal estimate was 500 civilian casualties minimum to execute it. They could do it. They had stormed the Tajbeg Palace in forty-five minutes in 1979 and eliminated the head of a sovereign state. On the afternoon of August 20, Alpha Group's officers voted—by show of hands, inside their unit—not to carry out the order. They notified KGB leadership that the mission was not feasible without a direct written order from the President of the Soviet Union. Gorbachev was under house arrest in Crimea and was not writing orders. The coup collapsed the following day. The unit that had ended the career of one head of state in forty-five minutes had decided, by vote, that this particular mission was not one they were going to execute. The Soviet Union dissolved four months later. In photographs from this period, Alpha Group officers look like men in their thirties and forties at an athletic facility. They do not look like the people who made that decision.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Marshal Sergei Akhromeyev — The Last Note
Soviet Armed Forces, Chief of the General Staff · Moscow · August 24, 1991

Akhromeyev was formed by the Great Patriotic War: a soldier since 1940, decorated at Leningrad, a career that ran from the Eastern Front through every decade of Soviet military history to his appointment as Chief of the General Staff in 1984. He negotiated with American counterparts at Geneva and Stockholm. He accompanied Gorbachev to Reykjavik. He was, by the late 1980s, the Soviet Union's senior military officer and a man who understood — and had spent his life serving — exactly what that meant. In August 1991 he flew to Moscow to support the Emergency Committee's coup against Gorbachev. He was in the Defense Ministry when it collapsed. He returned to his Kremlin office on August 24, 1991 — three days after the coup failed, two days before Gorbachev suspended the Communist Party of the Soviet Union. He left notes. One read: "I cannot watch the destruction of everything that gave meaning to my life. Age and everything I've gone through give me the right to leave. I fought until the end." He hanged himself. The rope broke; he tried again. He was 68. He had spent his adult life in service to a state that had ceased to exist while he was still alive to watch it end. His military photographs — the broad-shouldered uniform, the gold stars of a marshal, the precise bearing of a Soviet officer of his generation — show a man who looks exactly like what he was: the last of the officers who had been formed by a war and had served a system that they believed, correctly, would outlive them, and had instead outlived it. He was wrong about one thing. He left three notes total, all in his own hand, all written in the Kremlin office where he had served the Soviet Union until there was no Soviet Union left to serve.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Vympel — The Second Refusal, October 1993
Russian Federal Counterintelligence Service (former KGB) · Moscow · October 4, 1993

In October 1993, the constitutional crisis between Boris Yeltsin and the Russian Parliament produced a second standoff at the Moscow White House—the same building where, two years earlier, Alpha Group had voted not to arrest Yeltsin. This time Yeltsin was using force. After army tanks shelled the parliament building on October 4th and opened a breach, Vympel—which had survived the Soviet Union's dissolution as a Russian Federation security unit—was ordered to enter the building and secure it. Vympel's commanders refused, assessing the order as a political mission rather than a counterterrorism one. The building was secured by other forces. Two days later, Yeltsin signed an order transferring Vympel to the Ministry of Internal Affairs—effectively disbanding it from the intelligence apparatus it had been built to serve. Most of the unit's officers resigned rather than serve under MVD authority. The unit that had been created for wartime deep-penetration operations against NATO targets, and had trained for a decade to be the most capable sabotage force in the Soviet arsenal, was disbanded because it declined a political assignment. Alpha Group had refused an order in 1991 and survived. Vympel refused an order in 1993 and did not. In their final unit photographs at Balashikha, they look like men at the end of something. The photographs do not explain what that something was.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
GRU Spetsnaz — First Chechen War, Grozny
Russian Armed Forces · Grozny, Chechnya · December 1994–August 1996

Defense Minister Pavel Grachev told the press in November 1994 that a single airborne regiment could take Grozny in two hours. On New Year's Eve, Russian forces entered the city in armored columns without infantry support, without coherent air coordination, and without the kind of urban warfare doctrine the mission required. The 131st Maikop Brigade drove its armored vehicles into the city center and stopped at the central train station, where Chechen fighters — positioned in basements and upper floors with RPG-7s — destroyed the column at close range over the following eighteen hours. Estimates of Russian military dead in January 1995 alone range from one thousand to two thousand. The army that had defeated Nazi Germany, maintained nuclear parity with the United States for forty years, and garrisoned Eastern Europe through the entire Cold War era was being destroyed by will and rocket-propelled grenades in the rubble of a provincial city. GRU Spetsnaz and Interior Ministry special forces performed significantly better than line units — they trained for small-team urban operations, they adapted faster, they avoided the armored-column tactics that the regular army kept using after those tactics had demonstrably failed — but they could not compensate for the structural collapse everywhere else. The 1996 Khasavyurt Accords acknowledged Russian defeat after twenty-one months of war. Grachev's two-hour estimate had been wrong by approximately two years and ten thousand Russian lives. The GRU Spetsnaz who fought in the rubble of Grozny in January 1995 had trained their entire careers for war against NATO in Central Europe. They had not trained for this. The men who survived Grozny came out with a different set of operational lessons. The Russian Army that returned to Chechnya in 1999 was meaningfully different from the one that had entered in 1994. The men who made it different had been in the rubble in January 1995, and they remembered what had not worked.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Gen. Alexander Lebed — 14th Army, Transnistria
Russian Army · Tiraspol, Transnistria (Moldova) · 1992–1995

Lebed commanded the 14th Guards Army stationed in Transnistria — the strip of predominantly Russian-speaking eastern Moldova that had declared independence after the Soviet dissolution, backed by Russian officers, Russian weapons, and the presence of 9,000 Russian soldiers under Lebed's command. When active fighting between Transnistrian forces and the Moldovan government erupted in 1992, Lebed was the decisive military factor in the conflict. He deployed his forces in July 1992 and told both sides, with characteristic directness, that if the shooting did not stop he would make it stop. It stopped. He was blunt in a way that post-Soviet Russian military figures had rarely been in public: he held press conferences, gave candid interviews to Western journalists, and said openly that Russian security interests required Russian forces in place. He was not performing. He was the template for the post-Soviet Russian military commander as political actor: technically competent, contemptuous of civilian politicians, genuinely popular with the men who served under him, potentially very dangerous to anyone who mistook his directness for simplicity. He ran for the Russian presidency in 1996, came in third, sold his endorsement to Yeltsin in exchange for a national security appointment, used that appointment to negotiate the Khasavyurt Accords ending the First Chechen War, and was dismissed by Yeltsin five months later for being too prominent and too independent. He died when his helicopter struck power lines in Siberia in April 2002. In his photographs from the Transnistrian period he looks like a large, gray, close-cropped Russian military officer at a checkpoint who is not satisfied with how the day is going. He was not. He never was, anywhere. This is accurate.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Vasili Mitrokhin
KGB First Chief Directorate — Archivist · defected to UK, 1992

For twelve years, from 1972 to 1984, Mitrokhin was the KGB's chief archivist in the First Chief Directorate's foreign intelligence archive. He spent those years copying by hand the most sensitive operational records in Soviet intelligence history: agent networks, dead drops, clandestine meeting sites, technical operations, assassination plots, intelligence sources inside Western governments. He wrote them in tiny script on scraps of paper, hid them in his shoes, transferred them to typewritten pages at home, and buried them in milk canisters under his dacha. When the Soviet Union fell, he tried to give the archive to the CIA. They passed. He went to MI6. They sent a team to Estonia to dig up the milk canisters—six trunks of material—and flew him to England. His photographs show the world's most unremarkable man: cardigan, glasses, a face that has never caused anyone to look twice. He was carrying the most consequential intelligence archive of the Cold War under his vegetable garden.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Col. Stanislav Lunev — GRU Defector
GRU (Soviet/Russian Military Intelligence) · United States under cover · defected 1992

Lunev was the highest-ranking GRU officer to defect to the United States, crossing in 1992 — after the Soviet Union's dissolution but at a moment when Russian military intelligence was continuing operations with minimal institutional interruption under its renamed organizational identity. He had served as a GRU officer in the United States under diplomatic cover and had been tasked with — among other missions — identifying and pre-positioning caches of weapons and communications equipment for use by GRU agents in the event of war or a period when normal diplomatic access was severed. His debriefs confirmed the existence of this pre-positioning program and its general methodology. He testified before Congress in 1998 and described GRU's continued targeting of the United States after 1991, the operational continuity through the dissolution period, and GRU's ongoing interest in the RA-115 nuclear suitcase devices — portable nuclear demolition weapons whose existence he was among the first former Soviet intelligence officers to confirm publicly in a congressional setting. His book, "Through the Eyes of the Enemy," published in 1998, described GRU's collection priorities and tradecraft in terms that the American government did not publicly contradict. He lived in the United States under a protected identity for years following his defection. In congressional testimony photographs he looks like a middle-aged Russian businessman in a tie at an American conference table — which he was, in the only sense that mattered for that afternoon's session. The pre-positioned caches he had described, if they existed as located, were never publicly accounted for.

Soviet
Gennady Vasilenko
KGB officer · Moscow; Guyana; Washington, DC · imprisoned by KGB 1988, 2005; freed 2010

Vasilenko was a KGB officer who was genuinely, innocently friends with CIA counterintelligence officer Jack Platt. The KGB did not believe this was possible. They arrested him in Guyana in 1988, brought him back to Moscow, and interrogated him. They arrested him again in 2005, after Aldrich Ames had been in American custody for a decade and Robert Hanssen had been arrested in 2001—and the KGB had, by process of elimination, concluded that Vasilenko must have been the source. He wasn't. He spent years in Lefortovo Prison. Robert De Niro, who had become friends with Vasilenko through Platt, advocated for his release. Vasilenko was freed in the June 2010 spy swap—the same one that brought Anna Chapman home. In every photograph from his KGB years, he looks like a Soviet physical education teacher, which was his cover. He was genuinely friends with a CIA officer. That was his whole crime.

Soviet
Col. Sergei Skripal — The Career Arc
GRU / MI6 asset · Madrid; Moscow · recruited c. 1995 · arrested December 2004 · freed July 2010

Skripal was a GRU colonel who was recruited by MI6 during his posting in Madrid in the mid-1990s and provided Russian military intelligence for several years: the identities of GRU officers operating in Europe, Russian military capabilities assessments, the structure of Russian intelligence networks. He returned to Moscow, continued passing material, and was arrested by the FSB in December 2004. He was convicted of high treason in August 2006 and sentenced to thirteen years. He was released in July 2010 as part of the same spy swap that freed Gennady Vasilenko and returned Anna Chapman and nine other Russian illegals to Moscow — the exchange conducted at Vienna's Schwechat Airport in the same atmospheric grammar as the Glienicke Bridge exchanges fifty years earlier, with the same geography of men in overcoats crossing a neutral threshold in both directions. He settled in Salisbury, England. He fed ducks. He walked his dogs. He drove a used BMW. On March 4, 2018, he and his daughter Yulia were poisoned with Novichok nerve agent in Salisbury — a GRU-organized assassination attempt that nearly killed both of them, killed a local woman who came into contact with the discarded perfume bottle used to transport the agent, and produced the largest coordinated diplomatic expulsion of Russian intelligence officers in Western history since the Cold War. He and his daughter survived. The two GRU officers responsible were identified by British intelligence within days from CCTV footage; they appeared on Russian television claiming to be tourists who had come to Salisbury to admire the cathedral's spire. They looked, in those interviews, exactly like two men with something to explain who were trying very hard not to look like it. The cathedral's spire is 123 meters tall. Skripal's Salisbury photographs — a heavy, gray-haired man in a park, slightly stooped, entirely ordinary in appearance — are the photographs of the man his cover said he was. The cover was not false by the time those photographs were taken. He was that man. He had been that man for years. The GRU that trained him sent men to kill him anyway.

Soviet
Anna Chapman — The 2010 Sweep
SVR Directorate S · New York City · arrested June 27, 2010

On June 27, 2010, the FBI arrested ten Russian illegals in coordinated operations across the northeastern United States — the largest sweep of Russian intelligence officers in American history since VENONA. The network, which the FBI had monitored for years under Operation Ghost Stories, had been embedded across suburban New York, New Jersey, Boston, and Washington: couples with children, mortgage holders, some embedded since the mid-1980s, all operating under false identities built from the Directorate S model that Rudolf Abel had made famous in 1957. Anna Chapman was 28 when she was arrested in New York. She had a Russian passport, a Manhattan address, and an internet real estate startup with actual clients. She was not the most operationally significant of the ten — that distinction belonged to officers like the Murphys in New Jersey, who had been embedded for decades. She became by far the most publicly visible: her FBI arrest photographs were striking, her subsequent Russian media career was extensive, and she became, in the Western press, the face of the post-Soviet illegal. The cover at the time of her arrest was not deep: her communications with the Moscow Center had been monitored by the FBI for years. The network was exchanged in July 2010 at Vienna's Schwechat Airport — the same neutral geography, fifty years later, as the exchanges that run throughout this compendium. Chapman and nine others walked in one direction; Sergei Skripal and three others walked in the other. She returned to Russia and became a television celebrity, a model, a government official, and periodically a subject of Western press profiles. She looks, in every post-exchange photograph, exactly like what she became: a woman who was a spy and is entirely comfortable with the fact. The compendium opened with a man who looked like a retired schoolteacher in Brooklyn. It ends with a woman who looks like a television presenter in Moscow. Both worked for the same organization. Both were exactly what they appeared to be, and neither was anything like what they appeared to be. The photographs are the point. They always were.
