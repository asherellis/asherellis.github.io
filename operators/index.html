In the years between the fall of Saigon and the fall of the Soviet Union, the world's most capable men cultivated the world's most ordinary appearances. KGB illegals drove Chevrolets and attended PTA meetings. Delta operators wore polo shirts and grew mustaches. CIA case officers carried briefcases through airport terminals on three continents.

They looked exactly like your dad.

A compendium. c. 1975–1995.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Col. Charles "Chargin' Charlie" Beckwith
Founder, 1st Special Forces Operational Detachment–Delta · Fort Bragg, NC · c. 1978

Before he was the father of Delta Force, Beckwith was a halfback at the University of Georgia under Wally Butts. Before that he was a kid from Macon who liked football and the Army. He spent a year embedded with the British SAS in 1962—the first American to do so—and came back to the United States with a vision for a counterterrorism unit that he spent the next fifteen years failing to get anyone to fund. The Pentagon finally relented in 1977. He looked like a football coach. He coached football. He was also one of the most consequential figures in the history of American special operations.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Maj. Richard "Dick" Meadows
U.S. Army Special Forces (Ret.) · Tehran, Iran · January 1980

In January 1980, six weeks before Operation Eagle Claw was set to launch, Meadows flew into Tehran under a false identity. He was there to case safe houses, coordinate assets, and prepare the ground for a rescue mission that would extract 52 American hostages from the U.S. Embassy. He looked like an American businessman with legitimate reasons to be in Iran, which was not a simple thing to look like in January 1980. He had led the raid on Son Tay prison in North Vietnam in 1970, which recovered zero prisoners (they'd been moved) but was executed with such technical precision that the Army still studies it. He was retired. He volunteered for Tehran. He looked like nobody in particular.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Cdr. Richard Marcinko
Founding Commander, SEAL Team Six · Dam Neck Annex, Virginia Beach · 1980

When the Navy told Marcinko to build a maritime counterterrorism unit after Eagle Claw exposed the gaps in U.S. capability, he built something that looked unlike any military unit anyone had seen. He recruited from the Teams but took the weirdest people he could find: bikers, longshoremen types, men with beards and tattoos who had somehow ended up in the Navy. The unit's dress code was: civilian clothes appropriate to the operational environment. He was dishonorably discharged in 1992 for defrauding the government. Before that he built SEAL Team Six. The hair was part of it. The whole thing was part of it.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Delta Force, C Squadron — Snipers
1st SFOD-D · Fort Bragg, NC · c. 1990–91

Three men standing outdoors. One has a mustache. They are wearing the kind of clothes you would wear to go fishing or to a barbecue or to pick your kids up from school. Their rifles are visible in the background. They are some of the best snipers in the world. The mustache on the one in the center is not ironic. The photo circulated on JSOC Archive and became, briefly, the canonical image of what the culture of these units actually looked like during this period: competent, quiet, unremarkable. You would walk past all three of them in a grocery store without a second look. That was the point.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
CIA Special Activities Division / Ground Branch
Directorate of Operations · Central America, Afghanistan, Africa · c. 1982–1989

The officers of CIA Ground Branch—the paramilitary arm of the Directorate of Operations—had a uniform: khakis, hiking boots, and whatever shirt implied they were with an NGO or a mining company or an agricultural consultancy. In the 1980s they were active in Nicaragua, El Salvador, Angola, Chad, and Afghanistan. They trained and equipped insurgent forces, conducted direct action missions under Title 50 authority, and returned to Langley looking like they'd been at a conference in Denver. Photographed almost never. When they were, they looked like project managers from the Midwest.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Task Force Ranger
1st SFOD-D / 75th Ranger Regiment · Mogadishu, Somalia · August–October 1993

Operation Gothic Serpent deployed Delta operators and Rangers to Mogadishu in the summer of 1993 with the mission of capturing Mohamed Farrah Aidid's senior lieutenants. The pre-mission photos show men who look like oilfield contractors or private security on a boring assignment somewhere hot. On October 3rd and 4th, 1993, they fought for eighteen hours in one of the most intense urban combat engagements since Vietnam. Eighteen Americans died. Delta operators Gary Gordon and Randall Shughart were posthumously awarded the Medal of Honor. In their photos from before the mission, they look like guys you'd see at a Home Depot on a Saturday.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
KGB First Chief Directorate — Directorate S (Illegals)
Yasenevo, Moscow · Yuri Drozdov, chief from 1979 · c. 1979–1991

Directorate S ran the Soviet Union's most sensitive operation: embedding intelligence officers in Western countries under false identities, sometimes for decades. The officers—called "illegals"—were selected for their ability to learn languages without accents, to absorb the cultural texture of foreign places, and above all to be forgotten. They drove sensible cars. They kept tidy lawns. They joined local civic organizations. Yuri Drozdov, who ran Directorate S from 1979 until the Soviet collapse, described the program's core requirement as "the ability to disappear into the social landscape of the target country." The landscape was usually American suburbia. They disappeared into it perfectly.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Andrei Bezrukov ("Don Heathfield") & Elena Vavilova ("Ann Foley")
KGB Directorate S · Toronto; Cambridge, MA · c. 1985–2010

The Bezrukovs were KGB illegals embedded in Canada in the early 1980s. They moved to the Boston area in the 1990s, where Andrei—as "Don Heathfield"—obtained a Harvard Kennedy School master's degree and worked as an international consultant. Elena homeschooled their two sons, who grew up native English speakers and had no idea their parents were Soviet intelligence officers until after their arrest in 2010. In their Canadian and American photos: unremarkable couple, late 30s to early 40s, reasonable house in a reasonable neighborhood, family car. Rolled up as part of the FBI's 2010 Illegals Program sweep. Their sons, who were Americans by every measure, were left behind when the parents were exchanged.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
KGB Alpha Group (Directorate A)
KGB First Chief Directorate · Moscow · formed July 1974

Alpha Group was formed in 1974 following the Munich massacre as the KGB's direct-action counterterrorism unit—the Soviet answer to the question that Eagle Claw and Mogadishu would force the Americans to keep asking. Their early training photos look like images from a Soviet physical education program, which was the point. Men in nondescript athletic clothes running, shooting, and climbing on equipment that could belong to any training facility. In later photos from the 1980s, men in civilian clothes—jackets, slacks—stand together outside a concrete building. They look like colleagues at a Soviet technical institute. In December 1979, Alpha Group stormed the Tajbeg Palace in Kabul and killed Afghan president Hafizullah Amin, initiating the Soviet-Afghan War.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Vympel (KGB Directorate V)
KGB First Chief Directorate · Balashikha, Moscow Oblast · formed August 19, 1981

If Alpha Group was the Soviets' Delta Force, Vympel was something harder to categorize. It was created for wartime missions in foreign countries: sabotage of critical infrastructure, assassination of key Western targets, penetration of nuclear facilities. All officers spoke at least one Western language fluently. All had studied abroad or spent extended time in target countries. All had neutral faces—not handsome, not ugly, not memorable. This was not coincidence. The unit's founding selection criteria, as described in post-Soviet accounts, explicitly favored physical unremarkability. They trained at a facility in Balashikha. Their photo from that era could be mistaken for a regional volleyball team.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Col. Victor Cherkashin
KGB Washington Rezidentura — Counterintelligence · Washington, DC · 1981–1985

In 1985, Cherkashin received two unsolicited approaches from American intelligence officers who wanted to spy for the Soviet Union. The first was Aldrich Ames, a CIA counterintelligence officer who handed over the names of every Soviet asset the CIA was running. The second, a few months later, was Robert Hanssen, an FBI counterintelligence agent who handed over the names of every Soviet asset the FBI was running. Cherkashin handled both simultaneously, without either knowing about the other. He looked, in every available photo, like a mid-level Soviet diplomatic attaché at a Georgetown cocktail party. He was the most successful Soviet recruiter in Washington since the 1940s.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Aldrich H. Ames
CIA Directorate of Operations — Soviet/East European Division · Langley, VA · 1985–1994

Ames was not a KGB officer. He was a CIA officer, and the most successful KGB recruitment in the agency's history. Beginning in 1985, he handed over the names of every Soviet asset the CIA was running. At least ten were executed. He did this over nine years, pocketing roughly $4.6 million. He drove a Jaguar XJ6 to work at CIA headquarters on a GS-14 salary. Nobody asked. He was arrested in February 1994 and told the FBI he had compromised "virtually all Soviet agents of CIA and other US intelligence agencies." He looked, in both his CIA badge photo and his FBI arrest photo, like a man who had recently lost track of himself. He had. The remarkable thing is how ordinary he looks in both pictures.
