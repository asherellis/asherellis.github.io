A Compendium · c. 1975–1995

Cold War Dads

In the years between the fall of Saigon and the fall of the Soviet Union, the world's most capable men cultivated the world's most ordinary appearances. KGB illegals drove Chevrolets and attended PTA meetings. Delta operators wore polo shirts and let their hair grow out. CIA case officers carried briefcases through airport terminals on three continents and looked, to a customs agent, like mid-level regional managers on their way to a sales conference.

They looked exactly like your dad. This was not an accident.

KGB Directorate S. 1st SFOD-D. Ground Branch. Alpha Group. Vympel. SEAL Team Six. GRU Spetsnaz. The quiet professionals and the forgettable faces.

The Archetype
The Template That Held
UNCLASSIFIED
Soviet
Col. Rudolf Abel (William August Fisher)
KGB Directorate S · Brooklyn, New York · arrested June 1957 · exchanged February 1962

He was born in Newcastle upon Tyne to Russian émigré parents, joined Soviet intelligence in the 1920s, and spent the late 1940s and 1950s running Soviet intelligence networks from a studio apartment in Brooklyn under the name Emil Goldfus—artist and photographer. When the FBI arrested him in 1957, they found shortwave radios, cipher pads, a hollow shaving brush, and a hollow nickel that had once passed through a Brooklyn newsboy's hands containing a microdot. He named himself Rudolf Abel to his captors—a prearranged signal to Moscow that he had been taken. His FBI mugshot shows a mild-looking man in his late 50s who could be a retired schoolteacher. He was the most sophisticated KGB illegal ever caught in America, and the aesthetic template the entire program would follow for the next forty years.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Konon Molody — "Gordon Lonsdale" · The Portland Spy Ring
KGB Directorate S · London, UK · 1955–1961 · exchanged April 1964

Molody spent part of his childhood in California with an aunt, acquiring an American English accent he later adapted to pass as Canadian. The KGB built him a legend: Gordon Arnold Lonsdale, born 1924 in Cobalt, Ontario. From 1955 he operated in the UK as Lonsdale—a small-scale businessman who sold jukeboxes and bubblegum machines, attended dinner parties in London, had a car and a social calendar and a reputation for being good company. In parallel he was managing a network of agents inside Britain's Portland Naval Base stealing Royal Navy underwater warfare secrets, and a support cell—Morris and Lena Cohen, operating as "Peter and Helen Kroger" from a house in Ruislip—who relayed the material to Moscow by shortwave transmitter. Acquaintances described him as charming, sociable, and thoroughly pleasant: the kind of company people actively sought. MI5 arrested him in January 1961. He was exchanged at the Heerstraße checkpoint in Berlin in April 1964 for Greville Wynne, the businessman who had carried messages for Oleg Penkovsky. The photographs from his British years show a pleasant-looking man in his late thirties at social functions who appears entirely at ease in the room. This was the design. Abel had been forgettable. Lonsdale was likable. Both worked.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Harold "Kim" Philby — Moscow Residency
KGB consultant (former MI6 / Cambridge Five) · Moscow · 1963–1988

Philby defected to Moscow in January 1963 and lived there for the next twenty-five years in a KGB-provided flat, advising on Western intelligence services and occasionally lecturing at KGB training programs. His photographs from the Moscow years show an elderly British gentleman in tweeds—a reasonable approximation of a retired Oxbridge don or a senior civil servant between appointments, except that he was in Moscow and had not aged particularly well. He was the most successful penetration agent in Western intelligence history: recruited by Soviet intelligence in the 1930s at Cambridge, he rose to become head of MI6's Soviet counterintelligence section—meaning that from 1944 to 1951, the man running British intelligence against the Soviets was a Soviet intelligence officer. He passed to the KGB the identities of every British agent inside the Soviet Union, the details of the Anglo-American intelligence sharing relationship, and the plans for Operation Valuable, the disastrous 1950 attempt to infiltrate anti-Soviet partisans into Albania. He died in Moscow on May 11, 1988. His KGB funeral was attended by senior officers. He had outlasted everyone. The tweeds were real.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Morris & Lena Cohen — "Peter and Helen Kroger"
KGB Directorate S · Ruislip, London · 1954–1961 · exchanged July 1969

They were born in the Bronx—Morris in 1910, Lena in 1913—and became Soviet intelligence agents in the 1930s through Soviet-aligned labor activism. During the 1940s they supported the atomic espionage network connected to Julius Rosenberg, handling material in New York. After the Rosenbergs' arrest in 1950, the Cohens disappeared. By 1954 they had resurfaced in London as Peter and Helen Kroger: New Zealand-born antiquarian booksellers. They settled into a semi-detached house at 45 Cranley Drive in the suburb of Ruislip, joined local social clubs, and were known to neighbors as a quiet, pleasant couple who had traveled a good deal. Peter collected books and held occasional sales. Helen baked. The house contained a shortwave transmitter concealed in a cigarette lighter, a high-speed burst transmitter hidden behind a bookcase, microdot equipment in the kitchen, and approximately $6,000 in cash in a hole beneath the floor. When MI5 arrested them in January 1961—the same day as Lonsdale—Helen Kroger was being walked to a police car when an officer noticed she was clutching her handbag. Inside was a microdot package bound for Moscow. They were exchanged in 1969 for Gerald Brooke, a British lecturer imprisoned in the Soviet Union. In every photograph from their London years, they look like a retired New Zealand couple who have done moderately well in the book trade. This was an accurate description of everything MI5 could prove before they found the shortwave transmitter.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Christopher Boyce — "The Falcon"
TRW Inc. (CIA contractor) · Redondo Beach, California · 1974–1977 · arrested January 21, 1977

Boyce was 21 when he got a job at TRW Inc., a defense contractor whose satellite operations facility handled encrypted communications between CIA headquarters and the KH-11 Keyhole reconnaissance satellite program. His father was a former FBI agent with connections. The background check was light. He began photographing classified documents in 1974 and passed them to the Soviets through his childhood friend Andrew Daulton Lee—a drug dealer who traveled to Mexico City to meet with a KGB officer at the Soviet Embassy. Boyce spent the payments on falconry, hence the code name. He was arrested in January 1977 when Mexican police apprehended Lee outside the Soviet Embassy; Lee, facing drug charges, quickly gave up his source. Boyce was 23, had no formal intelligence training, maintained no operational cover, and inadvertently caused one of the most damaging satellite-intelligence compromises in CIA history—including the exposure of CIA monitoring of Australian domestic politics, which contributed to a constitutional crisis in Canberra. He escaped from federal prison in 1980, robbed banks for nineteen months as a fugitive, and was recaptured in 1981. His TRW badge photograph shows a young man who got a good job through his father's contacts. This is accurate. It is also the entire cover, which is the point: the most dangerous look is no look at all.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Col. Charles "Chargin' Charlie" Beckwith
Founder & first commander, 1st SFOD-D · Fort Bragg, NC · 1977–1981

Before he was the father of Delta Force, Beckwith was a halfback at the University of Georgia under Wally Butts. In 1962, he became the first American officer to serve with the British SAS—a year embedded with 22 SAS in Malaya that changed the trajectory of American special operations. He spent the next fifteen years trying to convince the Pentagon to build something like it. He wrote memos. He briefed generals. He was passed over for promotion and given bad postings. He kept writing memos. In 1977, Fort Bragg's post commander finally called him in and told him to go build his unit. He looked like a football coach. He sounded like a football coach. The unit he built was, and remains, the most capable direct-action force in the world.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
GRU Spetsnaz — 8th Separate Special Forces Brigade
Main Intelligence Directorate (GRU) · various postings, Warsaw Pact and Afghanistan · 1970s–1980s

The GRU—Soviet military intelligence—maintained its own special operations capability entirely separate from the KGB. By the early 1980s, Spetsnaz GRU comprised more than 30,000 operators organized into brigades of 1,000-1,300 men, with independent companies assigned to every Soviet Army and Fleet. Their designated wartime mission was to operate behind NATO lines: destroying nuclear weapon storage sites, airfields, and command facilities; assassinating NATO commanders; disrupting communications. They trained in the same language schools as KGB illegals. In their administrative uniforms they were disguised as paratroopers; in the field they wore whatever the environment required. In Afghanistan, Spetsnaz GRU conducted reconnaissance and direct-action missions from 1979 onward—the first extended combat deployment of Soviet special operations forces. They looked, in group training photographs from this period, like ordinary Soviet Army men at physical training. The photographs do not suggest what the unit's actual purpose was.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Gen. Yuri Drozdov — KGB First Chief Directorate, Directorate S
Chief of Illegals Directorate · Yasenevo, Moscow · 1979–1991

When Drozdov took command of Directorate S in 1979, he inherited the most carefully managed secret in Soviet intelligence: a worldwide network of officers embedded in Western countries under false identities, sometimes for decades. He expanded it. Under his leadership, Directorate S ran illegals in the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, West Germany, and Australia. The selection criteria were unusual for a military organization: ability to acquire foreign languages without an accent, capacity to internalize a foreign culture so completely that the officer could pass as a local in a random conversation with a stranger, and above all a neutral, unmemorable physical appearance. Drozdov described the ideal illegal as "a man who could sit on a park bench for six hours without anyone noticing him." He wore a cardigan.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
KGB Alpha Group (Directorate A) — Kabul, December 1979
KGB First Chief Directorate · formed July 1974 · operational by 1979

Alpha Group was formed in 1974 in response to the Munich massacre, when the Soviet leadership looked at a Palestinian terrorist operation and decided they needed something equivalent. Their group photos from the late 1970s show men in nondescript athletic clothes who could be a provincial volleyball team. On the night of December 27, 1979, Alpha Group operators stormed the Tajbeg Palace in Kabul, killed the Afghan president Hafizullah Amin and his guards in forty-five minutes, and initiated the Soviet-Afghan War. One KGB officer died in the assault, along with 200 of Amin's guards. The Soviet leadership had decided he was unreliable. The corrective measure took forty-five minutes. The Alpha operators involved received state awards at a quiet ceremony with no press.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Special Forces Detachment-A — West Berlin
10th Special Forces Group (Airborne) · West Berlin, West Germany · 1956–1984

When the Soviet Army crossed the Inner German Border, the soldiers of Detachment-A would stay behind. For three decades, a small cadre of Special Forces soldiers from the 10th SFG(A) at Bad Tölz maintained covers as West Berlin civilians—employed at American firms, the embassy annex, military support organizations—while holding a wartime mission that would activate the moment Soviet tanks entered the city. In that event, they would not attempt to leave. They would go to ground, activate pre-positioned assets, establish communications with Allied command, and conduct unconventional warfare operations in a city under occupation. They attended their children's school plays. They coached their kids' sports teams. They drove to the commissary on Saturdays for groceries, and maintained weapons caches and communications plans and exfiltration routes in the same city where their children went to school. Their wives were told some portion of what their husbands actually did; their children were told nothing. Det A was one of approximately thirty similar stay-behind networks established across NATO Europe under the GLADIO program—funded through CIA channels, organized through national intelligence services, maintained by ordinary men in ordinary covers with an extraordinary contingency. It was deactivated in 1984 as the Army reorganized its Special Forces structure. In their group photographs from the Berlin years, they look like American men of military bearing living abroad: the suburban fathers of a particular era, raising families in a divided city at the edge of the empire they were prepared to fight for from inside.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Markus Wolf — "The Man Without a Face"
HVA (East German foreign intelligence, Stasi) · East Berlin · 1952–1986

Wolf ran East Germany's foreign intelligence service—the Hauptverwaltung Aufklärung, the foreign intelligence arm of the Stasi—for thirty-four years. Western intelligence agencies had no confirmed photograph of him until 1978, when a source photographed him at a social function in Stockholm. The photograph showed a pleasant-looking man in his early fifties who could be a theater director or a professor of European literature. He was not KGB. He was, in many operational respects, more effective. His "Romeo operations"—recruiting attractive East German men to seduce lonely secretaries at West German government ministries, the Chancellery, and NATO headquarters—gave the HVA access to NATO war plans, West German defense policy, and the personal files of senior political figures across two decades. His most successful agent, Günter Guillaume, penetrated the personal staff of Chancellor Willy Brandt so thoroughly that when Guillaume was exposed in 1974, Brandt resigned. Wolf himself was a product of the German Communist émigré community—educated in Moscow during the Stalin years, shaped by genuine anti-fascist conviction rather than institutional loyalty. He wrote poetry. He retired in 1986. He was prosecuted by unified Germany after 1990 and received a suspended sentence. In every photograph from his career he looks like an academic of a particular Central European type: well-read, comfortable at conferences, a man whose face you would not retain after a conversation.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Maj. Richard "Dick" Meadows
U.S. Army Special Forces (Ret.) · Tehran, Iran · January–April 1980

In January 1980, Meadows flew into Tehran under a false identity and set up a cover as an Irish businessman. His mission: case safe houses, meet local assets, and prepare the ground for Operation Eagle Claw—the Delta Force rescue of 52 American hostages from the U.S. Embassy. He was retired. He volunteered. He looked like a retired American insurance executive, which was not an easy thing to look like in Tehran in January 1980. A decade earlier, he had led the raid on Son Tay prison in North Vietnam: technically perfect, prisoners had been moved, zero extracted, no operators lost. The Army still studies it. When Eagle Claw was aborted on April 25th after a mechanical failure and a fatal collision at Desert One, Meadows and his team exfiltrated independently. He was not captured. Nobody knew he had been there.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Antonio "Tony" Mendez — CIA Office of Technical Service
Chief of Disguise, Directorate of Science & Technology · Tehran; Hollywood, CA · January 1980

Six American diplomats who had escaped the U.S. Embassy takeover in Tehran on November 4, 1979 were hiding in the Canadian Ambassador's residence with nowhere to go. Mendez's solution was to create a fake Canadian film production company called Studio Six Productions, obtain real production permits from the Iranian Film Council, and turn the six diplomats into crew members of a science-fiction film called Argo. He flew into Tehran himself to deliver the documentation and walk them through their cover identities. On January 28, 1980, he and the six diplomats boarded a Swissair flight at Mehrabad Airport. The Iranian border agents stamped their production credentials and waved them through. What made the cover work was what always makes covers work: everything was real. Studio Six had a real Hollywood office on the old Columbia lot. A real phone listing. Real storyboards. Real production permits. In his OTS career photograph from this period, Mendez looks like an art director between assignments. This was not entirely inaccurate.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Cdr. Richard Marcinko — SEAL Team Six
Founding Commander, DEVGRU predecessor · Dam Neck Annex, Virginia Beach · 1980–1983

When the Navy tasked Marcinko with building a maritime counterterrorism unit after Eagle Claw exposed the gaps in American capability, he made a deliberate decision about what the unit would look like. He recruited from the Teams but favored the strange ones: men with beards, tattoos, and motorcycle jackets who had somehow ended up as Navy SEALs. The unit's dress code was: whatever fits the environment. Marcinko's own look during this period—shaggy hair, mustache, denim—was indistinguishable from a longshoreman or a biker or a bar owner. He was the commanding officer of the most elite maritime unit in the world. He was dishonorably discharged in 1992 for contracting fraud. He also wrote a memoir, and it sold. The hair was always part of it.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
William J. Casey — Director of Central Intelligence
CIA Director · Langley, Virginia · 1981–1987

Casey was Ronald Reagan's campaign manager, a former OSS officer from World War II, a Wall Street tax lawyer, and the first DCI to attend Cabinet meetings as a statutory member. He ran CIA covert operations at their Cold War peak: arming the Afghan mujahideen, mining Nicaraguan harbors without notifying Congress, trading arms to Iran through Israeli intermediaries to fund Contra rebels. He wore suits that had been wrinkled since approximately 1962. He mumbled in congressional testimony to the point where committee members routinely asked him to repeat himself. His briefings to Reagan were dense, fast, and referenced documents classified well above the room he was briefing in. He died of a brain tumor in May 1987—one day after Bob Woodward published an account of a deathbed interview in which Casey allegedly admitted knowing about the Iran-Contra diversion. His family disputed the interview had occurred. He was unable, by that point, to dispute it himself. His photographs from the director years show a slightly hunched man in a rumpled gray suit who looks like a retired securities lawyer being asked to explain a complicated tax shelter to a Senate subcommittee. Which is, in every practical sense, what he was doing.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Operation Urgent Fury — Grenada
Delta Force / SEAL Team Six / 1st & 2nd Ranger Bn · Point Salines, Grenada · October 25, 1983

Urgent Fury is remembered as the failure that built modern American special operations. The invasion of Grenada in October 1983 was the largest U.S. military operation since Vietnam, and it was chaotic. Delta Force's mission at Richmond Hill Prison—where American-aligned political prisoners were being held—nearly came apart due to intelligence failures and coordination breakdowns. Four Navy SEALs drowned during an evening parachute insertion into rough seas near Point Salines—their boat swamped, their motor died, and three of the four were lost. SEAL Team Six operators rescued Grenadian Governor-General Paul Scoon. Services couldn't communicate with each other on compatible radio frequencies. An Army officer famously used a civilian credit card to call in air support on a commercial phone. The Goldwater-Nichols Act came three years later. SOCOM was stood up in 1987. Every lesson learned in Grenada was learned because Urgent Fury demonstrated, in real time, what happened when the most capable operators in the world were sent in without the organizational structure to support them. In the pre-mission photos, they look like men at a normal briefing.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Task Force 160 / 160th SOAR — The Night Stalkers
160th Special Operations Aviation Regiment (Airborne) · Fort Campbell, KY · formed October 1981

The 160th was born from Eagle Claw. The lesson of the failed Iran hostage rescue—in which three of eight helicopters broke down before reaching the staging area—was that special operations needed dedicated aviation, trained to a standard the regular Army did not need. In October 1981, Task Force 160 was formally stood up at Fort Campbell from a cadre of 101st Airborne pilots. Their motto was "Never Again." They trained exclusively at night, at tree-top altitude, at speeds that had no peacetime application. Their aircraft were heavily modified: terrain-following radar, forward-looking infrared, suppressors, refueling probes. Their pilots looked, in their civilian clothes, like regional airline captains or flight instructors at a small airport. They flew Delta Force and SEAL Team Six into every major JSOC operation from Grenada onward. Nobody looked at them twice.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Intelligence Support Activity (ISA) — "The Activity"
Army INSCOM / JSOC · Fort Belvoir, VA (and forward deployed) · formed 1980

The ISA was assembled from the wreckage of Eagle Claw specifically to fill the intelligence gap that had killed the Iran rescue mission: American special operations had no dedicated human and signals intelligence collection capability to support hostage rescue or direct-action operations. ISA operators—drawn from throughout the special operations community—deployed in civilian cover to collect intelligence in environments where uniformed military personnel officially were not. Through the early 1980s they worked in El Salvador, Honduras, Lebanon, Libya, Iran, and across Europe. Their cover identities trended toward commercial communications contractors, agricultural development consultants, and similar technocratic roles with reasons to be in difficult countries. In 1983 a front organization called Yellow Fruit was established to support their Central America operations; a contracting fraud scandal in 1986 triggered court-martials and nearly killed the unit. ISA photographs from this period—always rare and frequently compromised by the unit's own operational security measures—show men with civilian equipment in settings that could be construction sites, communications facilities, or small business offices. The civilian clothes are ordinary. The equipment is not.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
1st Lt. John T. Carney Jr. — Desert One Advance Team
USAF Combat Control Team / JSOC · Dasht-e Kavir, Iran · January 1980

Three months before the Iran hostage rescue mission, Air Force Combat Control officer John Carney flew into the Iranian desert alone. His task: verify Desert One—a remote stretch of road in the Dasht-e Kavir salt desert selected as the helicopter refueling and staging point—and secretly embed landing lights for the follow-on force. He traveled by commercial air under cover, spent a night alone at the site with navigation equipment and a shovel, buried sensor pods and infrared strobes at each end of the planned runway, and exfiltrated without contact. On the night of April 24–25, 1980, when eight RH-53D Sea Stallions launched from the USS Nimitz toward Desert One, they were guided in by the lights Carney had placed three months earlier. Three helicopters turned back before reaching the site. The mission was aborted. During the abort, a helicopter and a C-130 collided and burned, killing eight airmen. Carney was on the ground at Desert One when it happened. He helped load the bodies. He flew out with the surviving force. In his photographs from this period, he looks like a U.S. Air Force officer in his late twenties during an ordinary duty day. There is nothing in the photograph that suggests the preceding three months.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Vympel (KGB Directorate V)
KGB First Chief Directorate · Balashikha, Moscow Oblast · formed August 19, 1981

Vympel was created for a category of mission that did not officially exist: wartime deep-penetration operations in foreign countries—sabotage of nuclear power stations, railways, and communications infrastructure; assassination of key Western civilian and military leaders; disruption of the NATO command structure in the opening hours of a conflict. All officers spoke at least one Western language fluently. All had studied or worked in target countries. All had what the selection board called "neutral physiognomy"—faces that did not invite a second look. Training photos from their Balashikha facility show men who could be physical education teachers or municipal engineers. The selection criteria explicitly favored those who were physically forgettable. This was considered a talent.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
KGB Service A — Operation Denver (INFEKTION)
KGB First Chief Directorate, Department A (Active Measures) · Moscow / New Delhi · 1983–1987

In July 1983, the Indian newspaper Patriot published a letter from an anonymous "well-known American scientist and anthropologist" claiming that HIV/AIDS was a biological weapon developed at Fort Detrick, Maryland, released from a laboratory accident. The letter was a KGB plant, written in Moscow by officers of Department A—the First Chief Directorate's active measures service—and transmitted through the KGB's New Delhi residency. Over the following four years, the story was amplified through Novosti Press Agency, sympathetic publications in Europe, and a network of front organizations. The East German Stasi, which coordinated its portion of the operation under the codename DENVER, contributed forged documents and arranged testimony from compliant academics. By 1986 the story had been republished in more than 80 countries. By 1987 it had appeared in mainstream Western media. The State Department published a report specifically rebutting it. The KGB assessed INFEKTION as one of its most successful active measures operations of the decade. The officers who ran it worked out of offices in central Moscow and attended international journalism conferences as academics or press officers. They looked like academics or press officers. This was not coincidental.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Col. Vladimir Vetrov — "FAREWELL"
KGB Directorate T (Line X, scientific-technical intelligence) · Moscow; Paris · 1981–1982

In 1981, Vetrov began passing KGB documents to French intelligence through a French business contact in Moscow. His code name was FAREWELL. Over fourteen months he provided 4,000 classified documents revealing the entire architecture of Soviet technology-theft operations in the West: the identities of 250 Line X officers operating across NATO countries, the structure of KGB technical collection networks, and a list of 35,000 Western technology items the Soviets were actively attempting to acquire. Reagan was briefed. In 1983, 47 Soviet intelligence officers were expelled from Western countries simultaneously—the largest coordinated expulsion in Cold War history. Vetrov was not suspected. He was arrested in 1982—not for espionage but for attacking a Moscow policeman during a domestic dispute that had turned violent. During the murder investigation that followed, the espionage was discovered. He was executed by firing squad in 1985. His KGB personnel photograph shows a heavyset man in his early forties in a Soviet bureaucratic interior who looks like a department head in a medium-sized ministry. He was, in that photograph, carrying four thousand documents out of the building under his coat.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
William F. Buckley
CIA Station Chief, Beirut · captured March 16, 1984 · died June 1985

On March 16, 1984, Buckley was taken outside his Beirut apartment by Islamic Jihad, a Hezbollah-linked group operating at Iranian direction. He was the CIA's station chief and the most valuable intelligence officer in the most important intelligence station in the Middle East. His captors systematically tortured him for fourteen months, extracting what the CIA later assessed was a comprehensive account of American intelligence assets and methods in the region. Videotapes of the interrogations were delivered to the CIA station in Athens. He died in June 1985. His remains were identified in December 1991. His CIA badge photo shows a quiet man in a jacket and tie who looks like a professor at a small university. He had spent 30 years in the agency. Nobody who saw him in Beirut suspected he was anything other than what his cover said.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
7th Special Forces Group — El Salvador
U.S. Army Special Forces, 7th SFG(A) · Ilopango Air Base and elsewhere, El Salvador · 1981–1992

Congress restricted U.S. military presence in El Salvador to 55 advisors. The 7th Special Forces Group, which had the Latin America mission, provided ODA teams that rotated through the country under this ceiling—training Salvadoran Army and security forces in small-unit tactics, marksmanship, and counterinsurgency operations. Congressional sensitivity about American military involvement meant advisors worked under significant constraints: they were not to accompany Salvadoran units on combat operations. They did not always observe this restriction. The photographs that periodically emerged from their deployments—and that periodically caused congressional controversy—show American soldiers in civilian clothes or unmarked camouflage, beards often present, in settings that could be agricultural extension programs or development projects in a rural country. They look like Peace Corps volunteers who have made different choices. Some of the photographs from this period became documentary subjects in their own right: the look they captured—competent, informal, relaxed in a dangerous environment—became the template against which all subsequent SOF photography would be measured.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Jack Platt & Gennady Vasilenko
CIA / KGB · Washington, DC · 1978–ongoing

In 1978, CIA counterintelligence officer Jack Platt and KGB officer Gennady Vasilenko arrived in Washington within months of each other. Both were assigned to recruit the other. Instead, they became friends. They played tennis. They drank beer. They wore polo shirts and had mustaches that were indistinguishable from each other's. Neither was able to definitively say, in the early years, whether the friendship was a recruitment effort by the other or something genuine. It was something genuine. The KGB eventually arrested Vasilenko multiple times on suspicion of being a CIA asset—he wasn't, but his friendship with Platt made him look like one. He spent years in Soviet prisons. He was freed in the 2010 spy swap alongside Anna Chapman and nine other Russian illegals. Robert De Niro reportedly intervened on his behalf. The polo shirt period predated all of this.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Operation RYAN — Able Archer 83
KGB First Chief Directorate / GRU · worldwide · November 7–11, 1983

RYAN (Raketno-Yadernoye Napadeniye — Rocket-Nuclear Attack) was a KGB/GRU program established in 1981 to monitor Western indicators of preparations for a nuclear first strike. Rezidenturas worldwide were tasked with watching specific signals: unusual activity at blood banks, government personnel movements from cities at odd hours, increased lighting at military installations, changes in the patterns of official limousine traffic. When NATO ran Able Archer 83 in November 1983—a more realistic nuclear-release exercise than previous years, simulating an escalation from conventional to nuclear war—the RYAN monitoring network interpreted it as a possible real event. Soviet long-range aviation units in East Germany and Poland were put on elevated alert. KGB and GRU rezidenturas filed urgent reporting. The Soviet leadership came closer to interpreting a Western military exercise as a real first strike than at any point since the Cuban Missile Crisis. Gordievsky's intelligence about RYAN and Able Archer reached Margaret Thatcher and Ronald Reagan. Reagan, genuinely disturbed by how frightened the Soviets actually were of American intentions, moderated his anti-Soviet rhetoric. Future NATO exercises were made less realistic. The monitoring network that nearly misread a war game as war was staffed by professional intelligence officers in ordinary offices in Moscow, filing ordinary intelligence reports on ordinary schedule. The reports were not ordinary.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Gust Avrakotos — CIA Near East Division, Afghan Program
CIA Directorate of Operations · Langley, VA / Islamabad, Pakistan · 1984–1986

Avrakotos grew up in Aliquippa, Pennsylvania, the son of Greek immigrants who ran a tavern. He joined the CIA in 1962 and spent the following two decades in field operations across Europe and Africa. He wore bowling shirts to CIA headquarters. He once smashed a superior's glass office partition during an argument over operational authority. In 1984 he was given the Afghan account and spent the next two years running what he called "a bureaucratic covert war"—arguing with headquarters, assembling what colleagues called "the dirty dozen," a team of experienced case officers who operated outside normal bureaucratic channels, and building the pipeline that moved money, weapons, and intelligence through Pakistan to the mujahideen. He was replaced by Milton Bearden in 1986 after disagreements with management. By the time Bearden arrived, the program was delivering $700 million per year in materiel to 250,000 fighters and the decision to provide FIM-92 Stinger missiles—the decision that ended Soviet air superiority and changed the war—had been approved. Avrakotos took no public credit and gave no interviews. He told colleagues he was "a plumber who fixed other people's broken pipes." He died in 2005. His CIA career photograph shows a heavyset man in late middle age who could be a contractor in any industry, looking past the camera as though he has somewhere else to be. He did.

Soviet
Col. Victor Cherkashin — Washington Rezidentura
KGB First Chief Directorate, Line KR (Counterintelligence) · Washington, DC · 1981–1985

In April 1985, Aldrich Ames walked into the Soviet Embassy on Wisconsin Avenue in Washington and handed an envelope to Cherkashin. The envelope contained the names of three Soviet citizens Ames assessed were working for the CIA, and an offer to provide more. A few months later, Robert Hanssen mailed a letter to the Washington KGB residency containing classified material and an offer to cooperate. Cherkashin handled both of them, simultaneously, without either knowing about the other. He looked, in every available photograph from this period, like a mid-level Soviet diplomat at a Georgetown cocktail party, which was the cover. In his memoir he writes that he never thought of himself as a spy. He thought of himself as a diplomat. This is, in most ways, exactly what made him so effective.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
WO John Anthony Walker Jr. — The Walker Ring
USN (Ret.) · Norfolk, VA · 1967–1985 · arrested May 20, 1985

In 1967, Walker walked into the Soviet Embassy in Washington and handed the duty officer a month's worth of Navy cryptographic key lists, along with a note explaining what he wanted in return. He did this for seventeen years. During that time he recruited his brother Arthur Walker (Navy reservist), his son Michael Walker (active-duty Navy seaman), and his friend Jerry Whitworth (Navy radioman). The NSA's post-arrest assessment called it the most damaging compromise in U.S. Naval history—the Soviets could decrypt at least one million classified messages. Walker did this out of a private detective office he ran in Norfolk, Virginia. He drove a modest car. He wore the kind of clothes a retired Navy guy might wear. He had a family. He told the Soviets who he had recruited while they were still active duty. When the FBI arrested him in 1985, they found KGB tradecraft materials in his car. His arrest photograph—portly, balding, slightly disheveled—is one of the great documents of this era. He looks like a man who just got pulled over for a minor traffic violation.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Gen. Dmitri Polyakov — "BOURBON" / "TOPHAT"
GRU General (CIA/FBI asset 1961–1986) · Washington; New Delhi; Moscow · arrested 1986 · executed 1988

Polyakov was the GRU's highest-ranking officer ever to work as an American asset. He began providing intelligence to the FBI in 1961—not for money but out of genuine contempt for the Soviet leadership. Over twenty-five years he passed the identities of hundreds of Soviet intelligence officers, details of Soviet military doctrine and weapons specifications, and the names of Soviet assets inside Western services. He was a hunter and fisherman. He sent his sons to Soviet universities. He drove Soviet cars. He asked for nothing. Western analysts assessed his information as the most valuable intelligence provided to any Western service during the Cold War. He was betrayed by Aldrich Ames in June 1985—named in the first batch of sources Ames sold to the KGB at the Soviet Embassy on Wisconsin Avenue. The KGB arrested him in 1986. He was executed in 1988. In his photographs from his various military attaché postings—Washington, New Delhi—he looks like a senior Soviet military officer doing administrative work abroad: heavy-set, gray-haired, a face shaped by decades of institutional life in the Soviet Army. Nothing in the photographs suggests what was happening in parallel. He was one of the most effective intelligence assets of the twentieth century, and he looked exactly like what his uniform said he was.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Robert Philip Hanssen
FBI Special Agent, Intelligence Division (and KGB asset) · Washington, DC · 1979–2001

Hanssen began selling secrets to the GRU in November 1979, eighteen months after joining the FBI's counterintelligence division. He stopped on his own initiative in 1981, then restarted with the KGB in 1985—the same year Aldrich Ames made his first approach at the Soviet Embassy on Wisconsin Avenue. He never knew about Ames. Ames never knew about him. The CIA's best sources were being betrayed by the CIA's own counterintelligence analyst; the FBI's sources were being betrayed by the FBI's own counterintelligence analyst. Neither organization knew. For twenty-two years, Hanssen drove an Oldsmobile, attended 6:30 a.m. mass at a Northern Virginia parish, coached his daughter's soccer team, and delivered thousands of documents and disks to Russian intelligence via dead drops in suburban parks. He was arrested on February 18, 2001, placing a garbage bag at a dead drop in Foxstone Park, Vienna, Virginia, two months before mandatory retirement. His FBI badge photo shows a man who looks like a deacon at a Catholic parish. He was an Opus Dei member. The badge photo is accurate.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Ronald Pelton — Operation IVY BELLS
NSA Signals Intelligence · Fort Meade, MD / Washington, DC · 1980–1985 · arrested November 24, 1985

Pelton resigned from the NSA in 1979 due to personal bankruptcy—he owed $65,000 and owned almost nothing. In January 1980, he walked into the Soviet Embassy in Washington and offered to sell what he knew. He knew a great deal. He had spent fourteen years as an NSA communications analyst and had memorized enough about the agency's technical collection programs to damage them severely. The most critical information he sold was the existence of Operation IVY BELLS: a joint NSA/Navy program in which U.S. Navy divers had placed a recording device on a Soviet military communications cable on the floor of the Sea of Okhotsk. The cable carried unencrypted traffic between the Soviet Pacific Fleet's headquarters on Kamchatka and Moscow; NSA analysts assessed it as one of the most productive signals collection operations in the agency's history. After Pelton's approach, the Soviets dispatched a recovery vessel to the Sea of Okhotsk and retrieved the tap. It is now on display in Moscow. Pelton received $35,000 in total payments. He drove an aging car. He was portly, financially ruined, and had a face that expressed nothing in particular. He was identified in August 1985 by Vitaly Yurchenko—who had personally reviewed the file—during Yurchenko's brief defection to the CIA. He was arrested in November 1985. Yurchenko re-defected to Moscow two days later. Pelton was convicted and sentenced to three concurrent life terms. He was freed in 2015 after thirty years. His courthouse photograph shows a slightly dazed-looking man in a suit that doesn't quite fit. He looks exactly like a mid-career federal employee facing a bad day.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Aldrich H. Ames
CIA Directorate of Operations, Soviet/East European Division · Langley, VA · 1985–1994

Ames was not a KGB officer. He was a CIA counterintelligence analyst—the man responsible for coordinating the agency's Soviet sources—who in April 1985 began selling those sources to the KGB for money. At least ten were executed. He did this for nine years. He earned approximately $4.6 million. He drove a Jaguar XJ6 to CIA headquarters on a GS-14 government salary. His supervisors noticed. They filed a report expressing concern. The report sat. He was arrested in February 1994. His FBI arrest photo—taken at his home in Arlington—shows a slightly dazed man in his early 50s in rumpled clothes who looks as though he's been pulled from bed at an inconvenient hour. He had. The remarkable thing about the photo is that it looks exactly like every other photo of him from the previous decade. Nothing in his appearance, in any photograph from any year, suggests what he was.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Col. Oleg Gordievsky — Operation PIMLICO
KGB London Rezidentura (and MI6 asset from 1974) · exfiltrated July 19, 1985

Gordievsky was the KGB's station chief in London. He was also an MI6 asset who had been providing intelligence to the British since 1974. His intelligence about Operation Ryan—the Soviet program monitoring for signs of a Western first strike—and his account of how Able Archer 83, a NATO nuclear exercise, had been interpreted in Moscow as potentially real, contributed directly to Reagan's decision to tone down his anti-Soviet rhetoric. In May 1985, Ames told the KGB there was a British asset within the KGB's London station. Moscow recalled Gordievsky. On July 19, 1985, he shook his surveillance team and made his way to the Finnish border. British intelligence officers, traveling in diplomatic cars, picked him up near Vyborg and hid him in the trunk of their vehicle. At the Finnish border, an MI6 officer's wife changed her baby's dirty diaper on the trunk of the car at the checkpoint, distracting the KGB's trained dogs. He heard Sibelius' Finlandia on the car radio when they crossed the border.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Edward Lee Howard — The Escape
CIA Clandestine Service (and KGB source) · Santa Fe, New Mexico · September 21, 1985

Howard had been specifically trained for the Moscow station—the most demanding assignment in the CIA's clandestine service—and then fired in 1983 after failing a polygraph about admitted drug use and petty theft. Over the next two years, living in Santa Fe with his wife and infant son, he sold the CIA's Moscow-station collection to the KGB: the names of Soviet assets, American collection methods inside the USSR, the technical operations that provided Western intelligence access to Soviet communications. Sources were rolled up. People died. In September 1985, the FBI moved to arrest him. On the night of September 21, Howard and his wife drove out of Santa Fe through a practiced countersurveillance sequence. At a bend in the road, Howard rolled out of the passenger door. His wife placed an inflatable dummy in the seat and continued home through the routine of a suburban evening. The FBI surveillance team, following at distance, tracked the car back to the house without realizing he was gone. He was on a plane to Austria by the following morning. He died in Moscow in 2002. He is the only CIA officer to successfully escape American law enforcement during the Cold War. The FBI learned he was gone when they called the house the next day.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Gen. Vitaly Yurchenko — Au Pied de Cochon
KGB Fifth Directorate / Washington Station · defected August 1985; re-defected November 1985

Yurchenko defected to the CIA in Rome on August 1, 1985 and was assessed as the most valuable Soviet defector in years. Over three months, he identified two Americans working for Soviet intelligence: NSA analyst Ronald Pelton (arrested November 1985) and CIA officer Edward Lee Howard (who escaped to the Soviet Union before arrest). On November 2, 1985, Yurchenko and his CIA handler were having dinner at Au Pied de Cochon, a French restaurant on Wisconsin Avenue in Georgetown. He turned to his handler and said: "What would you do if I got up and walked out? Would you shoot me?" His handler said no. "I'll be back in 15 or 20 minutes," Yurchenko said. "If I'm not, it will not be your fault." He did not come back. Two days later, the Soviet Embassy held a press conference at which Yurchenko, in a suit and tie, said he had been kidnapped and drugged by the CIA. He flew to Moscow. He wore a beige suit to the Soviet press conference.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Lt. Col. Oliver North — The Basement Office
NSC Staff, Old Executive Office Building · Washington, DC · 1981–1986

North ran the NSC's covert operations from a basement office in the Old Executive Office Building, wearing his Marine dress uniform to work every day. The Iran-Contra operation—trading arms to Iran through Israeli intermediaries, diverting the proceeds to fund Nicaraguan Contra rebels in violation of the Boland Amendment—was coordinated from his desk. When the operation became public in November 1986, North and his secretary Fawn Hall spent three days shredding documents. Hall smuggled additional pages out of the building inside her clothes. In July 1987, North testified before Congress for six days in his Marine uniform, his medals laid out on the table before him. He said he assumed the President knew. He said he would do it again. He became, briefly, the most recognized face in America. The uniform was perfect. The bearing was perfect. The answers were fluent and said nearly nothing. He looked exactly like the Marine officer he was—and he was exactly the sort of person who could end up coordinating covert foreign policy from a basement, which is both a critique and a description of the kind of institutional competence that makes itself useful in ways that eventually become problems. He was convicted on three felony counts. The convictions were later vacated on procedural grounds.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Andrei Bezrukov ("Don Heathfield") & Elena Vavilova ("Ann Foley")
KGB Directorate S · Toronto, then Cambridge, MA · c. 1985–2010

The Bezrukovs were KGB illegals embedded in Canada in the early 1980s, living as "Don Heathfield" and "Ann Foley." They relocated to the Boston area in the 1990s. Andrei—as Heathfield—obtained a Harvard Kennedy School master's degree and worked as an international business consultant. Elena ran their household and taught their two sons, who grew up as native English speakers and had no idea their parents were KGB officers. The family photographs from this period show exactly what you would expect: a professional couple in their late thirties or early forties, two boys, a reasonable house in a reasonable suburb of Boston, a sensible family car. In June 2010, they were among ten Russian illegals arrested in an FBI sweep. Their sons—who were American in every way that mattered—were left in the United States when their parents were exchanged.

Soviet
Lt. Col. Vladimir Putin — KGB Residency, Dresden
KGB First Chief Directorate · Dresden, East Germany · 1985–1990

Putin arrived in Dresden in 1985, age 33, assigned to liaise with the Stasi and recruit informants among foreign students and political contacts. His cover was a position at the Soviet-German House of Friendship. His East German personal identification photographs from this period show a slight man in a brown suit with close-set eyes who could be a regional procurement official, which was plausible. He jogged in the mornings. He played judo. He gained weight. He drove a Lada. When the protests that ended East Germany began in October 1989, his building—the KGB residency at 4 Angelikastraße—was near the route of the demonstrators. A crowd surrounded it. Putin walked outside and told them that Soviet troops inside would defend the building. There were no troops. He called Moscow for guidance. The duty officer said Moscow could not respond. He and his colleagues burned documents all night. He drove to Leningrad in January 1990, with what he later described as a sense that the country he was returning to no longer existed. He was an officer in his late thirties, slightly overweight, in a brown suit, carrying boxes of files that had not been burned in time. The East German photographs show nothing that suggests anything about the next thirty-five years.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Milton Bearden — CIA Islamabad Station
CIA Chief of Station, Islamabad, Pakistan · 1986–1989 · Operation Cyclone

By the time Bearden arrived in Islamabad in 1986, the CIA's covert Afghan program was already the largest in the agency's history. His job was to make it larger. CIA Director William Casey had given him $1 billion and an instruction: drive the Soviets out. Bearden oversaw the delivery of FIM-92 Stinger surface-to-air missiles to the mujahideen beginning in September 1986—the introduction that neutralized Soviet air superiority and changed the calculus of the war. He grew a beard for the assignment. His photographs from the period show a large, genial man in civilian clothes who could be an agricultural development consultant from Montana, which was roughly the cover. He managed approximately 250,000 fighters across nine provinces and helped end the Soviet-Afghan War. The beard was considerable.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Operation Earnest Will / Prime Chance — Persian Gulf
160th SOAR / SEAL Team Six · Mobile Sea Bases, Persian Gulf · 1987–1988

In 1987, Iran began mining international shipping lanes in the Persian Gulf. When the Reagan administration re-flagged Kuwaiti oil tankers under American colors, it also deployed Task Force 160 to modified oil-servicing barges anchored in international waters. The operators lived on the barges. They flew OH-6 and MH-6 Little Birds at night, at low altitude, with night-vision optics, looking for Iranian vessels laying mines. On September 21, 1987, a 160th SOAR crew caught the Iranian vessel Iran Ajr in the act of laying mines and disabled it with automatic fire; night-vision camera footage showed the crew frantically throwing mines overboard before Navy SEALs boarded and captured the ship's manifests and surviving crewmen. The footage aired on American television. The operation ran for fourteen months in an active conflict zone with no formal declaration of war. In their pre-deployment photographs at Fort Campbell, the 160th pilots and crew chiefs look like regional airline captains scheduled for a route change to somewhere warm. Their aircraft are in the background, modified beyond recognition. Neither the aircraft nor their crews look like what they were.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Operation Acid Gambit — Panama City
1st SFOD-D / 160th SOAR · Modelo Prison, Panama City · December 20, 1989

In May 1989, Kurt Muse—an American citizen operating a clandestine anti-Noriega radio station—was arrested and imprisoned in Modelo Prison in Panama City, across the street from the Comandancia, Noriega's military headquarters. Delta spent months constructing a full-scale mock-up of the prison at Fort Bragg and rehearsing an extraction. In the early morning hours of December 20, 1989—the first minutes of Operation Just Cause—23 Delta operators aboard four MH-6 Little Birds from the 160th SOAR landed on the roof of Modelo Prison, eliminated the sentries, and extracted Muse in minutes. The surrounding assault on the Comandancia was supported by AC-130 Spectre gunships. In pre-operation surveillance photos taken in Panama City's streets and markets, the reconnaissance element looks like Americans doing business in a tropical country. Which is what their covers said they were.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
JSOC "Scud Box" — Western Iraq
1st SFOD-D / 160th SOAR · H-2 and H-3 Airfields area, western Iraq · January–February 1991

When Iraqi SCUD missiles began striking Israel in January 1991, the central strategic concern of the Gulf War coalition became whether Israel would retaliate—and whether that retaliation would shatter the Arab governments that had aligned against Saddam Hussein. JSOC was given a sector of western Iraq—called the Scud Box—and tasked with finding and destroying the mobile SCUD launchers the Israeli government was threatening to respond to. Delta Force teams, inserted by 160th SOAR helicopters, operated from vehicle hides during the day and moved at night in Land Rovers and civilian trucks, covering terrain that had never been reconnoitered. They wore keffiyehs and clothing that would read, at distance, as local. The actual number of SCUD launchers destroyed by special operations forces during the campaign is disputed—assessments ranged from "several" to "none"—but the presence of Coalition SOF in the Scud Box changed the operational tempo of Iraqi launches. Israel did not enter the war. The teams operated in conditions of extreme cold, intermittent communications, and navigation uncertainty. In their pre-insertion photographs at their Saudi base, they look like contractors at a construction project in a Gulf country. Their vehicles are conventional. Their clothes are unremarkable. The equipment is stowed out of frame.

Delta Force, C Squadron — Snipers
1st SFOD-D · Fort Bragg, NC · c. 1990–91

Three men standing outdoors. One has a mustache. They are wearing the kind of clothes you would wear to go fishing, or to pick your kids up from school, or to a barbecue at a neighbor's house on a Saturday afternoon in Fayetteville, North Carolina. UNCLASSIFIED
American
CIA Special Activities Division / Ground Branch
Directorate of Operations · Central America, Afghanistan, Africa, the Balkans · c. 1982–1995

The officers of CIA Ground Branch—the paramilitary arm of the Directorate of Operations—maintained a dress code that could be summarized as: khakis, hiking boots, and whatever shirt implied they were with an agricultural NGO or a mining company or an infrastructure consultancy. Through the 1980s they were active in Nicaragua, El Salvador, Angola, Chad, and Afghanistan. They trained and equipped insurgent forces, conducted direct-action missions under Title 50 authority, and returned to Langley looking like they'd been at a mid-level project review meeting in Denver. They were photographed almost never. When they were, they looked like people who had been doing exactly what their cover said they were doing. That was the whole point.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Billy Waugh — CIA Paramilitary Operations Officer
CIA Special Activities Division · Khartoum, Sudan (and many others) · career 1961–2002+

Waugh served with U.S. Army Special Forces from 1961, was medically retired after Vietnam wounds in 1972, and spent the following three decades as a CIA contractor operating in places that did not require a uniform. In 1991, he was assigned to Khartoum, Sudan under light commercial cover, with one task among others: locate Ilich Ramírez Sánchez—Carlos the Jackal—who was living in Khartoum under Sudanese government protection. Waugh found Carlos' house, established his routine, and began jogging past it every morning at the same hour. Carlos was sometimes outside. Carlos sometimes waved. A CIA capture or kill operation was drafted and never approved during Waugh's tenure. The French DGSE, acting on CIA intelligence, eventually took Carlos from a Khartoum hospital in 1994. In photographs from across his career—Vietnam, Libya, Sudan, Afghanistan (where he was dropped in his early seventies after 9/11)—Waugh looks like a compact, fit man in late middle age with the bearing of a retired high school football coach. He is believed to be the most decorated living special operations soldier. He looks, in every photograph, like a very fit grandfather.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Task Force Ranger — Mogadishu
1st SFOD-D / 3rd Ranger Bn / 160th SOAR · Mogadishu, Somalia · August–October 1993

The pre-mission photographs of the Task Force Ranger operators—taken at their compound near the Mogadishu airport in the summer of 1993—show men who look like private contractors on a security detail in a hot country: tan, short-haired, wearing civilian clothes and carrying weapons in a practiced way that doesn't invite questions. Delta operators Gary Gordon and Randall Shughart, who requested permission to insert by rope to protect a downed Black Hawk pilot knowing they were outnumbered, were posthumously awarded the Medal of Honor. In their photographs from before the mission, they look like guys you'd see at a Home Depot.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Maj. Gen. William Garrison — After-Action
Commander, Joint Special Operations Command · Mogadishu, Somalia · October 1993

On October 7, 1993, four days after the battle that killed eighteen Americans and wounded seventy-three more, Garrison wrote a letter to the Secretary of Defense accepting personal responsibility for the operation's outcome. The letter is seventeen lines long. The second-to-last line reads: "The mission was not in vain." He was not formally reprimanded. He retired in 1996. In his photographs from the Somalia period—taken at the Task Force Ranger compound near the Mogadishu airport—he looks like a lean man in civilian clothes who has been asked to manage a difficult logistics project in a difficult location. He commanded the best-trained direct-action force in the world, deployed on a mission that was under-resourced, over-optimistic, and increasingly isolated from a unified chain of command. The operational after-action report identified seventeen specific failures across intelligence, planning, and execution. The letter accepted all of them. It was seventeen lines.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
SVR Directorate S — The Program Continues
SVR (successor to KGB First Chief Directorate) · Yasenevo, Moscow · 1991 onward

On December 25, 1991, Mikhail Gorbachev resigned and the Soviet flag was lowered from the Kremlin. The KGB's First Chief Directorate, which had run Soviet foreign intelligence for forty years, survived the dissolution intact—renamed the SVR, the Foreign Intelligence Service of the Russian Federation, and relocated to the same headquarters at Yasenevo. Directorate S, which managed the illegals, transferred with it. The officers embedded in the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, and Australia under false identities received no instruction to surface. Their cover identities were unchanged. Their missions were essentially unchanged. Some had children who had grown up as American or Canadian citizens. Some had mortgages and car payments and library cards. The Cold War had ended for their neighbors but had not ended for them in any operational sense that affected their daily lives. The FBI's 2010 sweep—which arrested ten Russian illegals in a single operation—revealed how unbroken the program had been: several of those arrested had been embedded since the mid-1980s, their cover lives spanning the entire post-Soviet period. In the family photographs from the 1990s and 2000s, the illegal families look exactly like their neighbors at every neighborhood function. The organization that deployed them no longer existed under its original name. The families at the barbecues remained exactly where they had been placed.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
KGB Alpha Group — The Vote, August 1991
KGB Directorate A · Moscow · August 19–21, 1991

On August 19, 1991, the Emergency Committee that had seized power from Mikhail Gorbachev tasked Alpha Group with arresting Boris Yeltsin, who was barricaded in the Russian Parliament building—the White House—with tens of thousands of civilians gathered outside. Alpha Group assessed the mission. Their internal estimate was 500 civilian casualties minimum to execute it. They could do it. They had stormed the Tajbeg Palace in forty-five minutes in 1979 and eliminated the head of a sovereign state. On the afternoon of August 20, Alpha Group's officers voted—by show of hands, inside their unit—not to carry out the order. They notified KGB leadership that the mission was not feasible without a direct written order from the President of the Soviet Union. Gorbachev was under house arrest in Crimea and was not writing orders. The coup collapsed the following day. The unit that had ended the career of one head of state in forty-five minutes had decided, by vote, that this particular mission was not one they were going to execute. The Soviet Union dissolved four months later. In photographs from this period, Alpha Group officers look like men in their thirties and forties at an athletic facility. They do not look like the people who made that decision.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Vympel — The Second Refusal, October 1993
Russian Federal Counterintelligence Service (former KGB) · Moscow · October 4, 1993

In October 1993, the constitutional crisis between Boris Yeltsin and the Russian Parliament produced a second standoff at the Moscow White House—the same building where, two years earlier, Alpha Group had voted not to arrest Yeltsin. This time Yeltsin was using force. After army tanks shelled the parliament building on October 4th and opened a breach, Vympel—which had survived the Soviet Union's dissolution as a Russian Federation security unit—was ordered to enter the building and secure it. Vympel's commanders refused, assessing the order as a political mission rather than a counterterrorism one. The building was secured by other forces. Two days later, Yeltsin signed an order transferring Vympel to the Ministry of Internal Affairs—effectively disbanding it from the intelligence apparatus it had been built to serve. Most of the unit's officers resigned rather than serve under MVD authority. The unit created for wartime deep-penetration operations against NATO targets, that had trained for a decade to be the most capable sabotage force in the Soviet arsenal, was disbanded because it declined a political assignment. Alpha Group had refused an order in 1991 and survived. Vympel refused an order in 1993 and did not. In their final unit photographs at Balashikha, they look like men at the end of something. The photographs do not explain what that something was.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Vasili Mitrokhin
KGB First Chief Directorate — Archivist · defected to UK, 1992

For twelve years, from 1972 to 1984, Mitrokhin was the KGB's chief archivist in the First Chief Directorate's foreign intelligence archive. He spent those years copying by hand the most sensitive operational records in Soviet intelligence history: agent networks, dead drops, clandestine meeting sites, technical operations, assassination plots, intelligence sources inside Western governments. He wrote them in tiny script on scraps of paper, hid them in his shoes, transferred them to typewritten pages at home, and buried them in milk canisters under his dacha. When the Soviet Union fell, he tried to give the archive to the CIA. They passed. He went to MI6. They sent a team to Estonia to dig up the milk canisters—six trunks of material—and flew him to England. His photographs show the world's most unremarkable man: cardigan, glasses, a face that has never caused anyone to look twice. He was carrying the most consequential intelligence archive of the Cold War under his vegetable garden.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Gennady Vasilenko
KGB officer · Moscow; Guyana; Washington, DC · imprisoned by KGB 1988, 2005; freed 2010

Vasilenko was a KGB officer who was genuinely, innocently friends with CIA counterintelligence officer Jack Platt. The KGB did not believe this was possible. They arrested him in Guyana in 1988, brought him back to Moscow, and interrogated him. They arrested him again in 2005, after Aldrich Ames had been in American custody for a decade and Robert Hanssen had been arrested in 2001—and the KGB had, by process of elimination, concluded that Vasilenko must have been the source. He wasn't. He spent years in Lefortovo Prison. Robert De Niro, who had become friends with Vasilenko through Platt, advocated for his release. Vasilenko was freed in the June 2010 spy swap—the same one that brought Anna Chapman home. In every photograph from his KGB years, he looks like a Soviet physical education teacher, which was his cover. He was genuinely friends with a CIA officer. That was his whole crime.

Further Reading

  • Delta Force — Charles Beckwith (1983)
    Beckwith's own account of founding the unit. Unvarnished.
  • The Master of Disguise — Antonio Mendez (1999)
    Mendez's account of thirty years in CIA's Office of Technical Service. The Argo operation is in here, alongside a career's worth of thinking about how people see and don't see each other.
  • The Quiet Professional — Alan Hoe (1993)
    Biography of Dick Meadows. The best book on what the quiet professional archetype actually looks like up close.
  • The Spy and the Traitor — Ben Macintyre (2018)
    Gordievsky biography. Probably the best single Cold War intelligence narrative written. The diaper-on-the-trunk detail is true.
  • The Main Enemy — Milton Bearden & James Risen (2003)
    CIA vs. KGB from the inside, through the final decade. Bearden's account of the Islamabad years is definitive.
  • Spy Handler — Victor Cherkashin (2005)
    The KGB handler's memoir. Remarkably candid about the Ames and Hanssen recruitments.
  • The Mitrokhin Archive — Christopher Andrew & Vasili Mitrokhin (1999)
    What Mitrokhin buried in his dacha. All of it.
  • Best of Enemies — Gus Russo & Eric Dezenhall (2018)
    The Platt-Vasilenko friendship. The last great spy story of the Cold War, as advertised.
  • My Silent War — Kim Philby (1968)
    Philby's memoir, written in Moscow. Curated for the KGB's preferred narrative and therefore doubly interesting to read for what it doesn't say.
  • 1983: The World at the Brink — Taylor Downing (2018)
    Able Archer 83 and the year the Cold War nearly became hot. The RYAN operation, the exercise, and Gordievsky's role in defusing it.
  • The Farewell Dossier — CIA Studies in Intelligence (1996, declassified)
    Gus Weiss's internal CIA account of the Vetrov operation and the coordinated 1983 expulsions. Available on the CIA FOIA reading room.
  • The Billion Dollar Spy — David Hoffman (2015)
    Definitive account of Polyakov/BOURBON. Uses newly declassified CIA files. The GRU general who asked for nothing.
  • Hunting the Jackal — Billy Waugh & Tim Keown (2004)
    Waugh's memoir. From Son Tay to Khartoum. The morning jogs past Carlos' house are in here.
  • Black Hawk Down — Mark Bowden (1999)
    Mogadishu. The standard.
  • Charlie Wilson's War — George Crile (2003)
    The definitive account of Operation Cyclone — and of Gust Avrakotos, the CIA officer who actually ran it. The bowling shirts are in here.
  • Man Without a Face — Markus Wolf & Anne McElvoy (1997)
    Wolf's own memoir. Revealing about method and motivation, appropriately guarded about sources. The Romeo operations are discussed in general terms.
  • No Room for Error — Col. John T. Carney Jr. & Benjamin F. Schemmer (2002)
    The Combat Control story: Desert One, Grenada, Panama, and beyond. Carney's account of his night alone in the Iranian desert is in Chapter 3.

Wolf (wolf.jpg): The 1978 Stockholm photograph circulated widely in Western press after Wolf's identity was established; reproduced in German press and BfV publications. Post-1990 press photographs of Wolf (at his trial, at book events) are clearly dated and credited in wire service archives. Wolf's East German Stasi personnel photographs are held at BStU (Bundesbeauftragter für die Unterlagen des Staatssicherheitsdienstes), now the BArch — some have been released to press.

Cohen / Kroger (kroger.jpg): Scotland Yard booking photographs from January 1961 arrest have been released in British press coverage and parliamentary materials. UK National Archives (Kew) holds MI5 files on the Portland Spy Ring (KV 2 series) — some photographs in these files were declassified. Original Soviet photographs of the Cohens may be in SVR historical holdings.

Carney (carney.jpg): USAF official portrait photographs are PD-USGov. DVIDS (dvidshub.net) search for "Combat Control" or "Carney." For Eagle Claw/Desert One imagery: NARA RG 330, also Air Force Historical Research Agency (AFHRA) at Maxwell AFB holds Eagle Claw photographic record.

North (north.jpg): North's 1987 congressional testimony was photographed extensively; all Congressional photographs from committee sessions are in the public domain as records of Congress. USMC official portraits (pre-dismissal) are PD-USGov. FBI/DOJ records from the Iran-Contra investigation released under FOIA include some imagery.

KGB/Soviet entries: Most Soviet intelligence officer photos from this period remain restricted or unverified. The CIA FOIA Reading Room (cia.gov/readingroom) has released some material on major cases. Putin/Dresden: East German Stasi personal identification photographs from the Dresden KGB residency have been reported in German press; originals held at BStU (Federal Commissioner for the Stasi Records). Vetrov: KGB personnel photograph published in French press coverage of the FAREWELL case. Alpha Group 1991: FSB official history releases contain some Alpha Group photographs from this period. Soviet military photographs taken before 1978 are generally public domain in the United States; later photographs are more complex.
