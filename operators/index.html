A Compendium · c. 1975–1995

Cold War Dads

In the years between the fall of Saigon and the fall of the Soviet Union, the world's most capable men cultivated the world's most ordinary appearances. KGB illegals drove Chevrolets and attended PTA meetings. Delta operators wore polo shirts and let their hair grow out. CIA case officers carried briefcases through airport terminals on three continents and looked, to a customs agent, like mid-level regional managers on their way to a sales conference.

They looked exactly like your dad. This was not an accident.

KGB Directorate S. 1st SFOD-D. Ground Branch. Alpha Group. Vympel. SEAL Team Six. GRU Spetsnaz. The quiet professionals and the forgettable faces.

UNCLASSIFIED
Soviet
Col. Rudolf Abel (William August Fisher)
KGB Directorate S · Brooklyn, New York · arrested June 1957 · exchanged February 1962

He was born in Newcastle upon Tyne to Russian émigré parents, joined Soviet intelligence in the 1920s, and spent the late 1940s and 1950s running Soviet intelligence networks from a studio apartment in Brooklyn under the name Emil Goldfus—artist and photographer. When the FBI arrested him in 1957, they found shortwave radios, cipher pads, a hollow shaving brush, and a hollow nickel that had once passed through a Brooklyn newsboy's hands containing a microdot. He named himself Rudolf Abel to his captors—a prearranged signal to Moscow that he had been taken. His FBI mugshot shows a mild-looking man in his late 50s who could be a retired schoolteacher. He was the most sophisticated KGB illegal ever caught in America, and the aesthetic template the entire program would follow for the next forty years.

Konon Molody — "Gordon Lonsdale" · The Portland Spy Ring
KGB Directorate S · London, UK · 1955–1961 · exchanged April 1964

Molody spent part of his childhood in California with an aunt, acquiring an American English accent he later adapted to pass as Canadian. The KGB built him a legend: Gordon Arnold Lonsdale, born 1924 in Cobalt, Ontario. From 1955 he operated in the UK as Lonsdale—a small-scale businessman who sold jukeboxes and bubblegum machines, attended dinner parties in London, had a car and a social calendar and a reputation for being good company. In parallel he was managing a network of agents inside Britain's Portland Naval Base stealing Royal Navy underwater warfare secrets, and a support cell—Morris and Lena Cohen, operating as "Peter and Helen Kroger" from a house in Ruislip—who relayed the material to Moscow by shortwave transmitter. Acquaintances described him as charming, sociable, and thoroughly pleasant: the kind of company people actively sought. MI5 arrested him in January 1961. He was exchanged at the Heerstraße checkpoint in Berlin in April 1964 for Greville Wynne, the businessman who had carried messages for Oleg Penkovsky. The photographs from his British years show a pleasant-looking man in his late thirties at social functions who appears entirely at ease in the room. This was the design. Abel had been forgettable. Lonsdale was likable. Both worked.

Soviet
Harold "Kim" Philby — Moscow Residency
KGB consultant (former MI6 / Cambridge Five) · Moscow · 1963–1988

Philby defected to Moscow in January 1963 and lived there for the next twenty-five years in a KGB-provided flat, advising on Western intelligence services and occasionally lecturing at KGB training programs. His photographs from the Moscow years show an elderly British gentleman in tweeds—a reasonable approximation of a retired Oxbridge don or a senior civil servant between appointments, except that he was in Moscow and had not aged particularly well. He was the most successful penetration agent in Western intelligence history: recruited by Soviet intelligence in the 1930s at Cambridge, he rose to become head of MI6's Soviet counterintelligence section—meaning that from 1944 to 1951, the man running British intelligence against the Soviets was a Soviet intelligence officer. He passed to the KGB the identities of every British agent inside the Soviet Union, the details of the Anglo-American intelligence sharing relationship, and the plans for Operation Valuable, the disastrous 1950 attempt to infiltrate anti-Soviet partisans into Albania. He died in Moscow on May 11, 1988. His KGB funeral was attended by senior officers. He had outlasted everyone. The tweeds were real.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
James Jesus Angleton — CI Staff
CIA Counterintelligence Staff · Langley, Virginia · 1954–1974

Angleton ran the CIA's counterintelligence staff for twenty years — from 1954 to the December morning in 1974 when William Colby called him in and told him his tenure was ending. He had spent the war years at OSS in London working alongside Kim Philby, who was then his closest professional contact. When Philby's defection was confirmed in 1963, Angleton concluded that Soviet intelligence had penetrated the CIA at a senior level and that the contaminated intelligence product had been shaping American foreign policy for a decade. The mole hunt that followed consumed him and the careers of the officers it touched — most of whom turned out to be innocent, including Yuri Nosenko, the KGB defector Angleton concluded was a provocation, who spent years in CIA custody on his orders. He ran counterintelligence as a closed compartment: few officers at headquarters understood what the CI staff was doing; most were not cleared to ask. He maintained particularly close ties with Israeli intelligence and oversaw domestic surveillance programs that became, after 1974, central exhibits in the Church Committee hearings. He grew orchids at his Virginia home and gave extended, elliptical interviews to journalists who found him fascinating and impenetrable. He had been close friends with T.S. Eliot at Harvard. He drank steadily and wore impeccable suits and thought in recursive, multi-layered constructs that colleagues described as the "wilderness of mirrors" — Eliot's phrase, which he applied to Soviet disinformation and which became the standard metaphor for the entire counterintelligence discipline. He was right that Soviet intelligence had penetrated the CIA at senior levels. He was wrong about where. The actual penetrations — Ames, Hanssen — were found only after his death in 1987. In photographs from across his twenty-year tenure he looks like an East Coast attorney who reads deeply and sleeps poorly and has considered views about what the documents actually mean when you read them in the right order. He did. His conclusion was wrong. The files he spent twenty years building are the reason we know what was in them.

Soviet
Morris & Lena Cohen — "Peter and Helen Kroger"
KGB Directorate S · Ruislip, London · 1954–1961 · exchanged July 1969

They were born in the Bronx—Morris in 1910, Lena in 1913—and became Soviet intelligence agents in the 1930s through Soviet-aligned labor activism. During the 1940s they supported the atomic espionage network connected to Julius Rosenberg, handling material in New York. After the Rosenbergs' arrest in 1950, the Cohens disappeared. By 1954 they had resurfaced in London as Peter and Helen Kroger: New Zealand-born antiquarian booksellers. They settled into a semi-detached house at 45 Cranley Drive in the suburb of Ruislip, joined local social clubs, and were known to neighbors as a quiet, pleasant couple who had traveled a good deal. Peter collected books and held occasional sales. Helen baked. The house contained a shortwave transmitter concealed in a cigarette lighter, a high-speed burst transmitter hidden behind a bookcase, microdot equipment in the kitchen, and approximately $6,000 in cash in a hole beneath the floor. When MI5 arrested them in January 1961—the same day as Lonsdale—Helen Kroger was being walked to a police car when an officer noticed she was clutching her handbag. Inside was a microdot package bound for Moscow. They were exchanged in 1969 for Gerald Brooke, a British lecturer imprisoned in the Soviet Union. In every photograph from their London years, they look like a retired New Zealand couple who have done moderately well in the book trade. This was an accurate description of everything MI5 could prove before they found the shortwave transmitter.

American
Christopher Boyce — "The Falcon"
TRW Inc. (CIA contractor) · Redondo Beach, California · 1974–1977 · arrested January 21, 1977

Boyce was 21 when he got a job at TRW Inc., a defense contractor whose satellite operations facility handled encrypted communications between CIA headquarters and the KH-11 Keyhole reconnaissance satellite program. His father was a former FBI agent with connections. The background check was light. He began photographing classified documents in 1974 and passed them to the Soviets through his childhood friend Andrew Daulton Lee—a drug dealer who traveled to Mexico City to meet with a KGB officer at the Soviet Embassy. Boyce spent the payments on falconry, hence the code name. He was arrested in January 1977 when Mexican police apprehended Lee outside the Soviet Embassy; Lee, facing drug charges, quickly gave up his source. Boyce was 23, had no formal intelligence training, maintained no operational cover, and inadvertently caused one of the most damaging satellite-intelligence compromises in CIA history—including the exposure of CIA monitoring of Australian domestic politics, which contributed to a constitutional crisis in Canberra. He escaped from federal prison in 1980, robbed banks for nineteen months as a fugitive, and was recaptured in 1981. His TRW badge photograph shows a young man who got a good job through his father's contacts. This is accurate. It is also the entire cover, which is the point: the most dangerous look is no look at all.

American
Operation AZORIAN — Hughes Glomar Explorer
CIA Directorate of Science & Technology · Pacific Ocean · 1974

In March 1968, the Soviet submarine K-129 sank in the Pacific northwest of Hawaii with 98 crew, three nuclear ballistic missiles, and cryptographic materials. The U.S. Navy found the wreck at 16,500 feet. The CIA determined it was worth raising. The problem was doing so secretly, in international waters, without Soviet knowledge, in a vessel that would not look like a covert intelligence operation. The solution was Howard Hughes. His corporate infrastructure provided the cover: Global Marine designed and Hughes funded the construction of the Glomar Explorer—a 619-foot deep-sea mining ship that was, in every visual and functional detail, a mining ship. CIA officers aboard were credentialed as Hughes employees. The cover story was ocean-floor manganese nodule mining. The Glomar Explorer sailed in 1974. The salvage successfully retrieved a portion of the K-129, including nuclear-armed torpedoes, cryptographic materials, and the remains of six Soviet crewmen, who were buried at sea with Soviet naval honors in a ceremony filmed by CIA. Soviet naval vessels shadowed the operation throughout but did not intervene. When journalist Seymour Hersh began investigating in 1975, the CIA asked major news organizations to delay publication; several complied. When the Los Angeles Times ran the story in March 1975, the cover collapsed. CIA officers responding to subsequent FOIA requests about the program developed what became standard agency practice: "We can neither confirm nor deny the existence of the information requested." The Glomar Response. The ship had looked exactly like a mining vessel because it was built as one, and had successfully concealed the most expensive covert operation in CIA history from Soviet intelligence for four years. The phrase outlasted the ship.

Soviet
George Blake — Operation GOLD
KGB (former MI6) · Berlin; London · 1955–1961 · escaped 1966 · Moscow 1966–2020

Blake was born in Rotterdam, evacuated from Dunkirk, worked with the Dutch resistance, and joined MI6 after the war. He was captured in Korea in 1950 and during 34 months of captivity became a committed Communist. The KGB recruited him in 1951. By 1955, when MI6 assigned him to the Berlin Station, he was simultaneously running British operations and passing them to Moscow. Operation GOLD—the Anglo-American tunnel dug from West Berlin to tap Soviet military communications cables, one of the most ambitious technical operations of the early Cold War—was betrayed to the KGB before the first shovel broke ground. The Soviets let it run eleven months to protect Blake, then arranged a theatrical discovery. He also passed approximately 400 Western agents to the KGB; many were executed. He was arrested in 1961 after a Polish defector identified him, and sentenced to 42 years—the longest sentence in British legal history. In October 1966 he escaped from Wormwood Scrubs with the help of a peace activist and a rope ladder woven from knitting needles. He lived in Moscow for the following 54 years, occasionally lecturing at the SVR Academy, writing careful memoirs, and giving thoughtful interviews to Western journalists. He died in December 2020 at age 98. In every photograph from his Moscow years he looks like a pleasant elderly British gentleman who might teach watercolors at a community center. He does not look like a man who spent a decade passing the identities of 400 people to an intelligence service that killed them.

Soviet
Sir Anthony Blunt — The Fourth Man
KGB / Cambridge Five (Surveyor of the King's / Queen's Pictures) · London · active 1936–1945; exposed publicly November 1979

Blunt was recruited by Soviet intelligence at Cambridge in the early 1930s as one of the five. He served as an MI5 officer during the Second World War, passing intelligence to Moscow, and returned to civilian life afterward as the Surveyor of the King's Pictures — later the Queen's Pictures — the keeper of the Royal Collection and one of the foremost authorities on Poussin and French Baroque painting in the English-speaking world. He was identified to MI5 privately in 1964 and given immunity from prosecution in exchange for full debriefing. In the fifteen years between his private identification and public exposure he continued his career at the highest levels of the British cultural establishment: lecturing, writing, advising on Royal Collection acquisitions, and appearing in the photographs of museum openings and academic conferences as exactly what his public record described: an elegant, slightly gaunt man in a well-cut suit who was one of the most respected art historians in Britain. Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher confirmed his identity to Parliament in November 1979. He was stripped of his knighthood. He died in March 1983. His photographs after the exposure — at a press conference in his Portman Square flat, composed and thin, in a suit — look exactly as his photographs had always looked. He said it was the intelligence work he regretted, not the convictions that produced it. Whether this was accurate is a judgment left to the reader. He is the end point of the Cambridge model: the operative who outlasted not just the war but the entire subsequent era of Cold War intelligence, continuing for decades in plain sight, and was exposed not by his tradecraft failing but by political decision. The photographs never changed. That was the design. That was also how long it worked.

American
Richard Welch — Athens Station Chief
CIA Chief of Station, Athens · killed December 23, 1975

On the night of December 23, 1975, Richard Welch returned to his home in Athens after a Christmas reception at the U.S. Ambassador's residence. A gunman shot him outside his front door in front of his wife and driver. He was the CIA's Athens station chief. His name had been published in Counterspy magazine—a publication running a campaign to identify and expose CIA officers worldwide—and subsequently republished in the Athens News three weeks before his murder. He was 46. He had served with the CIA since 1951. His cover was diplomatic: listed as a State Department officer, his Athens assignment had come in the turbulent years following the Greek junta's collapse. His death contributed directly to the Intelligence Identities Protection Act of 1982, which made it a federal crime to intentionally reveal the identity of a covert intelligence officer. He was buried at Arlington with full honors. In his CIA photographs he looks like what his cover said he was: a trim mid-career State Department officer attending diplomatic functions in a European capital. That cover identified him to a competent adversary and provided no protection in front of his own house. His is the case that every CIA cover officer knows: the appearance only works until it doesn't.

American
CIA Saigon Station — April 29, 1975
CIA Station Saigon / Thomas Polgar, Station Chief · U.S. Embassy, Saigon · April 29–30, 1975

On April 29, 1975, as North Vietnamese Army units entered Saigon, CIA Station Chief Thomas Polgar supervised the evacuation from the U.S. Embassy while directing the simultaneous destruction of the station's files, equipment, and everything that could identify Vietnamese assets to their captors. Officers worked burn bags through the night. Cryptographic equipment was smashed. Files accumulated across decades of the Vietnam War were shredded or fed to furnaces. Not all of them were destroyed in time. Not all the Vietnamese personnel the CIA had worked with for a decade were evacuated: some were promised evacuation and left behind, some were captured, some were eventually executed. Polgar left on one of the last helicopters. The CIA lost one of its largest stations in a single morning and spent the following twenty years rebuilding an institutional capacity that would look nothing like what Saigon had been. Delta Force was created in 1977 because Vietnam demonstrated what American special operations could not do. The CIA's clandestine service was rebuilt from the assumptions that the Saigon collapse exposed as wrong. The compendium you are looking at takes place in the shadow of that April morning. In the photographs taken as the helicopters lifted from the embassy roof, Polgar looks like an extremely tired man in late middle age managing a catastrophe that had been developing for years. He was. The men who built everything described in this collection knew what they were building away from.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
William Colby — The Family Jewels
Director of Central Intelligence · Langley, Virginia · 1973–1976

Colby became DCI in September 1973 — one month after Allende's overthrow in Chile, as congressional scrutiny of CIA political operations was reaching a level the agency had not faced before. Over the following three years he made a series of decisions that CIA veterans describe as either principled or catastrophic depending on who you ask: he turned over to the Church Committee the "Family Jewels" — the CIA's internal 693-page audit of potentially illegal activities since 1959, including assassination plots against foreign leaders, domestic mail-opening programs, and surveillance of American anti-war activists. He testified cooperatively before Congress on the theory that institutional survival required institutional transparency. He was dismissed by President Ford in November 1975 — the "Halloween Massacre" that also removed James Schlesinger from Defense and James Angleton from counterintelligence — and replaced by George H.W. Bush. The intelligence officers in the field who read their classified programs described in open congressional testimony developed what colleagues called "the paralysis": a reluctance to take actions that might one day produce a congressional exhibit. The Church Committee's revelations shaped the operational climate of the following decade. The men entering the CIA's clandestine service in the late 1970s, as Delta Force was being built and the covert Afghan program was being planned, were trained into an agency still negotiating what it was permitted to do after Colby's disclosures. He drowned in a canoeing accident on the Wicomico River in April 1996; his paddle and life jacket were found separately from his body. In his official CIA portraits from the director years he looks like a thoughtful man in wire-rimmed glasses who has read the file and is prepared to explain it patiently to people who have not. He was the most controversial director in the agency's history and the one who, by his own decision, disclosed the most. The compendium you are reading opens in the shadow of what he disclosed.

American
Philip Agee — Inside the Company
CIA Directorate of Operations (Resigned 1968) · Quito; Montevideo; London · published January 1975

Agee joined the CIA in 1957, served as a case officer in Ecuador, Uruguay, and Mexico through the early 1960s, and resigned in 1968 citing moral objections to the agency's support for Latin American authoritarian governments. He spent the following seven years writing Inside the Company: CIA Diary, published in London in January 1975, which identified approximately 250 CIA officers, agents, and front organizations operating in Latin America. He continued further: he collaborated with the CovertAction Information Bulletin, a publication whose subscriber list a later Senate investigation described as a tool of Cuban and Soviet intelligence, assisting in campaigns to identify CIA station chiefs in multiple countries. The station chief in Athens was named in a Greek-language newspaper three weeks before Richard Welch was shot outside his front door in December 1975. The Intelligence Identities Protection Act of 1982 was written because of Philip Agee. He is the inverse image of every subject in this compendium: not the operator whose unremarkable appearance was perfect cover, but the inside man who turned perfect institutional knowledge outward. The CIA officers whose assets were rolled up by services that read his work never had a photograph of him that mattered. His face in every publicity photograph from his post-CIA years — bearded, relaxed, at book events in Europe or press conferences in Havana — looks like a political science professor from a second-tier university with a cause and a paperback to sell. He died in Havana in January 2008. His U.S. passport had been revoked in 1979. He lived abroad for thirty years. The book outsold everyone else on this list combined.

American
Col. Arthur "Bull" Simons — EDS Tehran Extraction
U.S. Army Special Forces (Ret.) / H. Ross Perot · Tehran, Iran · January–February 1979

In January 1979, as the Iranian Revolution was dismantling the Shah's government, two executives of Electronic Data Systems—a Texas technology company owned by H. Ross Perot—were arrested by Iranian authorities on charges Perot believed were politically motivated. When the State Department indicated it could not ensure their safety in increasingly unstable Iranian prisons, Perot hired retired Army Colonel Arthur Simons to extract them. Simons had commanded the Son Tay raid in 1970—the technically perfect operation against a North Vietnamese prison that found the cells empty after the prisoners had been moved weeks earlier. He recruited a team from EDS employees and former Special Forces soldiers, flew into Tehran under commercial cover, and conducted reconnaissance while waiting for an opportunity. The opportunity arrived on February 11, 1979, when a revolutionary mob stormed Evin Prison and released thousands of inmates including the EDS executives in the general chaos. Simons' team drove them to the Turkish border in rented vehicles. The cover throughout—American businessmen helping their colleagues during a business emergency in a country experiencing a revolution—was accurate in most details. Simons wore a civilian overcoat and looked like what he was: a large, gray-haired Texan methodically solving a problem. He died of heart failure in May 1979, three months after the extraction. His career had run from World War II through Korea, Vietnam (Son Tay), and a private Iranian prison rescue, all in civilian clothes at the end.

American
Col. Charles "Chargin' Charlie" Beckwith
Founder & first commander, 1st SFOD-D · Fort Bragg, NC · 1977–1981

Before he was the father of Delta Force, Beckwith was a halfback at the University of Georgia under Wally Butts. In 1962, he became the first American officer to serve with the British SAS—a year embedded with 22 SAS in Malaya that changed the trajectory of American special operations. He spent the next fifteen years trying to convince the Pentagon to build something like it. He wrote memos. He briefed generals. He was passed over for promotion and given bad postings. He kept writing memos. In 1977, Fort Bragg's post commander finally called him in and told him to go build his unit. He looked like a football coach. He sounded like a football coach. The unit he built was, and remains, the most capable direct-action force in the world.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Gen. John K. Singlaub — The Private Network
USA (Ret.) / World Anti-Communist League · Washington, DC; various · 1977–1990

Singlaub was the Army Chief of Staff in South Korea when, in May 1977, he told a Washington Post reporter that Carter's planned withdrawal of U.S. forces from Korea would lead to war. Carter summoned him, relieved him of command, and eventually forced his retirement. He was 55. He responded by becoming the most effective private-network covert operator of the Cold War's final decade. He ran the World Anti-Communist League and coordinated private funding, weapons, and personnel for anti-Communist resistance movements on four continents — including the Nicaraguan Contras, after Oliver North needed weapons delivery that the Boland Amendment prohibited U.S. government funding for. Singlaub arranged them through WACL membership in Taiwan, South Korea, and Saudi Arabia. He carried no official portfolio. He wore suits and had the bearing of the Army general he had been and the manner of a man who has managed difficult situations before and expects to manage them again. He testified before Congress. He answered questions precisely. He said he believed he was acting in the national interest. He was. Whether the national interest should have been defined as he defined it is the question that congressional oversight was designed to answer and that the private network was designed to circumvent. He is the prototype of the retired senior officer as private foreign policy instrument: the man who carries the relationships, the operational knowledge, and the informal institutional clearances of forty years of service into the missions the government cannot officially conduct. He lived to 100 years old, dying in 2022. In every photograph from every decade he looks exactly like a retired Army general who has a plan. He always had a plan.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Operation IAFEATURE — Angola
CIA Directorate of Operations · Kinshasa, Zaire / Angola · 1975–1976

In 1975, as Portugal withdrew from Angola, three factions fought for control: the MPLA, the FNLA, and UNITA. Cuba deployed 50,000 troops in support of the MPLA. The CIA deployed officers and contracted paramilitary personnel under Operation IAFEATURE to train and supply FNLA and UNITA fighters. The CIA also recruited foreign mercenaries through intermediaries in Zaire. By December 1975, the operation had spent approximately $31 million — and Congress passed the Clark Amendment, cutting off all covert funding for Angola. Operationally, CIA teams in the field were given 30 days to get out. FNLA forces, which had been receiving CIA training, were routed by Cuban forces at Quifangondo in November 1975. Angola became a People's Republic. UNITA continued fighting with South African support and eventually with CIA support again (under Reagan, after the Clark Amendment was repealed in 1985). The CIA officers and paramilitary contractors who deployed to Angola in 1975 had arrived as the most capable organization in the world. They were withdrawn because the Senate voted to stop the operation. In their field photographs from Angola — civilian clothes, African terrain, equipment consistent with agricultural development projects — they look like they are at the beginning of something. They were at the end.

Lt. Viktor Belenko — The MiG-25 Defection
Soviet Air Defense Forces (PVO) · Hakodate Airport, Japan · September 6, 1976

On September 6, 1976, Soviet Air Defense Forces pilot Viktor Belenko broke from a training flight near Vladivostok, flew his MiG-25P at very low altitude to evade Soviet radar, crossed into Japanese airspace, and landed at Hakodate Airport. He was 29, had a wife and son, and had been planning the defection for months. The MiG-25 — which NATO had designated the "Foxbat" and been estimating as significantly superior to American fighters based on its reported performance — was disassembled, crated, and shipped to American intelligence facilities. What the analysis found was not what the performance estimates had suggested: the Foxbat's electronics were based on vacuum tubes rather than the solid-state systems assumed; its frame was primarily nickel steel rather than the titanium that performance projections had implied; its missiles were fixed and could not maneuver; its operational ceiling and sustained speed were more constrained than the assessments. American fighter design programs that had been built partly around the Foxbat threat were recalibrated. The Soviets demanded the aircraft back. The Japanese returned it thirty days later, in crates, with an invoice for disassembly and reassembly. Belenko was resettled in the United States and became an American citizen and defense consultant. In his defection-day photograph — still in his flight gear at Hakodate, surrounded by Japanese emergency responders and police, with the expression of a man working through the reality of what he has just done — he looks exactly like that: a Soviet Air Force pilot who has made an irreversible decision and is sitting with it. He had. The aircraft behind him became the most thoroughly examined Soviet military aircraft in history. The vacuum tubes inside it changed American defense procurement.

Soviet
Georgi Markov — Waterloo Bridge
KGB / Bulgarian DS (targeted dissident) · London · September 7, 1978

Georgi Markov was a Bulgarian dissident and writer who had defected to the West in 1969 and was broadcasting for the BBC and Radio Free Europe. The Bulgarian government, with KGB technical support, decided to kill him. On September 7, 1978, as Markov waited for a bus on Waterloo Bridge, a man carrying an umbrella brushed past him and jabbed the tip into his thigh. Markov felt a sting. The man walked on. Markov died four days later. The pathologist found a pellet — 1.7 mm in diameter, drilled and filled with ricin — embedded in his leg. The assassin had fired it from the modified umbrella's tip using a pneumatic mechanism concealed in the handle. The assassin was Francesco Giullino, a Danish citizen of Italian descent who worked for Bulgarian intelligence. He boarded a flight and left London that day. He has never been prosecuted. The pellet is displayed at Scotland Yard. The operation — conducted on a busy bridge in broad daylight, by a man who walked past, using a mechanism that looked like an umbrella because it was an umbrella — is the apotheosis of the principle this entire compendium documents: that the most dangerous things look like the most ordinary things. The extraordinary is concealed inside the absolutely unremarkable.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
GRU Spetsnaz — 8th Separate Special Forces Brigade
Main Intelligence Directorate (GRU) · various postings, Warsaw Pact and Afghanistan · 1970s–1980s

The GRU—Soviet military intelligence—maintained its own special operations capability entirely separate from the KGB. By the early 1980s, Spetsnaz GRU comprised more than 30,000 operators organized into brigades of 1,000-1,300 men, with independent companies assigned to every Soviet Army and Fleet. Their designated wartime mission was to operate behind NATO lines: destroying nuclear weapon storage sites, airfields, and command facilities; assassinating NATO commanders; disrupting communications. They trained in the same language schools as KGB illegals. In their administrative uniforms they were disguised as paratroopers; in the field they wore whatever the environment required. In Afghanistan, Spetsnaz GRU conducted reconnaissance and direct-action missions from 1979 onward—the first extended combat deployment of Soviet special operations forces. They looked, in group training photographs from this period, like ordinary Soviet Army men at physical training. The photographs do not suggest what the unit's actual purpose was.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Arkady Shevchenko — UN Under-Secretary-General
Soviet Ministry of Foreign Affairs (and CIA asset 1975–1978) · New York City · defected April 1978

Shevchenko was Under-Secretary-General of the United Nations and one of the closest advisors to Soviet Foreign Minister Andrei Gromyko—among the most senior Soviet officials operating abroad. In 1975, he approached the CIA. Over the next three years, he provided intelligence on Soviet foreign policy, the internal composition of the Politburo's foreign policy discussions, Soviet positions in arms control negotiations, and the workings of Soviet delegations at international bodies. He defected in April 1978, reportedly after suspicions at the Soviet mission. His photographs from his UN years show a heavyset Soviet diplomat in his late forties at receptions in the Delegates' Lounge—precisely the kind of man you would expect to see there. He provided the CIA with intelligence that influenced American negotiating positions throughout SALT II. He was the highest-ranking Soviet official ever to have worked as an American intelligence asset. He wrote Breaking with Moscow in 1985. His CIA debriefers described him as methodical and composed. He looked, in every photograph, like what he was: a senior Soviet official at a cocktail reception, pleased to be in New York, entirely at home in the room.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Gen. Yuri Drozdov — KGB First Chief Directorate, Directorate S
Chief of Illegals Directorate · Yasenevo, Moscow · 1979–1991

When Drozdov took command of Directorate S in 1979, he inherited the most carefully managed secret in Soviet intelligence: a worldwide network of officers embedded in Western countries under false identities, sometimes for decades. He expanded it. Under his leadership, Directorate S ran illegals in the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, West Germany, and Australia. The selection criteria were unusual for a military organization: ability to acquire foreign languages without an accent, capacity to internalize a foreign culture so completely that the officer could pass as a local in a random conversation with a stranger, and above all a neutral, unmemorable physical appearance. Drozdov described the ideal illegal as "a man who could sit on a park bench for six hours without anyone noticing him." He wore a cardigan.

Soviet
KGB Alpha Group (Directorate A) — Kabul, December 1979
KGB First Chief Directorate · formed July 1974 · operational by 1979

Alpha Group was formed in 1974 in response to the Munich massacre, when the Soviet leadership looked at a Palestinian terrorist operation and decided they needed something equivalent. Their group photos from the late 1970s show men in nondescript athletic clothes who could be a provincial volleyball team. On the night of December 27, 1979, Alpha Group operators stormed the Tajbeg Palace in Kabul, killed the Afghan president Hafizullah Amin and his guards in forty-five minutes, and initiated the Soviet-Afghan War. One KGB officer died in the assault, along with 200 of Amin's guards. The Soviet leadership had decided he was unreliable. The corrective measure took forty-five minutes. The Alpha operators involved received state awards at a quiet ceremony with no press.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Special Forces Detachment-A — West Berlin
10th Special Forces Group (Airborne) · West Berlin, West Germany · 1956–1984

CIA Directorate of Operations · Beirut; Khartoum; Dushanbe; Salahuddin, Kurdistan · 1976–1997

Baer joined the CIA in 1976 and spent the following twenty years as a case officer in Beirut, Khartoum, Djibouti, Tajikistan, and finally Northern Iraq, where he ran the agency's most ambitious post-Cold War covert operation: beginning in 1994, working from a CIA liaison office in Salahuddin in the Kurdish-controlled north, he assembled a coalition of Kurdish factions — the INC, the KDP, the PUK — along with Iraqi Army defectors and tribal leaders, into an organized coup attempt against Saddam Hussein. The operation involved thousands of committed Iraqi participants, had advanced to a state of readiness, and was aborted by the Clinton White House in March 1995, which had concluded the attempt would fail and was unwilling to accept attribution. The Iraqis who had been promised American support were left in place. Many were subsequently killed by Saddam's security services. Baer was recalled and subjected to an FBI investigation for allegedly soliciting the assassination of a foreign head of state — a charge stemming from a cable he had sent to headquarters. The investigation was closed without charges. He resigned from the CIA in 1997 and wrote See No Evil (2002), which became one of the defining field-officer memoirs of the era and the basis for the film Syriana. In his CIA photographs from across his postings — Beirut in the years after the embassy bombing, Kurdistan in the years before the Iraq War — he looks like an extremely fit man in his late thirties in civilian clothes who has arrived recently and is trying to understand the situation. He was. The situation was always more complicated than the briefings had suggested, and the cable he needed to send was always one that Langley did not want to receive.

Billy Waugh — CIA Paramilitary Operations Officer
CIA Special Activities Division · Khartoum, Sudan (and many others) · career 1961–2002+

Waugh served with U.S. Army Special Forces from 1961, was medically retired after Vietnam wounds in 1972, and spent the following three decades as a CIA contractor operating in places that did not require a uniform. In 1991, he was assigned to Khartoum, Sudan under light commercial cover, with one task among others: locate Ilich Ramírez Sánchez—Carlos the Jackal—who was living in Khartoum under Sudanese government protection. Waugh found Carlos' house, established his routine, and began jogging past it every morning at the same hour. Carlos was sometimes outside. Carlos sometimes waved. A CIA capture or kill operation was drafted and never approved during Waugh's tenure. The French DGSE, acting on CIA intelligence, eventually took Carlos from a Khartoum hospital in 1994. In photographs from across his career—Vietnam, Libya, Sudan, Afghanistan (where he was dropped in his early seventies after 9/11)—Waugh looks like a compact, fit man in late middle age with the bearing of a retired high school football coach. He is believed to be the most decorated living special operations soldier. He looks, in every photograph, like a very fit grandfather.

American
Search Bloc — The Hunt for Pablo Escobar
Delta Force (advisors) / NSA / DEA · Medellín, Colombia · 1992–December 2, 1993

By 1992, Pablo Escobar had escaped from La Catedral — the prison he had effectively designed himself — and was a fugitive in the city where his cartel had been killing police officers, judges, politicians, and journalists for a decade. The Colombian National Police's Search Bloc was hunting him. The United States contributed SIGINT collection from NSA, DEA technical assistance, and, according to several accounts confirmed by former operators, a small element of Delta Force advisors who assisted with signals exploitation and targeting methodology. The operators were officially DEA-assigned; their actual function was to help Colombian police exploit Escobar's communications. The methodology — tracking a cellular phone to a specific address through signal triangulation — was the same methodology that JSOC operators would carry from Colombia to Iraq and Afghanistan over the following decade. Escobar was located at a middle-class house in the Los Olivos neighborhood of Medellín by tracking a radio call to his son. He was killed December 2, 1993, the day after his birthday. In photographs from their Colombia rotations, the American advisors in the Search Bloc look like athletic men in their thirties in civilian clothes at a police facility in a tropical country — a description that matches their DEA cover identities exactly. The radio-direction-finding methodology they refined in Medellín became the template for time-sensitive targeting that defined every subsequent JSOC campaign.

Task Force Ranger — Mogadishu
1st SFOD-D / 3rd Ranger Bn / 160th SOAR · Mogadishu, Somalia · August–October 1993

The pre-mission photographs of the Task Force Ranger operators—taken at their compound near the Mogadishu airport in the summer of 1993—show men who look like private contractors on a security detail in a hot country: tan, short-haired, wearing civilian clothes and carrying weapons in a practiced way that doesn't invite questions. Operation Gothic Serpent was a mission to capture the senior lieutenants of Somali warlord Mohamed Farrah Aidid. On October 3rd, 1993, an assault on the Olympic Hotel became an eighteen-hour battle. Eighteen Americans died. Delta operators Gary Gordon and Randall Shughart, who requested permission to insert by rope to protect a downed Black Hawk pilot knowing they were outnumbered, were posthumously awarded the Medal of Honor. In their photographs from before the mission, they look like guys you'd see at a Home Depot.

American
Harold James Nicholson
CIA Case Officer / Station Chief (and SVR asset) · Malaysia; Thailand; Bucharest; "The Farm" · arrested November 1996

Nicholson joined the CIA in 1980, served as a case officer in Malaysia, Thailand, Japan, and the Philippines, and was posted as Chief of Station in Bucharest in 1990. He received consistently superior performance evaluations — precisely the kind of competent, presentable professional whose social fluency made him effective at recruiting foreign nationals in difficult postings. In 1994, he began selling intelligence to the Russian SVR while serving at CIA headquarters, and continued while posted as an instructor at the CIA's training facility at Camp Peary, where he was teaching new case officers the craft he was simultaneously selling. The material he provided identified CIA assets and operations across multiple field postings. He was arrested at Dulles International Airport in November 1996 while attempting to pass documents to an SVR contact; the FBI had been watching him for months. Total payments received: approximately $300,000. He pleaded guilty in 1997 and was sentenced to 23 years — the largest espionage sentence imposed on a CIA officer since Ames. In 2009, while still in prison, he recruited his son Bryan to collect additional SVR payments during a trip to Lima; both were charged and convicted. He was released in 2024. His CIA personnel photographs from his field officer years in Southeast Asia and Romania show a trim man in his late thirties in a jacket and tie who looks indistinguishable from the excellent officer his performance reviews describe. Those reviews were accurate. The standard vetting and evaluation process rated him extremely well on every criterion it measured. The question is which criteria were the right ones to measure.

American
Maj. Gen. William Garrison — After-Action
Commander, Joint Special Operations Command · Mogadishu, Somalia · October 1993

On October 7, 1993, four days after the battle that killed eighteen Americans and wounded seventy-three more, Garrison wrote a letter to the Secretary of Defense accepting personal responsibility for the operation's outcome. The letter is seventeen lines long. The second-to-last line reads: "The mission was not in vain." He was not formally reprimanded. He retired in 1996. In his photographs from the Somalia period—taken at the Task Force Ranger compound near the Mogadishu airport—he looks like a lean man in civilian clothes who has been asked to manage a difficult logistics project in a difficult location. He commanded the best-trained direct-action force in the world, deployed on a mission that was under-resourced, over-optimistic, and increasingly isolated from a unified chain of command. The operational after-action report identified seventeen specific failures across intelligence, planning, and execution. The letter accepted all of them. It was seventeen lines.

American
Fred Woodruff — CIA Tbilisi
CIA Senior Officer · Tbilisi, Republic of Georgia · August 8, 1993

Woodruff was the CIA's senior officer in Tbilisi, officially serving as an advisor to the Georgian President Eduard Shevardnadze's protective service as the former Soviet republic navigated an independence that had come faster than its institutions could absorb. On August 8, 1993, he was shot in the back of the head while riding in a vehicle driven by a senior Georgian intelligence official on a road outside Tbilisi. He died immediately. He was 45. A young Georgian soldier was subsequently convicted of a shooting; he later recanted his confession and claimed it had been coerced. The circumstances have never been fully explained. The Georgian intelligence official driving the car was under pressure from Russian intelligence services; Russian intelligence had strong operational motives to remove American advisors from Shevardnadze's inner circle; the shooting occurred in conditions inconsistent with a casual encounter. Woodruff was the first CIA officer killed in the territory of the former Soviet Union after the Cold War's formal end. His official cover as a government advisor made his presence in Tbilisi semi-public; his actual function was to help Shevardnadze build a Georgian intelligence service that would not simply function as an FSB extension. In his photographs from his Tbilisi period he looks like an American official in a warm country on a difficult assignment: fit, slightly tan, the kind of quietly purposeful middle-aged professional appearance that government advisory work produces in the field. He was 45 years old. The case was never closed. The soldier who confessed spent years in a Georgian prison and maintained his innocence. The road where it happened is not marked.

Soviet
SVR Directorate S — The Program Continues
SVR (successor to KGB First Chief Directorate) · Yasenevo, Moscow · 1991 onward

On December 25, 1991, Mikhail Gorbachev resigned and the Soviet flag was lowered from the Kremlin. The KGB's First Chief Directorate, which had run Soviet foreign intelligence for forty years, survived the dissolution intact—renamed the SVR, the Foreign Intelligence Service of the Russian Federation, and relocated to the same headquarters at Yasenevo. Directorate S, which managed the illegals, transferred with it. The officers embedded in the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, and Australia under false identities received no instruction to surface. Their cover identities were unchanged. Their missions were essentially unchanged. Some had children who had grown up as American or Canadian citizens. Some had mortgages and car payments and library cards. The Cold War had ended for their neighbors but had not ended for them in any operational sense that affected their daily lives. The FBI's 2010 sweep—which arrested ten Russian illegals in a single operation—revealed how unbroken the program had been: several of those arrested had been embedded since the mid-1980s, their cover lives spanning the entire post-Soviet period. In the family photographs from the 1990s and 2000s, the illegal families look exactly like their neighbors at every neighborhood function. The organization that deployed them no longer existed under its original name. The families at the barbecues remained exactly where they had been placed.

Evgenii Primakov — SVR Director
Director, External Intelligence Service (SVR) · Yasenevo, Moscow · December 1991–January 1996

Primakov was appointed director of the External Intelligence Service on December 26, 1991 — the day after Gorbachev resigned and the Soviet flag was lowered. He had spent decades as an Arabist, academic, and trusted Soviet foreign policy operative: the kind of figure who had moved through the intersection of intelligence and foreign policy always in the clothes of an academic or diplomat, always with the deniability of an institutional role that was not quite intelligence. His task was to transform the KGB's First Chief Directorate — which had just lost its country, its budget, and its ideological reason for existing — into a viable foreign intelligence service for a government that did not yet know what it wanted. He kept the illegals program. He kept Yasenevo. He kept the analytical pipelines and the rezidentura network and the full institutional staff. He added diplomatic sophistication: he was the first SVR director with genuine foreign policy experience, and he ran the service less as a secret police operation than as a foreign policy instrument — more like the BND or the DGSE than like the KGB it had been. He served until 1996, when Yeltsin appointed him Foreign Minister — a trajectory that had not been available to KGB chairmen in the Soviet system. He later became Prime Minister. In his directorial photographs from the early 1990s he looks like a distinguished Russian academic and diplomat of the Brezhnev-era foreign policy establishment, slightly rumpled in the Eastern European academic tradition, with the expression of a man who has thought carefully about the situation. The institution he rebuilt survived him by several decades. So did its methods.

Soviet
KGB Alpha Group — The Vote, August 1991
KGB Directorate A · Moscow · August 19–21, 1991

On August 19, 1991, the Emergency Committee that had seized power from Mikhail Gorbachev tasked Alpha Group with arresting Boris Yeltsin, who was barricaded in the Russian Parliament building—the White House—with tens of thousands of civilians gathered outside. Alpha Group assessed the mission. Their internal estimate was 500 civilian casualties minimum to execute it. They could do it. They had stormed the Tajbeg Palace in forty-five minutes in 1979 and eliminated the head of a sovereign state. On the afternoon of August 20, Alpha Group's officers voted—by show of hands, inside their unit—not to carry out the order. They notified KGB leadership that the mission was not feasible without a direct written order from the President of the Soviet Union. Gorbachev was under house arrest in Crimea and was not writing orders. The coup collapsed the following day. The unit that had ended the career of one head of state in forty-five minutes had decided, by vote, that this particular mission was not one they were going to execute. The Soviet Union dissolved four months later. In photographs from this period, Alpha Group officers look like men in their thirties and forties at an athletic facility. They do not look like the people who made that decision.

Soviet
Vympel — The Second Refusal, October 1993
Russian Federal Counterintelligence Service (former KGB) · Moscow · October 4, 1993

In October 1993, the constitutional crisis between Boris Yeltsin and the Russian Parliament produced a second standoff at the Moscow White House—the same building where, two years earlier, Alpha Group had voted not to arrest Yeltsin. This time Yeltsin was using force. After army tanks shelled the parliament building on October 4th and opened a breach, Vympel—which had survived the Soviet Union's dissolution as a Russian Federation security unit—was ordered to enter the building and secure it. Vympel's commanders refused, assessing the order as a political mission rather than a counterterrorism one. The building was secured by other forces. Two days later, Yeltsin signed an order transferring Vympel to the Ministry of Internal Affairs—effectively disbanding it from the intelligence apparatus it had been built to serve. Most of the unit's officers resigned rather than serve under MVD authority. The unit that had been created for wartime deep-penetration operations against NATO targets, and had trained for a decade to be the most capable sabotage force in the Soviet arsenal, was disbanded because it declined a political assignment. Alpha Group had refused an order in 1991 and survived. Vympel refused an order in 1993 and did not. In their final unit photographs at Balashikha, they look like men at the end of something. The photographs do not explain what that something was.

Soviet
Gen. Alexander Lebed — 14th Army, Transnistria
Russian Army · Tiraspol, Transnistria (Moldova) · 1992–1995

Lebed commanded the 14th Guards Army stationed in Transnistria — the strip of predominantly Russian-speaking eastern Moldova that had declared independence after the Soviet dissolution, backed by Russian officers, Russian weapons, and the presence of 9,000 Russian soldiers under Lebed's command. When active fighting between Transnistrian forces and the Moldovan government erupted in 1992, Lebed was the decisive military factor in the conflict. He deployed his forces in July 1992 and told both sides, with characteristic directness, that if the shooting did not stop he would make it stop. It stopped. He was blunt in a way that post-Soviet Russian military figures had rarely been in public: he held press conferences, gave candid interviews to Western journalists, and said openly that Russian security interests required Russian forces in place. He was not performing. He was the template for the post-Soviet Russian military commander as political actor: technically competent, contemptuous of civilian politicians, genuinely popular with the men who served under him, potentially very dangerous to anyone who mistook his directness for simplicity. He ran for the Russian presidency in 1996, came in third, sold his endorsement to Yeltsin in exchange for a national security appointment, used that appointment to negotiate the Khasavyurt Accords ending the First Chechen War, and was dismissed by Yeltsin five months later for being too prominent and too independent. He died when his helicopter struck power lines in Siberia in April 2002. In his photographs from the Transnistrian period he looks like a large, gray, close-cropped Russian military officer at a checkpoint who is not satisfied with how the day is going. He was not. He never was, anywhere. This is accurate.

Soviet
Vasili Mitrokhin
KGB First Chief Directorate — Archivist · defected to UK, 1992

For twelve years, from 1972 to 1984, Mitrokhin was the KGB's chief archivist in the First Chief Directorate's foreign intelligence archive. He spent those years copying by hand the most sensitive operational records in Soviet intelligence history: agent networks, dead drops, clandestine meeting sites, technical operations, assassination plots, intelligence sources inside Western governments. He wrote them in tiny script on scraps of paper, hid them in his shoes, transferred them to typewritten pages at home, and buried them in milk canisters under his dacha. When the Soviet Union fell, he tried to give the archive to the CIA. They passed. He went to MI6. They sent a team to Estonia to dig up the milk canisters—six trunks of material—and flew him to England. His photographs show the world's most unremarkable man: cardigan, glasses, a face that has never caused anyone to look twice. He was carrying the most consequential intelligence archive of the Cold War under his vegetable garden.

Soviet
Gennady Vasilenko
KGB officer · Moscow; Guyana; Washington, DC · imprisoned by KGB 1988, 2005; freed 2010

Vasilenko was a KGB officer who was genuinely, innocently friends with CIA counterintelligence officer Jack Platt. The KGB did not believe this was possible. They arrested him in Guyana in 1988, brought him back to Moscow, and interrogated him. They arrested him again in 2005, after Aldrich Ames had been in American custody for a decade and Robert Hanssen had been arrested in 2001—and the KGB had, by process of elimination, concluded that Vasilenko must have been the source. He wasn't. He spent years in Lefortovo Prison. Robert De Niro, who had become friends with Vasilenko through Platt, advocated for his release. Vasilenko was freed in the June 2010 spy swap—the same one that brought Anna Chapman home. In every photograph from his KGB years, he looks like a Soviet physical education teacher, which was his cover. He was genuinely friends with a CIA officer. That was his whole crime.

Further Reading

  • Delta Force — Charles Beckwith (1983)
    Beckwith's own account of founding the unit. Unvarnished.
  • The Master of Disguise — Antonio Mendez (1999)
    Mendez's account of thirty years in CIA's Office of Technical Service. The Argo operation is in here, alongside a career's worth of thinking about how people see and don't see each other.
  • The Quiet Professional — Alan Hoe (1993)
    Biography of Dick Meadows. The best book on what the quiet professional archetype actually looks like up close.
  • The Spy and the Traitor — Ben Macintyre (2018)
    Gordievsky biography. Probably the best single Cold War intelligence narrative written. The diaper-on-the-trunk detail is true.
  • The Main Enemy — Milton Bearden & James Risen (2003)
    CIA vs. KGB from the inside, through the final decade. Bearden's account of the Islamabad years is definitive.
  • Spy Handler — Victor Cherkashin (2005)
    The KGB handler's memoir. Remarkably candid about the Ames and Hanssen recruitments.
  • The Mitrokhin Archive — Christopher Andrew & Vasili Mitrokhin (1999)
    What Mitrokhin buried in his dacha. All of it.
  • Best of Enemies — Gus Russo & Eric Dezenhall (2018)
    The Platt-Vasilenko friendship. The last great spy story of the Cold War, as advertised.
  • My Silent War — Kim Philby (1968)
    Philby's memoir, written in Moscow. Curated for the KGB's preferred narrative and therefore doubly interesting to read for what it doesn't say.
  • 1983: The World at the Brink — Taylor Downing (2018)
    Able Archer 83 and the year the Cold War nearly became hot. The RYAN operation, the exercise, and Gordievsky's role in defusing it.
  • The Farewell Dossier — CIA Studies in Intelligence (1996, declassified)
    Gus Weiss's internal CIA account of the Vetrov operation and the coordinated 1983 expulsions. Available on the CIA FOIA reading room.
  • The Billion Dollar Spy — David Hoffman (2015)
    Definitive account of Polyakov/BOURBON. Uses newly declassified CIA files. The GRU general who asked for nothing.
  • Hunting the Jackal — Billy Waugh & Tim Keown (2004)
    Waugh's memoir. From Son Tay to Khartoum. The morning jogs past Carlos' house are in here.
  • Black Hawk Down — Mark Bowden (1999)
    Mogadishu. The standard.
  • Charlie Wilson's War — George Crile (2003)
    The definitive account of Operation Cyclone — and of Gust Avrakotos, the CIA officer who actually ran it. The bowling shirts are in here.
  • Man Without a Face — Markus Wolf & Anne McElvoy (1997)
    Wolf's own memoir. Revealing about method and motivation, appropriately guarded about sources. The Romeo operations are discussed in general terms.
  • No Room for Error — Col. John T. Carney Jr. & Benjamin F. Schemmer (2002)
    The Combat Control story: Desert One, Grenada, Panama, and beyond. Carney's account of his night alone in the Iranian desert is in Chapter 3.
