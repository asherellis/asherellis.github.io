A Compendium · c. 1975–1995

Cold War Dads

In the years between the fall of Saigon and the fall of the Soviet Union, the world's most capable men cultivated the world's most ordinary appearances. KGB illegals drove Chevrolets and attended PTA meetings. Delta operators wore polo shirts and let their hair grow out. CIA case officers carried briefcases through airport terminals on three continents and looked, to a customs agent, like mid-level regional managers on their way to a sales conference.

They looked exactly like your dad. This was not an accident.

KGB Directorate S. 1st SFOD-D. Ground Branch. Alpha Group. Vympel. SEAL Team Six. GRU Spetsnaz. The quiet professionals and the forgettable faces.

UNCLASSIFIED
Soviet
Col. Rudolf Abel (William August Fisher)
KGB Directorate S · Brooklyn, New York · arrested June 1957 · exchanged February 1962

He was born in Newcastle upon Tyne to Russian émigré parents, joined Soviet intelligence in the 1920s, and spent the late 1940s and 1950s running Soviet intelligence networks from a studio apartment in Brooklyn under the name Emil Goldfus—artist and photographer. When the FBI arrested him in 1957, they found shortwave radios, cipher pads, a hollow shaving brush, and a hollow nickel that had once passed through a Brooklyn newsboy's hands containing a microdot. He named himself Rudolf Abel to his captors—a prearranged signal to Moscow that he had been taken. His FBI mugshot shows a mild-looking man in his late 50s who could be a retired schoolteacher. He was the most sophisticated KGB illegal ever caught in America, and the aesthetic template the entire program would follow for the next forty years.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Francis Gary Powers — Operation GRAND SLAM
CIA / Lockheed U-2 · Sverdlovsk, USSR · May 1, 1960

Powers was an Air Force pilot recruited by the CIA to fly U-2 reconnaissance aircraft over the Soviet Union at 70,000 feet — above the service ceiling of Soviet interceptors and surface-to-air missiles, or so the program's architects believed. On May 1, 1960, his U-2 was struck by an SA-2 Guideline missile over Sverdlovsk and he ejected. He was captured alive. The aircraft's wreckage — including the KH-type camera system and its film — was substantially recovered by Soviet forces. President Eisenhower initially denied the aircraft was on a spy mission; Khrushchev then revealed that they had the pilot and the wreckage. Eisenhower's cover story collapsed in real time. The Paris summit that had been scheduled between Eisenhower and Khrushchev collapsed with it. Powers was tried publicly in Moscow on espionage charges and sentenced to ten years. He served twenty-one months. On February 10, 1962, he was walked across the Glienicke Bridge in Berlin and exchanged for Rudolf Abel — the entry that opens this compendium. It was the archetypal Cold War exchange: one side gives back a man in an overcoat on a gray morning; the other side gives back a man in an overcoat on the same gray morning; the bridge connects the two halves of the divided city; both men walk toward their handlers without looking back. When Powers returned to the United States, CIA and congressional critics questioned why he had not activated the aircraft's self-destruct mechanism and had not taken his suicide needle. The criticism was largely unfair given the speed of the ejection sequence. He worked for Lockheed afterward and then as a traffic helicopter reporter in Los Angeles. He died on August 1, 1977, when his helicopter ran out of fuel near Los Angeles and he held the aircraft away from a group of children playing on a baseball field below. He was 47. The Glienicke Bridge exchange image — two overcoated men at opposite ends of a span on a winter morning — became the defining visual grammar of the Cold War spy trade. Abel had been on one end. Powers had been on the other. Neither had looked like what they were.

Soviet
Konon Molody — "Gordon Lonsdale" · The Portland Spy Ring
KGB Directorate S · London, UK · 1955–1961 · exchanged April 1964

Molody spent part of his childhood in California with an aunt, acquiring an American English accent he later adapted to pass as Canadian. The KGB built him a legend: Gordon Arnold Lonsdale, born 1924 in Cobalt, Ontario. From 1955 he operated in the UK as Lonsdale—a small-scale businessman who sold jukeboxes and bubblegum machines, attended dinner parties in London, had a car and a social calendar and a reputation for being good company. In parallel he was managing a network of agents inside Britain's Portland Naval Base stealing Royal Navy underwater warfare secrets, and a support cell—Morris and Lena Cohen, operating as "Peter and Helen Kroger" from a house in Ruislip—who relayed the material to Moscow by shortwave transmitter. Acquaintances described him as charming, sociable, and thoroughly pleasant: the kind of company people actively sought. MI5 arrested him in January 1961. He was exchanged at the Heerstraße checkpoint in Berlin in April 1964 for Greville Wynne, the businessman who had carried messages for Oleg Penkovsky. The photographs from his British years show a pleasant-looking man in his late thirties at social functions who appears entirely at ease in the room. This was the design. Abel had been forgettable. Lonsdale was likable. Both worked.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Harold "Kim" Philby — Moscow Residency
KGB consultant (former MI6 / Cambridge Five) · Moscow · 1963–1988

Philby defected to Moscow in January 1963 and lived there for the next twenty-five years in a KGB-provided flat, advising on Western intelligence services and occasionally lecturing at KGB training programs. His photographs from the Moscow years show an elderly British gentleman in tweeds—a reasonable approximation of a retired Oxbridge don or a senior civil servant between appointments, except that he was in Moscow and had not aged particularly well. He was the most successful penetration agent in Western intelligence history: recruited by Soviet intelligence in the 1930s at Cambridge, he rose to become head of MI6's Soviet counterintelligence section—meaning that from 1944 to 1951, the man running British intelligence against the Soviets was a Soviet intelligence officer. He passed to the KGB the identities of every British agent inside the Soviet Union, the details of the Anglo-American intelligence sharing relationship, and the plans for Operation Valuable, the disastrous 1950 attempt to infiltrate anti-Soviet partisans into Albania. He died in Moscow on May 11, 1988. His KGB funeral was attended by senior officers. He had outlasted everyone. The tweeds were real.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Guy Burgess & Donald Maclean — The Flight to Moscow
KGB / Cambridge Five (Foreign Office / British Embassy Washington) · London; Washington · 1934–1951 · fled May 25, 1951

They were both recruited at Cambridge in the early 1930s and both spent the following two decades in the upper tiers of the British foreign policy establishment while passing its secrets to Moscow. Maclean was serious, technically superb, and eventually head of the Foreign Office's American Department — with access to the full scope of Anglo-American postwar intelligence-sharing and the early architecture of Western nuclear policy. Burgess was brilliant, visibly dissolute, and spectacularly bad at maintaining any kind of cover; he was drunk at diplomatic functions in Washington and was eventually asked to leave the British Embassy for behavior his supervisors could no longer explain away. Both were Soviet agents since Cambridge. In May 1951, as MI5's investigation of "Homer" (Maclean's cover name in KGB cables) was closing in, Kim Philby — then posted in Washington as MI6's liaison to the CIA — arranged a warning. On May 25, 1951, Burgess and Maclean boarded a Channel ferry in Southampton and disappeared. They surfaced in Moscow. Burgess spent the rest of his life there drinking steadily, stocking his Moscow apartment with English books and Union Jacks, receiving occasional British journalists, and being visibly homesick until he died in August 1963. Maclean adapted better — he became a Soviet academic and wrote a book on British foreign policy, died in 1983. Their defection blew the Cambridge network open and initiated the investigation that eventually led to Philby. Of the Cambridge Five, they are the pair that look least like what the archetype demands: Maclean was the serious one, a tall gray-haired diplomat whose photographs across a Foreign Office career show a man of impeccable professional bearing; Burgess was the one who looked like what he was — a man who drank too much and had strong opinions and did not always behave well at parties. Both were KGB intelligence officers. One had the right look. The other didn't need to.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Col. Oleg Penkovsky — HERO / YOGA
GRU Colonel (acting for CIA / MI6) · Moscow / London · April 1961–October 1962

Penkovsky was a GRU colonel with access to the Soviet military's most sensitive technical programs who approached Western intelligence voluntarily in 1960, was rebuffed initially, and made contact successfully in April 1961 through British businessman Greville Wynne acting as a courier. Over the following seventeen months he passed approximately 5,000 pages of photographs of classified Soviet military documents to CIA and MI6 — specifications and performance data for Soviet missiles, nuclear doctrine, vulnerability assessments of Soviet weapons systems, identities of GRU officers stationed abroad under cover. Codenamed HERO by the CIA and YOGA by MI6, he was handled by CIA officer George Kisevalter, among others. His motivation combined genuine ideological disillusionment with the Soviet system and what analysts described as personal vanity and a taste for Western consumer goods that the KGB subsequently emphasized in characterizing him. The intelligence he passed during the Cuban Missile Crisis in October 1962 was crucial: his earlier reporting had given US analysts the technical specifications to identify the missiles from U-2 photographs and to assess that the missiles were not yet operational, giving President Kennedy the margin to choose a naval blockade over a preemptive strike. He was arrested in October 1962 — the timing, in the middle of the crisis, has never been fully explained, and some analysts believe the KGB knew of him for months before acting. He was tried publicly in May 1963 and executed, probably on May 16, 1963. Greville Wynne was simultaneously tried and sentenced to eight years; he was exchanged in 1964 for Konon Molody. Of the figures in this compendium, Penkovsky may have had the highest direct impact on a specific world-historical outcome. In his GRU service photographs he looks like a Soviet military officer in his early forties, composed and precise, with the expression of a man who has made a decision about something important and is not planning to revisit it. He was not.

American
James Jesus Angleton — CI Staff
CIA Counterintelligence Staff · Langley, Virginia · 1954–1974

Angleton ran the CIA's counterintelligence staff for twenty years — from 1954 to the December morning in 1974 when William Colby called him in and told him his tenure was ending. He had spent the war years at OSS in London working alongside Kim Philby, who was then his closest professional contact. When Philby's defection was confirmed in 1963, Angleton concluded that Soviet intelligence had penetrated the CIA at a senior level and that the contaminated intelligence product had been shaping American foreign policy for a decade. The mole hunt that followed consumed him and the careers of the officers it touched — most of whom turned out to be innocent, including Yuri Nosenko, the KGB defector Angleton concluded was a provocation, who spent years in CIA custody on his orders. He ran counterintelligence as a closed compartment: few officers at headquarters understood what the CI staff was doing; most were not cleared to ask. He maintained particularly close ties with Israeli intelligence and oversaw domestic surveillance programs that became, after 1974, central exhibits in the Church Committee hearings. He grew orchids at his Virginia home and gave extended, elliptical interviews to journalists who found him fascinating and impenetrable. He had been close friends with T.S. Eliot at Harvard. He drank steadily and wore impeccable suits and thought in recursive, multi-layered constructs that colleagues described as the "wilderness of mirrors" — Eliot's phrase, which he applied to Soviet disinformation and which became the standard metaphor for the entire counterintelligence discipline. He was right that Soviet intelligence had penetrated the CIA at senior levels. He was wrong about where. The actual penetrations — Ames, Hanssen — were found only after his death in 1987. In photographs from across his twenty-year tenure he looks like an East Coast attorney who reads deeply and sleeps poorly and has considered views about what the documents actually mean when you read them in the right order. He did. His conclusion was wrong. The files he spent twenty years building are the reason we know what was in them.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Richard Helms — The Professional
CIA Directorate of Plans / Operations · Director of Central Intelligence 1966–1973 · Langley; Tehran

Helms was recruited by the Navy during the Second World War from a European journalism career — he had been covering Germany for United Press in the 1930s, including a 1936 interview with Hitler — and joined the OSS in London, where he spent the war years learning the tradecraft he would practice for the next quarter century. He joined the CIA at its founding in 1947 and rose entirely through the Directorate of Plans: a career case officer who became the clandestine service's institutional memory, its operational conscience, and eventually its director. He served under Johnson and Nixon from 1966 to 1973, overseeing the Phoenix Program in Vietnam, Operation CHAOS (CIA domestic mail-opening and surveillance of American citizens), and the FUBELT operation against Allende in Chile. When Nixon ordered him to participate in the Watergate cover-up by pressuring the FBI, he refused. Nixon fired him in November 1972 and parked him as Ambassador to Iran, a posting he served dutifully while the CIA files accumulated. When he testified before the Church Committee in 1977, his account of the Chilean operation was artfully incomplete — technically accurate, strategically misleading about the full extent of CIA involvement. He was subsequently charged with perjury, convicted on two misdemeanor counts, fined $2,000, and given a two-year suspended sentence. When the verdict was announced, CIA officers spontaneously gathered to contribute to paying his fine. He wore the conviction as a professional credential. He was, in the judgment of most intelligence professionals, the model of the career CIA officer: technically superb, institutionally loyal, professionally discreet, and more loyal to the agency than to any individual president's preferences. He died on October 22, 2002, at 89. His photographs across fifty years in American intelligence show a slim, dark-suited man with excellent posture who could be an under-secretary of state, an attorney at a Washington firm, or a retired diplomat at a Georgetown dinner party — the same controlled expression at 40 and at 70, the face of a man who has spent a career knowing more than he will say about events that remain classified. He was the professional template. The men in this compendium who came after him were, in some sense, attempting to be Helms at different moments in the same institutional story.

Soviet
Morris & Lena Cohen — "Peter and Helen Kroger"
KGB Directorate S · Ruislip, London · 1954–1961 · exchanged July 1969

They were born in the Bronx—Morris in 1910, Lena in 1913—and became Soviet intelligence agents in the 1930s through Soviet-aligned labor activism. During the 1940s they supported the atomic espionage network connected to Julius Rosenberg, handling material in New York. After the Rosenbergs' arrest in 1950, the Cohens disappeared. By 1954 they had resurfaced in London as Peter and Helen Kroger: New Zealand-born antiquarian booksellers. They settled into a semi-detached house at 45 Cranley Drive in the suburb of Ruislip, joined local social clubs, and were known to neighbors as a quiet, pleasant couple who had traveled a good deal. Peter collected books and held occasional sales. Helen baked. The house contained a shortwave transmitter concealed in a cigarette lighter, a high-speed burst transmitter hidden behind a bookcase, microdot equipment in the kitchen, and approximately $6,000 in cash in a hole beneath the floor. When MI5 arrested them in January 1961—the same day as Lonsdale—Helen Kroger was being walked to a police car when an officer noticed she was clutching her handbag. Inside was a microdot package bound for Moscow. They were exchanged in 1969 for Gerald Brooke, a British lecturer imprisoned in the Soviet Union. In every photograph from their London years, they look like a retired New Zealand couple who have done moderately well in the book trade. This was an accurate description of everything MI5 could prove before they found the shortwave transmitter.

American
Project VENONA — The Signal in the Noise
Army Signals Intelligence Service / NSA · Arlington Hall, Virginia · 1943–1980

Beginning in 1943, Army signals intelligence analysts at Arlington Hall began working on a collection of encrypted Soviet diplomatic and intelligence cables intercepted during the 1940s. The cables had been encrypted using one-time pads — theoretically unbreakable — but Soviet operators, under wartime pressure and overwork, had reused key pages from those pads, creating cryptographic vulnerabilities. Over the following decades, as the effort was transferred to the NSA, analysts produced a partial decryption of thousands of cables. What the cables contained: the identities of Soviet intelligence sources inside the U.S. government during and after the Second World War. The decrypted traffic identified Julius Rosenberg (LIBERAL), his wife Ethel, Harry Dexter White (JURIST) in the Treasury Department, Alger Hiss (ALES) in the State Department, and more than 300 other Soviet sources across the U.S. government. It confirmed that the atomic bomb project had been comprehensively penetrated. The project was classified until 1995; for decades its existence was shared with only a handful of intelligence officials. The Rosenbergs were executed in 1953. The cables that had identified them could not be disclosed at their trial. Some VENONA decrypts were shared with British intelligence — where they were, presumably, available to Kim Philby, who was then MI6's counterintelligence liaison to the CIA. Whether Philby passed them to Moscow remains a question that VENONA itself cannot answer. In photographs from Arlington Hall during the 1940s and 1950s, the analysts working the problem look like mathematically-inclined young people at a government desk. The cables are text on paper. The text on paper eventually named everyone in the network. It just took thirty years to read it.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Christopher Boyce — "The Falcon"
TRW Inc. (CIA contractor) · Redondo Beach, California · 1974–1977 · arrested January 21, 1977

Boyce was 21 when he got a job at TRW Inc., a defense contractor whose satellite operations facility handled encrypted communications between CIA headquarters and the KH-11 Keyhole reconnaissance satellite program. His father was a former FBI agent with connections. The background check was light. He began photographing classified documents in 1974 and passed them to the Soviets through his childhood friend Andrew Daulton Lee—a drug dealer who traveled to Mexico City to meet with a KGB officer at the Soviet Embassy. Boyce spent the payments on falconry, hence the code name. He was arrested in January 1977 when Mexican police apprehended Lee outside the Soviet Embassy; Lee, facing drug charges, quickly gave up his source. Boyce was 23, had no formal intelligence training, maintained no operational cover, and inadvertently caused one of the most damaging satellite-intelligence compromises in CIA history—including the exposure of CIA monitoring of Australian domestic politics, which contributed to a constitutional crisis in Canberra. He escaped from federal prison in 1980, robbed banks for nineteen months as a fugitive, and was recaptured in 1981. His TRW badge photograph shows a young man who got a good job through his father's contacts. This is accurate. It is also the entire cover, which is the point: the most dangerous look is no look at all.

American
Operation AZORIAN — Hughes Glomar Explorer
CIA Directorate of Science & Technology · Pacific Ocean · 1974

In March 1968, the Soviet submarine K-129 sank in the Pacific northwest of Hawaii with 98 crew, three nuclear ballistic missiles, and cryptographic materials. The U.S. Navy found the wreck at 16,500 feet. The CIA determined it was worth raising. The problem was doing so secretly, in international waters, without Soviet knowledge, in a vessel that would not look like a covert intelligence operation. The solution was Howard Hughes. His corporate infrastructure provided the cover: Global Marine designed and Hughes funded the construction of the Glomar Explorer—a 619-foot deep-sea mining ship that was, in every visual and functional detail, a mining ship. CIA officers aboard were credentialed as Hughes employees. The cover story was ocean-floor manganese nodule mining. The Glomar Explorer sailed in 1974. The salvage successfully retrieved a portion of the K-129, including nuclear-armed torpedoes, cryptographic materials, and the remains of six Soviet crewmen, who were buried at sea with Soviet naval honors in a ceremony filmed by CIA. Soviet naval vessels shadowed the operation throughout but did not intervene. When journalist Seymour Hersh began investigating in 1975, the CIA asked major news organizations to delay publication; several complied. When the Los Angeles Times ran the story in March 1975, the cover collapsed. CIA officers responding to subsequent FOIA requests about the program developed what became standard agency practice: "We can neither confirm nor deny the existence of the information requested." The Glomar Response. The ship had looked exactly like a mining vessel because it was built as one, and had successfully concealed the most expensive covert operation in CIA history from Soviet intelligence for four years. The phrase outlasted the ship.

Soviet
George Blake — Operation GOLD
KGB (former MI6) · Berlin; London · 1955–1961 · escaped 1966 · Moscow 1966–2020

Blake was born in Rotterdam, evacuated from Dunkirk, worked with the Dutch resistance, and joined MI6 after the war. He was captured in Korea in 1950 and during 34 months of captivity became a committed Communist. The KGB recruited him in 1951. By 1955, when MI6 assigned him to the Berlin Station, he was simultaneously running British operations and passing them to Moscow. Operation GOLD—the Anglo-American tunnel dug from West Berlin to tap Soviet military communications cables, one of the most ambitious technical operations of the early Cold War—was betrayed to the KGB before the first shovel broke ground. The Soviets let it run eleven months to protect Blake, then arranged a theatrical discovery. He also passed approximately 400 Western agents to the KGB; many were executed. He was arrested in 1961 after a Polish defector identified him, and sentenced to 42 years—the longest sentence in British legal history. In October 1966 he escaped from Wormwood Scrubs with the help of a peace activist and a rope ladder woven from knitting needles. He lived in Moscow for the following 54 years, occasionally lecturing at the SVR Academy, writing careful memoirs, and giving thoughtful interviews to Western journalists. He died in December 2020 at age 98. In every photograph from his Moscow years he looks like a pleasant elderly British gentleman who might teach watercolors at a community center. He does not look like a man who spent a decade passing the identities of 400 people to an intelligence service that killed them.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Sir Anthony Blunt — The Fourth Man
KGB / Cambridge Five (Surveyor of the King's / Queen's Pictures) · London · active 1936–1945; exposed publicly November 1979

Blunt was recruited by Soviet intelligence at Cambridge in the early 1930s as one of the five. He served as an MI5 officer during the Second World War, passing intelligence to Moscow, and returned to civilian life afterward as the Surveyor of the King's Pictures — later the Queen's Pictures — the keeper of the Royal Collection and one of the foremost authorities on Poussin and French Baroque painting in the English-speaking world. He was identified to MI5 privately in 1964 and given immunity from prosecution in exchange for full debriefing. In the fifteen years between his private identification and public exposure he continued his career at the highest levels of the British cultural establishment: lecturing, writing, advising on Royal Collection acquisitions, and appearing in the photographs of museum openings and academic conferences as exactly what his public record described: an elegant, slightly gaunt man in a well-cut suit who was one of the most respected art historians in Britain. Prime Minister Margaret Thatcher confirmed his identity to Parliament in November 1979. He was stripped of his knighthood. He died in March 1983. His photographs after the exposure — at a press conference in his Portman Square flat, composed and thin, in a suit — look exactly as his photographs had always looked. He said it was the intelligence work he regretted, not the convictions that produced it. Whether this was accurate is a judgment left to the reader. He is the end point of the Cambridge model: the operative who outlasted not just the war but the entire subsequent era of Cold War intelligence, continuing for decades in plain sight, and was exposed not by his tradecraft failing but by political decision. The photographs never changed. That was the design. That was also how long it worked.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Kermit Roosevelt Jr. — Operation AJAX, Tehran 1953
CIA Near East Division · Tehran, Iran · August 1953

In the summer of 1953, Roosevelt drove into Tehran with a car full of cash and overthrew a government. Mohammed Mossadegh, the democratically elected Prime Minister of Iran, had nationalized the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company — the predecessor to BP — and both Britain and the United States had concluded he needed to be removed. Roosevelt was the CIA's ground-level officer for Operation AJAX / TPAJAX: the joint CIA-MI6 effort to restore the Shah. The operational method was a demonstration of what the CIA believed, in 1953, that it could do cheaply and quietly: organize demonstrations, bribe press outlets, distribute cash to military officers and street gangs, build a coalition of anti-Mossadegh forces, and stage a coup. The total budget was approximately $1 million. Mossadegh was arrested on August 19, 1953. The Shah was restored. Roosevelt drove to the British ambassador's residence for dinner and reported success. The operation's effectiveness — a government change for a million dollars — convinced the Eisenhower administration that covert action was a reliable instrument of policy. This conclusion led directly to the Guatemala coup in 1954, to the planning for the Bay of Pigs, and to the operational theory that underwrote thirty years of CIA political action. The operation's other legacy — the Iranian anti-American sentiment that it planted and that grew for the next seven decades — was not visible in the immediate after-action assessment. Roosevelt resigned from CIA in 1958 and consulted for Gulf Oil and Persian Gulf governments for the following decades. He wrote Countercoup in 1979. He died in 2000. He was Theodore Roosevelt's grandson. In his photographs from the Tehran period he looks like a trim, well-dressed young American man in a hot city on a business trip. The business trip removed a government. This is, in some respects, the founding document of the operational theory that the rest of this compendium describes.

American
William King Harvey — ZR/RIFLE
CIA · Berlin; Washington; Key West; Rome · 1947–1976

Harvey was fired from the FBI by J. Edgar Hoover in 1947 and joined the CIA the same year. He was, from the beginning, the anti-Helms: the operator rather than the manager, the man who carried two loaded pistols at all times (including during Washington headquarters briefings), who kept a refrigerator in his West Berlin kitchen because, as he explained to guests, you never knew who might be hiding behind the one in the pantry. He ran the Berlin Tunnel (Operation GOLD) from the CIA's side — betrayed before it was operational by George Blake, whose entry appears earlier — and then returned to Washington to establish the CIA's assassination capability: Project ZR/RIFLE, the Executive Action program designed to kill foreign leaders. He ran the Cuban operations from JMWAVE, the Miami station, after the Bay of Pigs. He had a complex relationship with the Kennedy administration: Bobby Kennedy, who was running Operation Mongoose, had contempt for Harvey's tradecraft; Harvey returned it. When Harvey sent six sabotage teams into Cuba during the Cuban Missile Crisis — the precisely wrong operational moment — Kennedy had him transferred to Rome, where he spent his final CIA years overweight and visibly drinking. His contemporaries describe him as the most instinctively capable operations officer they had ever worked with and simultaneously the most ungovernable. He was the template for the covert action officer at the expense of institutional compatibility: useful precisely because he was willing to do what administrators could not, and eventually sent away for the same reason. He died in 1976. In his CIA photographs from the Berlin and Washington years he looks like a heavyset man with heavy-lidded eyes who is paying extremely close attention to something across the room that you cannot see. He usually was.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Maj. Pyotr Popov — The First
GRU (acting for CIA) · Vienna; East Berlin · 1953–1958 · executed 1960

In January 1953, a GRU officer stationed in Vienna dropped a note into the car of a U.S. diplomat: he was willing to work for American intelligence. He was Major Pyotr Popov, a Soviet military intelligence officer from a peasant family who had concluded that the Soviet system had failed the people it claimed to serve. He became the CIA's first significant penetration of Soviet military intelligence. For five years he passed GRU operational details, the identities of Soviet intelligence officers stationed abroad, and Soviet military order of battle to CIA handlers in Vienna and later East Berlin. The CIA had no established methodology for running a Soviet source; they built it around Popov's case. The dead drops, the brush passes, the signals systems, the cover identities for contact — the technical infrastructure of Cold War HUMINT in hostile territory was developed, case by case, in the years of Popov's operation. He was caught in 1958 — the means of his exposure have never been definitively established, though some analysts point to a letter that the CIA sent to his cover address that was intercepted by KGB surveillance — arrested, tried, and executed in January 1960. His case officer, George Kisevalter (who also later handled Penkovsky), spent years working to determine exactly how the compromise occurred. The methodology Popov's operation required the CIA to develop became the template for every subsequent operation in this compendium. In his GRU service photographs he looks like a Soviet military officer of his generation: plain-faced, straightforward, slightly uncomfortable in the frame. He was the first. Everything that followed him in this collection was built on the methodology his case forced the CIA to develop.

UNCLASSIFIED
American
Operation SOLO — Morris and Jack Childs
FBI · Chicago / Moscow / Beijing / Havana · 1952–1981

Morris Childs was a senior official of the Communist Party USA — the national liaison between the CPUSA and the Communist Party of the Soviet Union, whose function required him to travel regularly to Moscow, attend CPSU congresses, meet with Soviet General Secretary-level leadership, and return to Chicago with instructions and funding. His brother Jack was the CPUSA treasurer, managing the cash flows from Moscow to the American party. Both were FBI informants from 1952 onward, running under the code name SOLO for the following twenty-nine years. Morris met personally with Khrushchev, Brezhnev, Andropov, and Gorbachev. He sat in rooms at the Kremlin where Soviet leaders discussed their strategy toward the United States. He transmitted those conversations to the FBI. Jack managed the funding channel — at peak, approximately $3 million annually in cash from Soviet accounts — and documented every transfer. J. Edgar Hoover personally briefed President Nixon on SOLO intelligence in 1969. The operation ran until 1981, when Jack's declining health made continuation impossible. Morris received the Presidential Medal of Freedom from Ronald Reagan in 1987. In every photograph from the SOLO years — at party events, at Moscow meetings, in the corridors of CPSU offices — Morris Childs looks exactly like what his public biography said he was: an aging Jewish Communist from Chicago in a dark suit, slightly stout, with the expression of a senior party official at a committee meeting. For twenty-nine years, no one in the rooms where he was meeting suspected otherwise. The operation produced more intelligence from the upper levels of Soviet leadership than any other FBI operation in the Cold War. He weighed about 175 pounds. He wore glasses. He was indistinguishable from every other Communist Party functionary in every photograph of every event he attended. This was not an accident.

РАССЕКРЕЧЕНО
Soviet
Yuri Nosenko — The Prisoner in the Cage
KGB Second Chief Directorate (defected to CIA) · Geneva; Washington; Camp Peary · defected February 4, 1964 · imprisoned 1964–1967

Nosenko defected to the CIA in Geneva in February 1964, three months after the Kennedy assassination. He claimed to have personally reviewed the KGB's file on Lee Harvey Oswald — who had defected to the Soviet Union in 1959 and returned to the United States in 1962 — and to have confirmed that the KGB had never recruited Oswald and had considered him unstable and unreliable. This was either the most important disclosure a defector had ever provided, or a Soviet plant designed to deflect CIA investigation of the assassination. James Angleton concluded it was the latter. Over CIA objections, Nosenko was placed in hostile confinement: first in a safe house, then in a specially constructed room at a training facility in Virginia — no reading material, no exercise, no human contact beyond hostile interrogation. He remained there for 1,277 days. The case became the central battleground of the CIA's internal mole hunt: if Nosenko was genuine, Angleton's theory of deep Soviet penetration was questionable; if he was a plant, the CIA's entire Soviet operations program might be contaminated. He was subjected to repeated polygraphs; the results were disputed. He was interrogated under conditions a later CIA review described as amounting to psychological torture. In 1967, a new assessment concluded he was genuine. He was released, given a CIA consultant contract, and eventually U.S. citizenship. Angleton was not persuaded until his dismissal in 1974. The damage assessment conducted after Nosenko's release found no evidence he was a plant, and concluded that the decision to imprison him had destroyed an intelligence asset whose full cooperation might have been obtained by conventional means. He died in 2008. His defection photograph — a slight, composed man in a gray coat in a European city in winter — looks like every Cold War defection photograph. What it does not show is the next three years.

MACV-SOG — The Studies and Observations Group
Military Assistance Command Vietnam, Special Operations Group · Vietnam; Laos; Cambodia · 1964–1972

The Studies and Observations Group was the most classified American military unit of the Vietnam War: a joint CIA-Special Forces organization that ran operations the United States government publicly denied — reconnaissance and direct-action missions deep into Laos and Cambodia, where American troops were officially not operating, against the Ho Chi Minh Trail and North Vietnamese logistical networks. SOG ran its own air assets, its own intelligence collection, its own agent networks; it recruited and trained indigenous fighters across three countries. The operators who served in SOG in the mid-1960s through the early 1970s were the raw material of everything that came after: they were the NCOs Beckwith was looking for when he started building Delta, the men who understood that the capability gap Eagle Claw demonstrated had been visible since Son Tay, the officers who knew exactly what a hostile-environment reconnaissance mission required and had casualty records to prove it. SOG's casualty rate was the highest of any U.S. unit in Vietnam; operators spoke of the "lottery," the statistical certainty that if you ran enough missions you would not come back. The men who came back brought with them a specific understanding of what it was possible to do with small teams in denied territory, what it was not possible to do, and what it cost. They brought that understanding to Fort Bragg in the late 1970s and built it into the units that began operating in the following decade. In the SOG operational photographs from the Laotian and Cambodian missions — small teams of American NCOs and indigenous fighters in the jungle, carrying equipment chosen for function rather than uniformity, wearing nothing that identified them as U.S. military — they look like men who have been in the jungle long enough to understand that their survival depends on not being seen. They were correct.

Anatoly Golitsyn — The Long Deception
KGB First Chief Directorate (defected to CIA) · Helsinki · defected December 15, 1961

Golitsyn was a KGB officer who defected to the CIA in Helsinki in December 1961 and became James Angleton's primary source for a theory that nearly destroyed American intelligence. His initial intelligence was genuine and valuable: he identified several Soviet penetrations of Western services, including George Blake, and provided material that contributed to the identification of legitimate sources. Angleton found in Golitsyn a confirmation of his deepest beliefs about Soviet intelligence: that it operated through long-range strategic deception; that most subsequent defectors were KGB plants sent to discredit Golitsyn's revelations; and that the CIA had been penetrated at a senior level. Under Angleton's sponsorship, Golitsyn was given unprecedented access to CIA files to search for the mole. When Yuri Nosenko defected in 1964 with information that contradicted Golitsyn's theories, Golitsyn told Angleton that Nosenko was a plant — a conclusion that contributed directly to Nosenko's 1,277-day imprisonment. Golitsyn's continuing influence through the 1960s effectively paralyzed CIA Soviet operations: sources were suspected of being controlled, operations were canceled, officers were investigated. The mole he predicted was never found. The damage his influence caused — terminated operations, imprisoned legitimate defectors, destroyed careers — constituted, in the retrospective assessment of most intelligence historians, a greater injury to American intelligence than most actual Soviet penetrations of the period. He was the mirror image of an agent: not a Soviet officer who penetrated a Western service, but a genuine defector whose influence on his handlers was nearly as destructive as if he had been sent. He was still alive in 2025 and residing in the United States. In his defection photographs from Helsinki he looks like a man who has made a decision and is prepared to go wherever it leads. He did. So did everyone around him.

On the night of December 23, 1975, Richard Welch returned to his home in Athens after a Christmas reception at the U.S. Ambassador's residence. In his CIA photographs he looks like what his cover said he was: a trim mid-career State Department officer attending diplomatic functions in a European capital. That cover identified him to a competent adversary and provided no protection in front of his own house. His is the case that every CIA cover officer knows: the appearance only works until it doesn't.

Frank Snepp — Decent Interval
CIA Strategic Intelligence Analyst · Saigon Station · 1969–1975

Snepp was a CIA strategic intelligence analyst at Saigon Station from 1969 until the evacuation on April 29, 1975 — one of the last Americans to leave. He spent six years inside the intelligence apparatus of a losing war, watching the deterioration of South Vietnamese military capabilities, the quality of CIA analysis forwarded to Washington, and the pace of the drawdown. He was an analyst who decided that what he had witnessed required a public account. The CIA's response was to ensure that no one could profit from that decision. In his photographs from the 1970s he looks like a serious young man who worked hard at something important and made a decision about it that he does not plan to revisit. He has not revisited it.

Director of Central Intelligence · Langley, Virginia · 1973–1976

Colby became DCI in September 1973 — one month after Allende's overthrow in Chile, as congressional scrutiny of CIA political operations was reaching a level the agency had not faced before. In his official CIA portraits from the director years he looks like a thoughtful man in wire-rimmed glasses who has read the file and is prepared to explain it patiently to people who have not. CIA · Langley, Virginia · January 30, 1976–January 20, 1977

Bush was appointed DCI by President Ford in January 1976 — in the immediate aftermath of the Church Committee's public dissection of the CIA's postwar history, and after Ford's "Halloween Massacre" had removed Colby and the leadership that had accumulated those vulnerabilities. American
Team B — The Competitive Estimate, 1976
President's Foreign Intelligence Advisory Board / DCI Bush · Washington, DC · 1976

In 1976, DCI George H.W. Bush authorized an outside competitive analysis of the CIA's official National Intelligence Estimate on Soviet strategic capabilities and intentions. The CIA's professional analytic staff — "Team A" — had produced estimates concluding that Soviet strategic doctrine remained defensively oriented and that capabilities were consistent with deterrence. "Team B," an outside panel including Harvard historian Richard Pipes and Paul Wolfowitz, was given access to the same classified material and reached different conclusions: that Soviet military doctrine was oriented toward nuclear war-fighting and war-winning rather than deterrence, that capabilities were systematically underestimated, and that the CIA's analytical methods were subject to "mirror-imaging" — assuming Soviet decision-makers shared American strategic rationality. Team B's specific estimates turned out, in most cases, to be wrong: CIA's estimates of Soviet missile accuracy, throw-weight, and kill probability were more accurate on the specific questions. Team B's methodological critique — that the intelligence community was insufficiently alert to adversarial intentions fundamentally unlike American ones — was correct in ways the specific estimates did not reveal. The Team B report was classified; its conclusions leaked and became the intellectual foundation for the Committee on the Present Danger, the think-tank infrastructure that produced Reagan's defense policy, and the argument for the defense buildup that drove Soviet military spending to its final breaking point. The CIA career analysts who had been bypassed by the outside-team exercise were furious and largely right on the specifics. The outside team was right on the methodology. Bush authorized the exercise because he believed the intelligence community needed to be challenged from outside. The challenge changed American defense policy for a decade. Whether that change is the reason the Soviet Union fell is the question historians have been arguing since 1991. The analysts who argued both positions look, in photographs from the congressional testimony and policy meetings of the period, like the academic economists and policy professionals they were: men in suits in conference rooms in Washington, with documents and arguments. Neither side looked wrong at the time. Both were right about some of it.

Adm. Stansfield Turner — Director of Central Intelligence
CIA · Langley, Virginia · March 1977–January 1981

Turner was a Naval Academy classmate of Jimmy Carter — class of 1947 — and Carter appointed him DCI in March 1977. He arrived at Langley with a conviction that technical intelligence collection (signals intelligence, satellite imagery) had made many human intelligence operations redundant, that the risk and ethical complexity of running human sources could be reduced substantially without sacrificing intelligence product quality, and that the Directorate of Operations had grown beyond its useful size. In October 1977 he terminated approximately 820 career officer positions in the Directorate of Operations — what CIA veterans called the "October Surprise" or, within the clandestine service, simply the gutting. The officers who remained described a Directorate that had been halved and demoralized in a single quarter; the institutional memory and area expertise that takes decades to build was discharged in job lots. Turner believed SIGINT and imagery could replace what was lost. The intelligence record of his four years does not support this thesis: the CIA failed to adequately predict the fall of the Shah and the Iranian Revolution in 1978–79, failed to adequately predict the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in December 1979, and found itself with limited HUMINT capacity in precisely the regions where events were moving fastest. William Casey, when he arrived at Langley in January 1981, inherited the organization Turner had built. Casey spent the next seven years rebuilding the paramilitary and HUMINT capabilities Turner had eliminated. The officers entering the covert Afghan program, the Latin American programs, and the technical operations described in the rest of this compendium were in many cases replacements for the men Turner had fired. In his CIA photographs Turner looks like a Naval Admiral at a desk who has made a decision and is prepared to defend it at whatever congressional hearing is scheduled. He did defend it, for the rest of his life. He died in 2018. The Directorate of Operations he disbanded is still being rebuilt.

Lt. Viktor Belenko — The MiG-25 Defection
Soviet Air Defense Forces (PVO) · Hakodate Airport, Japan · September 6, 1976

On September 6, 1976, Soviet Air Defense Forces pilot Viktor Belenko broke from a training flight near Vladivostok, flew his MiG-25P at very low altitude to evade Soviet radar, crossed into Japanese airspace, and landed at Hakodate Airport. American fighter design programs that had been built partly around the Foxbat threat were recalibrated. The Soviets demanded the aircraft back. The Japanese returned it thirty days later, in crates, with an invoice for disassembly and reassembly. Belenko was resettled in the United States and became an American citizen and defense consultant. In his defection-day photograph — still in his flight gear at Hakodate, surrounded by Japanese emergency responders and police, with the expression of a man working through the reality of what he has just done — he looks exactly like that: a Soviet Air Force pilot who has made an irreversible decision and is sitting with it. He had. The aircraft behind him became the most thoroughly examined Soviet military aircraft in history. The vacuum tubes inside it changed American defense procurement.

The Counterterrorism Center was established in March 1986 by Director Casey and Clair George, initially under Dewey Clarridge's direction, in response to the cascade of terrorist incidents in 1985: TWA Flight 847, the Achille Lauro, the Rome and Vienna airport massacres. The officers who staffed the CTC in its 1990-95 formative period had been trained entirely in Cold War intelligence: Soviet order of battle, Warsaw Pact force structure, nuclear doctrine, counterintelligence methods against state services. They were learning a fundamentally different discipline — not a foreign state with defined geography and bureaucratic hierarchy, but a movement with no headquarters, no uniform, no fixed infrastructure, and no functional distinction between wartime and peacetime. The Cold War CIA had been optimized to understand and deter a specific state adversary. The CTC was being built to understand something the Cold War intelligence framework had not been designed to see. In photographs of the CTC from this period — the few that exist — it looks like any government interagency workspace: fluorescent lights, drop ceilings, men and women in their thirties and forties at government desks reviewing files. The distinction between the Cold War CIA and what followed was not in the workspace.
